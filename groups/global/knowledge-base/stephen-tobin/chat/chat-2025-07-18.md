> **Stephen Tobin** (14:38): Unfortunately it depends on the broker. On IBKR they call it an exit strategy and use a stop limit, on IG it is a limit. Both work OK

> **Steve Lang** (14:54): On Fidelity they're different. A stop sells at market price  when the stop price is hit. A stop limit sells at a limit price you specify when the stop price hits.

> **Devin Himel** (14:56): Would the stop be 17 or the limit?

> **Steve Lang** (15:04): In a stop limit you specify two values, a stop price and a limit price. You can make them both 17, in which case when the price drops to 17, the stock will only sell at 17 or higher. This protects you if the stock drops BELOW 17, the stock won't sell until it rises back up to 17. I find this useful when there's a lot of price volatility.

> **Steve Lang** (15:26): You can make the stop value and the limit value both 17 which is what i usually do. A plain stop order at 17 will generate a market order as soon as the stop price is reached. So when the stock price drops to 17, the generated market order might sell at 16.90, lets say, but if the stock price might jump back to 17.10, you would be stopped out but only get 16.90/shr. Had you placed a stop limit order with the stop price and the limit price both at 17, a limit order would be generated instead of a market order when the price dropped to 17 and the generated limit order would not execute unless the price rose back to 17.
