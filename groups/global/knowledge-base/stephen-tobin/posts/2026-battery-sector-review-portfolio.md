The purpose of this review is to decide whether I should continue looking to buy these 4 companies, move on to other battery targets, or perhaps drop the sector altogether.

The battery companies can be divided in two ways. Firstly, by their area of innovation, and secondly by their go-to-market strategy.

My complete Battery Sector has more than 20 names on it, down from 35 this time last year.

## The Technologies

The majority of companies are working with lithium-ion batteries, seeking to improve performance with a new Anode, Cathode, Separator, or Electrolyte.

A few companies are working on entirely different chemistries, and I will cover them separately.

AMPX, along with ENVX, NEO, and the pre IPO companies Silla + Group14, are working on Silicon anodes. Silicon Anodes offer much higher-density batteries with faster charging capabilities. The battery majors are all exploring Silicon; both Tesla and Panasonic are using higher content Silicon Anodes. LG is investing heavily along with Samsung and CATL

## Amprius Technology (AMPX)

Amprius is making excellent business progress; its shares reflect growth in its order book and revenue. AMPX is selling to the drone and aerospace industries and hopes to develop its defence business this year.

I have problems with AMPX. Before it went public, it spun off Amprius Nanjing as a separate company. Amprius Nanjing took some IP with it and AMPX kept the silicon nanowire IP.

The Silicon nanowire tech has failed; it has proven too difficult to manufacture and does not offer any significant advantages over the much cheaper-to-produce technology kept by Amprius Nanjing.

Amprius has morphed into a reseller of the Amprius Nanjing silicon Anode technology. Amprius Nanjing has been renamed Berzilius, and the CEO of AMPX has stepped down but will continue his position as the CEO of Berzilius.

AMPX has the exclusive rights to sell Berzilius technology-based batteries in North America; they call their battery cell the Si-Core. Despite a grant from US authorities, they will not produce cells in the US but in South Korea, using contract manufacturers.

Berzilious has signed contracts with multiple other battery companies and OEM companies, including OCSiAI, the world's largest producer of carbon nanotubes, which is expected to generate world-leading battery density.

They have signed multiple direct supply agreements with global tier-1 manufacturers for EVs, including cars and bikes. Aviation and drone companies, as well as consumer electronics companies.

It leaves AMPX without any IP, a limited market, and a contract with Berzilius that imposes minimum-volume constraints. On top of that, Berzilious is selling directly to the largest producers in the verticals AMPX is targeting. AMPX is now a reseller of Chinese technology inside Korean-produced batteries, limited to sales in North America.

AMPX is moving down to the sector list, and I will not be buying in 2026. That doesn't mean I don't think the shares will increase in price; it probably will, but I doubt it will be able to become a large company with its current strategies.

I still like the Silicon Anode technology, but I will be exploring other companies for investments.

Silicon offers higher density, but Li-metal offers even higher, and SES, QS, with pre ipo Factorial ProLogium and Lift.

## SES.AI

SES began as a li Metal anode developer and moved quickly to sign agreements with three major vehicle manufacturers. General Motors, Hyundai and Honda.

After A sample testing, General Motors dropped out, B sample testing was completed in 2025, and we have had a complete absence of news about Hyundai and Honda. SES repeatedly said C sample testing would begin in 2025 but it has not happened. I have concluded that both companies have decided not to move forward with the Li-metal anode from SES.

SES has completely pivoted its market offering and now focuses on its Molecular Universe AI software for electrolyte discovery.

This clearly has potential, the CEO of ENVX, one of the silicon anode companies I follow, spoke at great length about how his company was “tweaking” the electrolyte in its battery cells to meet the requirements of its primary cell phone customer, Honor, and how it had enabled the cells to meet the 1,000-cycle requirement.

Batteries in different environments or under different operating conditions can be optimized using different electrolytes. It is a key element that allows companies to differentiate their products.

SES is moving to the prospect list, and the key issue is whether it can monetize the Molecular Universe sufficiently to sustain profitability and drive revenue growth. My gut feeling is that the total market is too small and it will not keep growing but we will have to wait and see.

## Separators

Regardless of the electrolyte, you need to prevent the anode and cathode from touching. In lithium batteries, this is not easy, as dendrites form inside the battery, leading to a short circuit. Improved separators lead to longer battery life and safer operations; they do not improve battery density.

In the separator space, ELVA and QS are working on ceramic-based thin films.

## Electrovaya

Electrovaya has perfected its ceramic separator and has proven in multiple verticals that its batteries are safer and longer-lasting than anything the competition can currently produce. Selling my shares in ELVA is my biggest regret in 2025, I should have been adding not selling which is always easy to realize with the benefit of hindsight.

Electrovaya continues to grow its verticals and build its US based manufacturing plant. The company achieved profitability for the first time last year and I think 2026 will be another good year.

ELVA is targetting the autonomous robot market and the stationary storage verticals, if it can establish a similar market leadership position as it has in materials handling then ELVA is a ten bagger. Of course, there is a lot of competition, but no more than in the forklift truck market, where they are now widely viewed as the premium battery solution, offering the best total cost of ownership per battery, and are the only manufacturer of a battery that can last the life of its truck.

ELVA will remain on the eyes-on list and I will take the first opportunity to take a position.

## Business Models

The two companies that have been real success stories for us (ELVA and AMPX) chose a go-to-market strategy that allowed for quick acceptance. The testing regime from their customers was measured in months, and once the tests were passed, the revenue followed.

Multiple other companies chose more difficult end-user markets that may offer far greater ultimate gains. Many have failed and have now disappeared, but some have survived and seem to be getting stronger.

I will be doing deep dives on three in the coming weeks. Solid Power and Quantum Scape are targeting the EV market with their multi-phase testing regimes that seem destined to take at least 5 years to complete. ENVX targeted the Mobile Phone market with a similarly long lead time.

Solid Power is developing a solid electrolyte and appears to be making good, but slow progress with several EV manufacturers and creating a good relationship with SK, a large Korean battery maker.

QuantumScape is developing a ceramic separator combined with a Li-metal anode (actually, no anode, but a Li-metal one forms and an almost solid electrolyte). It is also making excellent progress toward commercialization.

ENVX now has a manufacturing facility and appears close to meeting Honors' requirements before entering full-scale production. The manufacturing facility is coming up to speed, and ENVX is looking at supplying batteries in multiple other verticals.

## Conclusion

AMPX is off the list, ELVA is on the “eyes on” list waiting for an entry point and SES has been pushed down the pecking order as we await commercial progress.

ULBI remains a conundrum; I will review it in more detail to see if we should continue following the company.

SLDP, QS and ENVX have been moved up to the prospect list for full evaluation in January.

Some other battery technologies are entering 2026 on a high note, and I will be looking for opportunities in them during Q1. The key technologies of interest are Sodium-ion, Iron-Air, Vanadium, Zinc, and Aluminium.

Diamond batteries remain a long-term prospect but are not yet on my evaluation list.
