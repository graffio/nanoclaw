# Trade Alert #75: Buying A Fuel Cell Company

*The first electricity generator for power centers*

I have been exploring the possibilities of siting data centers in the Permian Basin. It would seem to be a perfect area, with an ample supply of oil and gas, plenty of space, and few permit problems.

The problem lies in the amount of energy these data centers require, how it will be generated, and who will manufacture the necessary equipment.

I have decided that the most significant near-term opportunity is for fuel cell companies, and I will be investing in one today.

New nuclear is at least 5-10 years away. An executive at NextEra Energy told me the wait for new high-voltage connections to the grid was between 5 and 10 years, which is less than the wait for a gas turbine.

In the short term, fuel cells may be the best solution or perhaps the only solution.

## **Fuel Cell Technologies Overview**

Fuel cells are electrochemical devices that convert the chemical energy of a fuel and an oxidant (usually oxygen from air) into electricity, with water and heat as byproducts. They are highly efficient, have low to zero emissions, and offer scalable power generation. The type of fuel cell is defined by the electrolyte it uses, which determines its operating temperature and the applications for which it is suitable.

## Ceres Technology

Ceres operates across two primary technology segments serving different market applications. The **SOFC segment** focuses on stationary power applications, including data centers, commercial buildings, marine auxiliary power, and grid stabilization. The **SOEC segment** targets industrial decarbonization applications, particularly in steel production, ammonia manufacturing, and sustainable aviation fuel production, where green hydrogen is required.

**SOFC produces electricity from Natural Gas or other fuels**

**SOEC produces Hydrogen from electricity**

Ceres’ technology platform is built around proprietary solid oxide technology, specifically the SteelCell platform, which represents a significant departure from conventional ceramic-based fuel cell designs. This metal-supported cell design utilizes a cost-effective stainless steel substrate as its structural foundation, reducing manufacturing costs and supply chain risks.

The technology operates at lower temperatures (450-630°C) compared to Bloom Energy (800-1,000°C). This lower operating temperature enables the use of inexpensive stainless steel, resulting in a significant cost advantage.

The technology is inherently reversible, operating efficiently in both fuel cell mode for power generation and electrolyser mode for hydrogen production, thereby creating a single platform that serves multiple applications. In power generation mode, Ceres’ SOFCs achieve efficiencies above 90% when heat is captured. In electrolysis mode, the SOEC modules produce hydrogen at a rate of 37kWh/kg, representing the most efficient rate currently available and approximately 25% higher efficiency than incumbent alkaline and PEM technologies.

Ceres continues to innovate, with the Gen 7 cell design, due for release in 2026, promising a doubling in cell power, a three times improvement in life, and increased robustness, leading to what analysts describe as “an inflection point in fuel cell technological maturity.”

## **Ceres Business Model**

Ceres operates an asset-light licensing business model, which differentiates it from traditional, manufacturing-heavy fuel cell companies. Rather than building factories and manufacturing products directly, Ceres licenses its core cell and stack intellectual property to global manufacturing partners for mass production. This model, which has already secured six licenses, involves upfront payments for technology transfer (approximately £30 million) and ongoing per-unit royalties (around £70 per kW).

This approach enables the prospect of rapid global scaling while minimizing capital requirements and manufacturing risks by allowing larger, better-funded, and more established companies to handle the construction of facilities and manufacturing. These larger entities typically have existing demand for the product and significant industry expertise and contacts.

While this model offers the opportunity for rapid expansion and adoption of the technology, it does carry risks. The cancellation of a license by Bosch earlier this year left Ceres with only Asian licensees, making the signing of a European/American license a business priority.

## Time To Power: The near-term business opportunity

The electricity supply industry is changing, faced with unprecedented growth in demand and the need for reliable supply for data centers, the need for distributed electricity generation has grown dramatically.

The demand is worldwide and faces the same fundamental problem: Over the near term (the next five years), the world will experience a shortfall in its electricity supply. Ceres believes 25% of that shortfall will be in the US, 50% in Asia Pacific and the final 25% spread around the rest of the world.

There are several ways to generate electricity, but a new measure of time to power has emerged due to a bottleneck.

If you want a large gas turbine, you can expect a 7-year wait; nuclear SMRs likely have a 10-year wait. A high-voltage grid connection has a wait of 5-15 years, depending on the location, and the availability and practicality of renewable energy also depend on the location.

Almost the only readily available option is a Solid Oxide Fuel cell attached to a natural gas supply. The time to power is cut from years to months.

**Bloom Energy** is leading the way, currently the only SOFC available in the market; it has seen its share price rise almost 800% in the last year. This year, it will have its first competitor, but the market's demand is so high that they will likely coexist without impacting each other.

This is the opportunity that I believe will drive Ceres stock significantly higher. Bloom has said there will be a shortfall of 35-60GW in the US datacenter market alone between 2026 and 2030. I believe the first Doosan products will target the US data center market and offer the same performance as Bloom at a lower price.

If Bloom is correct and the deficit is 35 GW, then Ceres should capture 10% of this, generating £ 245 million in revenue and £190 million in operating income at 79% margins. That would be a tenfold increase in revenue and drive them to positive free cash flow for the first time.

## Finances

Ceres has a flawless balance sheet with shareholder equity of £135 million and zero debt. It has £104 million in cash on hand, providing a 4-year cash runway at current cash flow rates.

Revenue comes from signing new licenses, there is then a period where the customer builds its facilities and pays engineering fees finally when it goes into production Ceres receives royalties.

This business model’s margins are around 80%. It will be Ceres’s job to keep innovating and improving the fuel cell; the newest version will be released early next year.

Management has guided a revenue of £32 million for 2025, a drop of nearly 40%. Currently, they are entirely dependent on license fees, and they would need to sign one more deal this year to match the revenue from 2024.

Royalties are expected to ramp up quickly in 2026 and 2027 as Doosan production comes online and continues to grow as Delta transitions to full production.

Building a comprehensive model for pre-revenue companies is challenging, and I have never attempted to do so for this specific business model. I am presenting an indicative model. It depends on signing license agreements and the timings of SOP. With the two current manufacturing partners, assuming nothing changes, royalty revenue would be £50 million if they hit 80% capacity.

### **Competitive Analysis**

In the immediate term, the only competitor will be Bloom Energy. Bloom is now a large-scale incumbent with a proven solution. The Ceres Power solution will have similar specifications but will be significantly cheaper, due to the manufacturing process involved and the use of steel and aluminum rather than expensive ceramics.

Ceres Power maintains a strong competitive advantage through its superior solid oxide technology, especially the reversible nature of the fuel cell, protected by extensive IP and over 20 years of development. This creates high barriers for new entrants and limits customer bargaining power. While the threat of substitutes exists, solid oxide’s efficiency offers defense. The moat’s sustainability is robust in the medium term, supported by a licensing model that creates switching costs, but faces long-term challenges from increasing competition, particularly from China. Continued R&D is crucial for maintaining technological leadership.

## Conclusion

Ceres Power meets all of my criteria, just about to begin commercial operations, has industry-leading technology, and a clear route to profitability.

It will not be the first to market; Bloom Energy was first, and it has a very different business model, which seems to offer advantages and allow the technology to scale.

The risk is that they may struggle to find new production partners. In the coming months, we will see how Doosan fairs in the market. If the product is a success, I expect multiple production partners to emerge, Doosan to scale its operations, and Delta to move quickly to production.

This type of investing is always risky, but this one appears to be quite balanced.
