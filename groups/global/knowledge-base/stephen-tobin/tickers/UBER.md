## Company Overview

Uber Technologies is the global leader in ride-hailing and is positioning itself as the dominant platform operator in the emerging robotaxi sector. Unlike manufacturers who own the autonomous vehicle technology, Uber is pursuing an asset-light model — partnering with multiple autonomous vehicle manufacturers to provide rides on its platform.

**Robotaxi strategy:** Platform-first, asset-light. Uber has signed agreements with over 15 autonomous vehicle manufacturers globally, targeting all major markets. The strategy is to be the intermediary between AV manufacturers and passengers, leveraging Uber's massive existing customer base and brand.

**Key partnerships (as of March 2026):**

- **NVIDIA Hyperion** (foundational agreement): 100,000 vehicles targeting production in 2027. Mercedes, Stellantis, and Lucid as foundational manufacturers
- **Rivian**: \$1.25 billion investment. Targeting 10,000 taxis by 2028, option for additional 40,000 by 2030
- **Wayve (Europe)**: \$1.5 billion Series D investment. Planning launch in London with expansion to 10 more cities
- **WeRide (Middle East)**: 1,200 vehicles in Riyadh by 2027, planned to extend to 15 cities
- **Nissan + Wayve (Japan)**: Tokyo launch in late 2026
- **Pony AI**: Agreement covering Middle East and Europe

**Stephen's analysis:** Uber is "showing similar commitments" to the enormous robotaxi CAGR forecast and is not the only ride-hailing company expanding at this pace. However, Stephen raises a strategic concern: "UBER says it is asset light but is investing so much in its manufacturers I don't really accept that argument." There is also a fundamental question about how much robotaxis actually help Uber: "there is every possibility they will sell the same number of trips, but customers will just be getting into a different vehicle and probably paying less for the journey due to increased competition." Uber's platform monopoly advantage could be challenged if manufacturers develop their own apps.
