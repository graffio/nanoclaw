## Company Overview

Blue Owl Capital is an alternative asset manager with \$300 billion in assets under management. It operates as a bank for middle-market companies (revenues \$50M to \$1B) and provides very large loans that traditional banks cannot service under regulatory constraints.

**Revenue model:** OWL charges a 1% management fee on assets and a performance fee (a cut of profits above a predetermined level). 90% of earnings come from permanent capital — money locked in for many years — providing a steady income stream.

**Key fund structure:**

- Blue Owl Capital Corp (NYSE: OBDC) — business development company, traded on NYSE with daily liquidity
- Blue Owl Technology Income Corp (OTIC) — non-traded fund focused on software and technology lending
- OBDC II — the fund at the center of the crisis; a perpetual, non-traded BDC priced at NAV, where investors must wait for tender offers to exit

**Lending focus:** Two main areas — SaaS company lending (smaller deals) and AI infrastructure financing (massive deals with hyperscalers).

**AI Infrastructure deals:** OWL wants to finance Big Tech's AI buildout with "iron-clad" agreements. The flagship example is the META Hyperion data center JV in Louisiana:

- META owns 20% of the JV, OWL owns 80% (META keeps it off balance sheet by staying below 25% ownership)
- OWL provides \$30 billion to build the facility
- META pays rent to the JV for 30 years regardless of occupancy or usage
- META contracted to fund any cost overruns
- Similar deals being negotiated with MSFT, AMZN, and GOOG

## Investment Thesis

**Stephen's analysis (Feb 2026):** OWL is not a portfolio holding and was analyzed in the context of a potential credit crisis. Stephen's weekly update explored whether the OBDC II redemption halt signaled systemic risk comparable to the 2008 credit crunch.

**What happened:** On Feb 19, 2026, Blue Owl announced it was restricting quarterly redemptions from OBDC II. Redemptions had surged 20% in 2025 to \$150 million after the company raised the redemption cap from 5% to 17%. OWL sold \$1.4 billion of the fund's assets to preserve liquidity and placed the fund into liquidation mode — returning money each quarter through distributions equal to loan repayments and asset sales.

**Stephen's assessment of the risk:** The current share price values OWL's loans at 82% of its assets. Management clearly thinks this is a misprice:

- Recently started a \$300 million share repurchase scheme
- CEO and CFO making multi-million dollar personal purchases at market
- Multiple other directors buying smaller amounts
- Recent \$1.4 billion asset sale was sold to North American pension funds at 98% of NAV

**Credit quality:** Non-accrual rate of 2.30% at cost, written down to 1.10% at fair value. Interest coverage ratio indicates OWL customers have 2x the EBITDA required to repay loans. However, 23% concentration risk in software sector.

**The "double whammy" paradox:** Stephen identified a logical contradiction in the market's bearish case — OWL is being punished both for (a) SaaS customers at risk of AI disruption AND (b) AI infrastructure deals that may not work out. "The two things are mutually exclusive — if AI disrupts the SaaS industry then OWL AI infrastructure bets pay off. They can't both fail."

**Stephen's personal interest:** "If shares in NYSE:OWL drop much more, I will be buying, not in the Strategic Waves portfolio but in my long-term family fund. If I could invest in the funds, I would. 9.5% return backed up by iron-clad contracts with META, GOOG, and AMZN for 30 years. I like the sound of that business."

**Follow-up (Mar 2026):** Still monitoring. Described it as a "bank run style drop" and said he would buy "when I see clear evidence that the selling pressure has eased."
