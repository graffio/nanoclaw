The portfolio lost almost 9% in March and is heading to one of its worst performances. Since August 2023, I have had four months of double-digit percentage losses, and this could be the fifth.

It's a poor result by any standard, but it reflects the general market's decline and the high-beta nature of our holdings.

Concerns have only intensified; the 48-hour warning from President Trump has significantly increased the probability of a “Black Monday,” a big drop at opening, followed by prolonged selling.

We have had four Black Mondays: in 1929, a 12.8% drop was part of the Great Depression; in 1987, a 23% drop was part of a wider sell-off, and in 2020, COVID-19 induced two consecutive Black Mondays. March 9th saw an 8% drop, and March 16th a 13% drop. (I was in the trading pit for the 1987 crash, working for the Bank of America; my floor was closed shortly afterwards, and I decided to go to Business School)

All of these Black Mondays have been part of a bigger move, and they have not usually been the start of the move lower, just an accelerating crystallization of the problem.

Currently, the situation is reminiscent of the previous black Mondays and of the credit crunch, when, from October 2007 to March 2009, US markets dropped by more than 50%.

I covered the problems currently brewing in the private credit markets recently when I looked at OWL. There are signs that contagion is spreading. The problems started last summer with the failure of First Brands and Tricolor, after a quiet period OWL halted redemptions in February, this week Cliffwater limited redemptions followed by Morgan Stanley and Black Rock. The potential for contagion became clear when Deutsche Bank revealed a 26 billion euro exposure.

President Trump’s threat and the Iranian retaliatory threat that followed have induced some panic amongst investors. The S&P is down 5% this month following a 1% drop last month, so we are not actually in bear territory yet; however, Monday could change that.

One final point is that these are exactly the situations when the market changes direction, panic and chaos everywhere- strong conviction that the market is going to drop into freefall- it is the classic “blood on the streets” scenario- so real care is needed in managing your portfolio.

## Robot taxis:- Generational Wealth for Some

Autonomous driving and robotaxis, in particular, are areas I identified last year as poised to take off. It is a classical emerging technology investment, a new technology capable of disrupting one of the largest industries on earth. Grandview Research, one of the analyst companies I use, estimates the market will grow from $0.6 billion in 2025 to $147 billion in 2030, representing a CAGR of 99% and quite unprecedented.

I am already invested, but it is time to review the sector to ensure I am on the right horses and consider other sides of the industry. There appear to be several ways to play this market: buy the companies making the sensors, the companies producing the software, the companies making the cars, and the companies running the ride-hailing services. Most companies have some vertical integration, covering more than one area.

## The manufacturers

The manufacturers and the platform operators are both competitors and partners. The platforms own the customers, and the manufacturers own the technology. The platform is asset-light, the manufacturers are asset-heavy. Both are at risk, UBER could commoditize the taxis if it can dictate terms and play one manufacturer against the other but UBER could be sidelined if the manufacturers can build their own apps and develop their own customers.

At this point, we need to add the new manufacturers: Waymo (Google), Tesla, and Zoox (Amazon), each with its own selling points. Zoox is aiming for a premium experience with its self-driving taxis, but most of these companies are going to compete on price, and price comes down to two things. The cost of building a taxi and the cost of operating it, the cost of operating comes down to safety. Insurance costs will be related to safety incidents and time on the road will be related to repair time after crashes.

Waymo appears to have solved the safety issue. Waymo's any-injury data is 0.71 per million miles against human drivers, 3.9 per million miles (in the same area; data released March 19th). That sets the benchmark all others must try to meet.

Tesla is struggling here. The NHTSA has launched an investigation into Tesla’s FSD, publishing the folloing figures

Of course, the figures are not completely fair because the Tesla data includes all of its FSD vehicles rather than just robotaxis, but federal robotaxi data for Austin, Texas, where both Tesla and Waymo operate, suggests that Tesla’s incident rate is 3x higher than Waymo’s.

Indeed, a Tesla robotaxi appears 9 x worse than a Human driver with 1 incident every 55,000 miles, whereas Waymo is 3 x safer with an incident once every 1,400,000 miles (a human driver has an incident once every 500,000 miles).

The safety issue is generally accepted to be due to sensors; Tesla uses only cameras, whereas all other companies use cameras, LiDAR, and Radar at a minimum (most also have acoustic sensors).

Tesla’s next software update may solve the issue v14 “reasoning” update is due soon.

Tesla's Cybercab has a target production price of only $30,000, whereas Waymo's is currently nearly $150,000. If Tesla can solve the safety issue, it looks like a clear winner; however, Chinese companies have dramatically cut their production costs.

## Conclusions

Robotaxis are likely to be the most disruptive technology for the next few years, a 99% CAGR is off the charts. Fortunes will be made, and multiple companies will do extremely well. I want to make sure I have my money on the right ones.

Pony and WeRide appear to have a cost advantage, Waymo has a first-mover advantage and a safety advantage. Tesla needs its next upgrade to increase safety or they are unlikely to get full approval, autonomous cars having more crashes than humans is never going to work. If Tesla can sort out the safety issue, it will have an advantage over the entire field.

In ride-hailing, Uber is leading the way, but Didi is making great strides in China, which is scaling far more quickly than elsewhere. A battle will be fought between the manufacturers and the platforms and as yet the winner is not clear, UBER says it is asset light but is investing so much in its manufacturers I don’t really accept that argument. How much will Robotaxis help UBER remains to be seen, there is every possibility they will sell the same number of trips, but customers will just be getting into a different vehicle and probably paying less for the journey due to increased competition.

Numerous manufacturers are entering this field, key ones I will be evaluating are Rivian and Stellantis, both of which seem to have signed marque deals.

Baidu appears to be developing vertically integrated operations at scale. Bolt has jumped ahead in Europe, but Grab is making ground in Asia, so they are now on my list.
