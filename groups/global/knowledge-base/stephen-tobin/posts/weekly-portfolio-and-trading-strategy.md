## Emerging Technology Investing

I invest exclusively in emerging technology companies, often pre-commercialization, generally pre-revenue, and almost always pre-profit.

Emerging technology refers to **new and innovative technologies that are still in their early stages of development or adoption, but have the potential to significantly impact society, industries, and economies.** They are often characterized by:

-   **Novelty:** They introduce new concepts, principles, or applications that differ significantly from existing solutions. This can involve entirely new inventions or fresh applications of existing technologies.
    
-   **Rapid evolution:** Emerging technologies typically develop and improve at a fast pace, with continuous breakthroughs and discoveries expanding their capabilities.
    
-   **High impact:** They have the potential to disrupt existing industries or create entirely new ones.
    
-   **Uncertainty:** Due to their early stage, there's often uncertainty about their long-term viability, scalability, widespread adoption, and potential impacts.
    

## **Trading Strategy**

I have adapted the work of others to some of the learnings I have made from following emerging technology stocks.

All emerging technologies follow a path to profit; they begin with a technological breakthrough or a new idea, and it can take decades to get from that point to a profit-making product.

The speed of adoption and maturity of the technology is different in each case, but it is never overnight.

### Google Case Study

The World Wide Web was invented in 1989, Google was not incorporated until 1998, and had an IPO in 2004. The IPO price was $85, and it remained (split-adjusted) close to that price until 2019, when it started to accelerate and has since returned more than 8,000%.

One company, Gartner, has made a successful business by helping companies understand and implement new technologies. They are an American management consultancy firm started by former IBM employees in 1979, today they have over $6 billion in revenue and help large companies analyse and integrate new technology.

In 1995, Gartner published a research piece, “The Gartner Hype Cycle Framework,” that discusses a typical pattern in the maturity of emerging technologies and was distilled into a graphical representation by the report's author, Jackie Fenn.

## The Hype Cycle

The hype cycle is a key element of my trading plan; it is a visual representation of the path to adoption for emerging technologies, and I adapt it to give a path to profitability for emerging technology stocks.

I try to time my entry in the slope of enlightenment, aiming to enter one or two years before the company experiences substantial growth in its income.

When a company launches with a new exciting technology, it typically draws a lot of attention, receives editorial coverage in business newspapers and magazines. Online outlets that need to feed our desire for information constantly produce positive articles, which translate to hundreds and thousands of social media posts about the company.

It leads to a buying frenzy; the true driver of share price is buying and selling, so the price moves higher. A fast-moving price attracts more buying, and in our democratized share-dealing times, where we can buy at the click of a smartphone button, a new technology company can receive an avalanche of buy orders.

As I explained with the Google example, it could be twenty or thirty years before this company starts to deliver on its promise, but the hype cycle can come at any time and drive short-term price movement.

At the top of the hype cycle, Gartner's “the peak of inflated expectations,” short sellers see an opportunity. They point out the risks that lie ahead, news anchors discuss how the value has detached from the fundamentals, and the online experts who advised you to buy now tell you how stupid it is to own the stock, and the hype cycle goes into reverse with an avalanche of selling, causing the stock to crater.

## The Time To Buy

The perfect time to buy Google was 2019/2020; the previous 15 years saw small increases, but many other stocks and assets performed far better.

So what happened? In 2022, Google reported an 80% increase in operating income, following a period of single-digit growth.

The smart money investors had been following Google and realized in 2019/2020 that profit growth was on the short-term horizon, and they started buying. The share price began to front-run the coming business improvement.

I am not a smart money guy, but I have developed a trading plan that applies the Gartner Hype cycle to stock prices and links it with the learnings from Google and numerous other successful emerging technology companies.

I try to buy emerging technology stocks a year or two before they see growth in their business fundamentals.

### ABAT- A case study

In 2020, ABAT revealed its plan to recycle end-of-life EV batteries; it was obviously a good idea. Tesla was in full growth mode and EV’s were widely planned to take over all automobiles.

The hype cycle went game on, and the share price of ABAT went from below $1 in January 2020 to $73 in January 2021 as retail investors piled into this small company with a genius idea likely to be as big as Tesla in no time. The average retail investor had missed out on Tesla, but was not going to miss out this time.

In 2021, the short seller Spruce Capital published a report that, along with some scurrilous and unproven allegations, made specific observations.

-   We have no proof that the technology will work
    
-   We don't know the cost of building a recycling plant
    
-   We don't know what the cost of a battery made from this recycled material will be
    

And probably most importantly

-   It will be at least 10 years before any of these EV batteries need recycling.
    

Spruce was partially correct; LICY was ABAT's only competitor, trying to do exactly the same thing. Unfortunately, they did not manage to get the process working; the plant was too expensive to build, and they ran out of money, going bankrupt before any EV batteries needed recycling.

That is why I analyse all the companies in a sector, not just specific individuals and wait.

I have to pick the winners and then pick the time to invest.

I do this by listening to interviews, reading industry literature, following all of the competitors in the market, and speaking with employees of ABAT and any related company who will talk to me.

Subscribers have access to the full trade alert I published, containing all the information suggesting now is the time to act.

I may not be smart money, but I am following what I have seen smart money do and getting into these companies as they approach commercialization, exit the trough of disillusionment, Gartner would say.

The risk with my plan is the hype cycle, as soon as the prices go vertical, I have to decide if this is hype. Or is it real?

If it is hype the move may unwind as quickly as it arrived and we could lose all of the fantastic gains we made last week, if it is real we will likely see a small retraction before a continued move higher.

It is all about good decisions. I make mine using the Hype Cycle and deep research into the companies I follow.
