# Introduction

Energy Recovery (ERII) has been on the buy list for nearly a year, and a potential entry point may be approaching.

## The Background

ERII has a patented pressure exchange device (PX) that transfers pressure from a high pressure stream to a low pressure one, it is a simple idea but is extremely effective. The two streams of liquid approach the cylindrical PX device from opposite directions, the high pressure stream causes the device to rotate, this rotation pressurises the low pressure stream transferring the pressure from one stream to another. Y[ou can read about the tech on this page](https://energyrecovery.com/px-technology/)

It is not an emerging technology; ERII has been selling these devices for several years and has built a market-leading position supplying large-scale desalination sites. These are mega projects around the world, and as the need for clean water grows, more countries are looking to use their limitless supplies of saltwater to provide much-needed drinking water. North Africa, the Middle East, India, and China are the largest markets, but Southern Europe and Central America are growing.

De-salination forces seawater through a membrane at high pressure and is very energy intensive; the PX device transfers the pressure from the clean water leaving the membrane to the seawater approaching it. The device has been shown to save as much as 25% of the electricity used at sites.

How is this an emerging technology opportunity?

Like many companies, ERII is looking to improve its business fundamentals by reducing costs and increasing sales. Therefore, it has been searching for vertical markets that might benefit from the PX technology. So far, it has discovered two: wastewater and CO2 refrigeration.

The wastewater vertical fits our investment profile in a similar way to Aris Water. The PX allows companies to clean dirty water with less energy and in this vertical saves about 20% of energy bills.

The CO2 refrigerant vertical is a genuine emerging technology that I am keen to invest in.

Supermarket fridges and freezers, of which there are many, are being forced to change their refrigerant by regulation. At present, the dominant technology is Hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs), and it has been the main refrigerant of the industry for over three decades. HFCs were developed as a replacement for CFCs, phased out from the late 80s after the discovery of a hole in the ozone layer. The banning of CFCs showed how effective this type of regulation can be as the hole in the ozone layer has now closed.

HFCs do not harm the ozone layer; however, they have now been shown to be one of the most potent greenhouse gases and are being banned worldwide.

CO2 appears to be becoming the preferred replacement for HFCs. However, it is not the only possibility,; the others have significant downsides.

Hydrocarbons (propane, etc) are efficient, but their highly flammable nature is problematic, and they have a limited charge size.

Ammonia is also efficient but toxic and requires specialist handling and safety equipment, making it uneconomical.

Hydrofluoroolefins (HFOs) are both less effective and more expensive.

Various gas mixes and novel chemicals are being tested.

It seems likely that naturally occurring CO2 is the lead candidate for replacing HFCs and has been capturing more than 40% of the market in the last couple of years. It is a huge market greater than $40 billion in 2023, growing with a CAGR greater than 5%.

The main problems with CO2 are the amount of electricity used and its ability to cool when the ambient temperature gets to 35 degrees Celsius. This is where the PX comes in. ERII have redesigned the PX device to work with gas, it is the same concept and basic operation but uses higher levels of engineering tolerances.

The PX device has been installed in 30 supermarkets worldwide (that is below the company target of 50) and an independent engineering company has been monitoring the performance of the PX. Last month, they published a white paper showing a 15-20% reduction in energy use and much-improved cooling performance at 35 degrees. In the coming weeks, I will be going through this white paper more thoroughly to ensure the findings agree with the data, I was skeptical at first glance (as you may know, I am a career mathematician, so this kind of work is my thing).

ERII is the only company providing pressure exchange devices in this market, and the competition comes from companies that supply other coolants.

# The Opportunity

In the last earnings call, ERII discussed the performance of its mega project desalination sites. To hit annual guidance, which they reaffirmed, they will have to book 45% of 2024 revenue in Q4, and the CEO and CFO mentioned several times the possibility that revenue will drift into 2025. They stressed that the drift will not affect the company's fundamentals, just the timing of deliveries that are controlled by the progress of construction work at these huge installations.

As we all know, a large revenue miss will bring the share price down, perhaps significantly.

The CO2 business is at a critical point; ERII needs OEM fridge manufacturers to adopt the technology in new devices and offer it as a retrofitting service to existing CO2 fridges. The option should be energy-reducing, cost savings with a very short payoff time. That is, if the white paper headline results are a true reflection of the data and what is happening in the field.

Earnings are due in mid-February. By then, I will have completed my analysis of the white paper and will listen to the earnings call, looking for evidence of sales traction with the OEM fridge makers. If we receive a revenue miss combined with clear evidence of sales traction on CO2, it would be the perfect scenario for us, and I will buy.

It will likely be more nuanced, it nearly always is. If so I will try to arrange an interview with the CEO and dig out some specifics about the white paper and the traction with OEMs. If it works out, it should be a great trade as the two new verticals are likely to triple the revenue of this company in the next couple of years.
