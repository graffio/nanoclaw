## Commercial Validation

This is a key concept for my strategy. It tries to decide when a technology stops being a story and becomes a reality.

I will not invest in a company until it is selling its products and those products are being used, not tested or researched, but actually used in the final destination.

It is this that keeps me out of technologies like solid batteries. These products are sold only for evaluation, testing, and research purposes. They are not yet commercially validated, when solid batteries are being sold to go into cars you and I can buy they will be commercially validated. Today they are being sold to companies that are testing them, which makes them technically validated, but that is not enough for my strategy. The step to commercial validation could and does take years, and I never want my capital tied up for years.

# **Aurora vs Kodiak AI:**

I was considering increasing my investment in Aurora Innovation (AUR). They are making outstanding progress, and the recent Detmar deal provides, I think, clear evidence of a pathway to profitability and operational scale.

George, a subscriber whose input I rely on, was questioning the profitability of Aur, so I presented the following argument.

_“No denying Aurora's market cap and current revenue are out of step, and that people are using the forecast revenue to justify the valuation. It comes down to what the future revenue will actually be. The recent Permian basin deal made me really consider the value of Aurora's product, which enables Detmar to run its 30 trucks 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. How many human drivers would that take? I estimate 130, 3 shifts a day, plus weekends, holidays, and sick time. Average cost per driver in the US is somewhere near $70k, so 130x70=$9 million a year. On top of that, they have fewer incidents, lower fuel, insurance, and maintenance costs, plus no unions to deal with. I don’t yet know what they will pay Aurora but anything around $7 million a year seems realistic. That is just one tiny trucking company in one small area. If you scale it up for the whole of the US I can see billions of dollars of revenue in very short order. The other important point is that none of the competitors have yet managed to get approval to begin testing on the road. Aurora might have up to 5 years without any serious competition. As a guide, Aurora’s biggest customer, Uber Freight, has more than 500,000 trucks in its network. If you agree that Detmar is worth $7 mil a year, it means Uber is worth around $116 billion a year. Of course, I don’t know the time frame or have any details on the real prices or the limitations of service, but the potential seems almost endless.”_

My argument convinced me (not sure about George) so I put AUR back on the prospect list and completed a full deep dive.

That DD as always looked at all the competitors and moved Kodiak (KDK) the new de-spac autonomous driving company from a potentially busted SPAC to a possible high risk investment.

## Why Kodiak?

Kodiak AI (KDK) and Aurora Innovation (AUR) have adopted fundamentally different technological architectures, which dictate their divergent go-to-market strategies. While Aurora focuses on deep Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) integration and high-definition (HD) mapping for long-haul freight, Kodiak prioritizes a modular, map-less approach that enables flexibility across industrial, defense, and commercial verticals.

Kodiak's architecture is defined by its proprietary "SensorPods," which are modular hardware units containing cameras, radar, and LiDAR that can be swapped out or retrofitted onto various truck models

Kodiak is focusing on the off-road market; its technology is designed to operate without HD maps, making it suitable for a sizable market across forestry, oil and gas, mining, and defence verticals. These verticals are neither well suited to Aurora or one it is targeting, none of the other potential competitors are targeting the vertical implying that Kodiak may be able to establish a market leading position.

The deal with Atlas Energy, which has recently expanded its agreement with Kodiak from 30 trucks to 100, provides commercial validation of the technology and gives Kodiak a first-mover advantage. 100 trucks is the largest order to date for any autonomous trucking company. Atlas operates in the Permian and delivers sand across private roads to drilling facilities; they are a proof-of-concept customer. Having taken delivery and operated 30 trucks they are taking another 70. The existing 30 are operating 24/7 on private roads.

Commercial validation is a major part of the plan, and Atlas provides it for Kodiak.

My model assumes that Kodiak develops its off-road business quickly and does not focus all of its efforts on highway driving. A fleet of 1,500 trucks (revenue $250k per truck @ 30% gross margin) should deliver profitability and positive free cash flow. The Atlas deal with one customer running the product on a limited route taking 100 trucks, making the 1,500 seem achievable.

I assume that Kodiak will have limited success on the road, perhaps securing a minor market share relative to Aurora’s major share, but will have a cushion from the quick adoption of its off-road technology.

This multiple route to market circumvents one of the biggest issues these technologies face: delays in adoption.

## The Verticals

Kodiak first moved into the “Industrial” vertical. Off road/ private roads and the safety case is complete. This industry is estimated to be over $50 billion worldwide and Kodiak is the first mover. Often these use cases are in remote areas where human drivers are difficult to find; it could see much quicker adoption than the highly regulated highway vertical. Kodiak has now performed 5,200 hours of paid driverless operations in this vertical up 166% in Q3.

The defense vertical is an obvious market for their tech, its ability to drive off highway and adapt to the limits of the vehicle it is fitted to give it enormous potential to be adopted in war zones or just hazardous areas. Kodiak has a $30 million contract with the US army and has fitted the tech onto multiple vehicles for evaluation. I will be watching progress here in 2026 as it could be a meaningful driver of profits and hence share price appreciation.

Highway driving. Kodiak is making progress here but lags behind Aurora, the safety case is only 78% complete but Kodiak has guided to 100% this year. They have several large customer/partners and are shipping freight between their hubs in Dallas and Houston with a safety driver on board.

## Hardware

Aurora is pursuing an OEM business model, where its technology is fitted to specific new trucks and sold to potential users. Kodiak systems can be retrofitted onto existing trucks of any manufacturer.

The Kodiak driver uses AI software and vehicle-agnostic hardware developed on a modular basis. The “sensing Pods” can be replaced by existing automotive engineers and the off road vertical is already operating in all weather conditions.

Kodiak do not use HD maps, a key differentiator that allows them to work off road. The software is an end-to-end AI that makes decisions as it goes rather than relying on maps. It is this technology that opens up the off-road and defence verticals.

The systems are currently operational on class 8 vehicles, Ford F-150s and treaded military vehicles. This quarter they expanded to hauling two trailers (pulling doubles) an advanced driving technique not supported by Aurora but operational in the Permian with Kodiak.

## Partnerships

Kodiak is a capital-efficient and somewhat asset-light operation; its costs are a fraction of Aurora’s because it relies heavily on partners.

The hardware is modular; sensing pods, steering and actuation systems, and the AI brain can be fitted and upgraded independently.

Kodiak has a partnership with Roush to retrofit its technology. Roush has built a dedicated line to fit the system. We have a lot of experience with Roush through our investments in Bluebird, Roush developed and manufactured the Bluebird petrol and propane engines that drove our profits in Bluebird. They are a high quality small volume auto manufacturer, extremely well managed, and producing high-quality outputs

This modular hardware system probably reduces the risks of the technology in the chosen verticals of defense or off-road. The system is already operating on Ford F-150 Super Duty trucks as well as military vehicles for the US Army.

Kodiak has designed sensor pods that attach to the wing mirrors of trucks (or to each front corner as a minimum) and has entered into an agreement with Bosch to manufacture them in volume. It means they can quickly adapt to new sensing technology as it is developed. Bosch will be providing production-grade systems for use by OEMs

ZF, the global automotive tier 1 probably the world’s leading supplier of steering systems, has been making the steering and actuation system that includes steering and braking redundancy. ZF provides the equipment that Roush fits to existing vehicles. The partnership was renewed and expanded recently

This is a high-quality line of partners, global Tier 1 suppliers who have committed significant capital to the venture and bring provenance to the product when it is being looked at by trucking companies. It does, of course, add supply chain risk.

## Finances

Kodiak has shareholder equity of only $3 million and debt of $33 million, which is a precarious position, but it has improved from the negative equity position following the launch. We have seen 180% dilution in the first year.

Insiders own 29% and the general public 28%. The remaining 43% is owned by Wall Street, I like the high insider ownership but it is the 43% Wall Street ownership that makes me believe they can raise the cash.

## Raising the Money

Last time I evaluated Kodiak I decided against investing because I thought this was too much of a risk however I have changed my mind. They have institutional support that will likely give them an outlet for their new shares.

ARK has been on a Kodiak spending spree of late acquiring as many as 60,000 shares in a single week. ARK is also an investor in Aurora, the two ARK analysts who cover AUR and KDK have said there are several catalysts for their investment, including

**The “SaaS” Pivot:** ARK favors Kodiak’s “Kodiak Driver” as a software-first solution that can be integrated across different truck brands (agnosticism).

**The Bosch Partnership:** The January 2026 deal with Bosch to mass-produce autonomous hardware was a major “buy” signal for ARK, as it suggests Kodiak has moved from a research project to a manufacturing-ready company.

**Safety Data:** ARK frequently cites Kodiak’s high safety scores (such as their recent top marks in the Nauto Driver Safety Evaluation) as proof that the technology is ready for driverless commercialization.

## Conclusion

For **risk-tolerant investors**, Kodiak AI appears to offer a better risk/reward profile for 2026, with a significantly lower valuation, a capital-efficient business model, and diversified revenue streams, providing greater upside potential if capital is successfully secured.

For **risk-averse investors**, Aurora Innovation remains the safer “pure-play” choice for long-haul autonomous freight exposure, backed by a fortress balance sheet.

The **critical decision factor** centers on capital market access: Kodiak’s superior risk-adjusted returns depend on successfully navigating near-term financing requirements.

I am risk-tolerant but not risk-stupid, as a result, I am going to buy a half-size position and see how it goes.
