## Trade Alert #73: Buying American Battery Technology (ABAT)

### **Company Overview**

ABAT is a battery materials company that aims to increase domestic U.S. supply of **lithium, nickel, cobalt, and manganese.**

Its success will depend on the quality of its proprietary technology and its ability to scale its production plants in the face of competition from better-funded rivals and other small players.

ABAT has been through the hype cycle we see play out time and time again, with its stock price going from $2 to $70 and back to $2. The big rise was all hype, people jumping onto a new technology without considering the time to commercialization.

### **Business Model and Value Proposition**

ABAT operates through **two business units**.

**Battery Recycling Operations**: The company has designed and commercialized a proprietary system for recycling lithium-ion batteries at its facility near Reno, Nevada.

**Primary Lithium Extraction**: ABAT has purchased and developed lithium-bearing resources in central Nevada, developing proprietary technologies to extract lithium from the claystone mined at Tonopah Flats.

The business model involves providing lithium to battery manufacturers through its mining operation in Nevada, as well as a recycling operation serving the same customers, thereby establishing a closed-loop manufacturing process.

The recycling operation can recycle up to 95% of a lithium-ion battery, not just the lithium, but also the graphite, cathode metals, and intermediate parts, such as aluminum, copper, and plastic.

**Battery Recycling**

The battery recycling sector is set to experience **explosive growth** driven by the "retirement wave" of early EV batteries. This growth trajectory of EV’s creates both substantial opportunities and competitive pressures, as the industry faces a critical minerals crunch with insufficient domestic supplies of nickel, cobalt, and lithium.

With the lifespan of an EV being 8-15 years, 2026 will still be the early stages of the market for recycling them. The market is expected to grow with a CAGR of over 50% in the short term.

Using an 8-year average life span, 2026 should see around 375,000 EV batteries ready for recycling and a million in 2030. It's not just EVs; multiple modes of transport have become electrified, and huge stationary storage facilities are reaching the end of their life. All of these batteries need replacing and recycling. It is going to be a huge market.

ABAT's flagship innovation, and the technology that led to the hype cycle move, is its **battery recycling system** that operates as a feedstock-agnostic platform capable of processing batteries across a wide range of form factors, including packs, modules, cylindrical cells, prismatic cells, pouch cells, defect and intermediate waste cells, metal scraps, slurries, and powders.

The recycling technology employs a unique **two-phase process**.

The first phase involves automated de-manufacturing that sorts and separates subcell components. (Competitors typically shred the entire battery and go straight to a recycling process.)

The second hydrometallurgical phase refines the intermediate Black Mass, producing battery-grade lithium hydroxide nickel, cobalt, and manganese.

The initial step delivers a significant competitive advantage; all technologies in this field produce black mass and then either sell it or continue with further processing but the main competition cannot extract the copper, aluminium, plastics and often not even the lithium.

**Primary Lithium Extraction**

**Supply chain localization** has emerged as a strategic priority, with governments investing heavily in domestic capacity. ABAT's Tonopah Flats facility is being fast-tracked (under Fast Track 41 legislation) by the regulatory authorities.

ABAT mines lithium-bearing claystone and then extracts the lithium. ABAT has developed proprietary technologies that avoid the inefficient evaporation ponds associated with conventional lithium-from-brine mining. The company's extraction process employs a selective leaching method for low-cost extraction of lithium from claystone sedimentary resources, resulting in significantly lower acid consumption, reduced levels of contaminants in the generated leach liquor, and lower overall production costs.

In April 2025, EXIM **Bank sent ABAT an LOI for $900 million in funding to develop the Tonopah FLATS extraction facility.** The loan is still subject to further due diligence, but it is part of the US administration's attempts to onshore production of these critical minerals. The bank financing would seriously de-risk any investment in ABAT.

Additionally, ABAT was awarded a **$1 million collaborative agreement with Argonne National Laboratory's ReCell Center** in July 2025 to advance novel lithium manufacturing technologies. This partnership validates the company's technological approach and provides access to cutting-edge research capabilities.

### Lithium Mining

When I wrote my second article, they were still in the exploration stage, estimating reserves and conducting assay tests to determine whether the lithium could be economically extracted. At the time, lithium carbonate was selling for $29,000 per tonne, and ABAT estimated they had 15.8 million tonnes available.

Today, Lithium carbonate prices have fallen to $23,000 per tonne with ongoing oversupply affecting the market. The market is constantly changing, and a shortage of supply is expected to start appearing in mid-2026 which should begin to drive prices higher, in the last shortage (late 2022), prices went as high as $79,000 per tonne.

The project has now progressed beyond the evaluation stage and is moving towards commercialization. In January this year, they completed the latest test drills and increased the estimated volume of economically extractable lithium to 21 million tonnes, making it the largest deposit in North America.

ABAT has built and tested a pilot lithium hydroxide extraction plant and is moving forward with the construction of a commercial-scale site.

In May, the pilot site moved towards continuous operation with a 2-week-long run. The lithium hydroxide produced has been sent to customers, and negotiations towards long-term off-take agreements continue.

The commercial-scale plant is expected to produce 30,000 tonnes a year, which is revenue in excess of $600 million a year at today's prices.

Following the January results, the project was designated for fast tracking by the US federal government under the Fast-41 program, and following a feasibility study, the EXIM Bank signed a letter of intent saying it was interested in funding the project by providing a $900 million loan.

A government grant of $115 million has been awarded to help fund the lithium extraction plant.

The general public owns 78% of ABAT, that’s not ideal and often leads to price volatility. Insiders own 4%. Shareholders suffered almost 60% dilution this year and more can be expected as they scale operations.

ABAT has no Wall Street coverage and did not have any analyst questions at its recent earnings call. That is not as unusual as it sounds; indeed, neither Electrovaya, Hesai, nor D-Wave had any coverage when I first invested.

The majority of the CEO’s package (87%) is in the form of shares, suggesting good alignment with shareholders.

At the end of the last quarter ABAT had shareholder equity of $66 million and total debt of $7 million.

They sold a property in Fernley for $6.75 million and some water rights for $4.7 million which will add $11 million to the cash resources when the deals are finalized.

Total assets of $77 million seem stable, but cash declined by almost $23 million to $2.8 million from December 2024 to March this year (with an additional $5 million of restricted). Current liabilities dropped $5 million in the period as debt was paid down.

The company relies on external financing $26 million raised last year and $28 million the year before.

ABAT has less than 1 year of cash so it is a pressing challenge.

Revenue is accelerating and margin has improved dramatically but it is still negative.

Current operational performance can be characterized as rapidly improving but still fundamentally challenged.

The CEO said, “We still have large amounts of government grants under contract, including transferable tax credits, and we continue to work to monetize these.”

**Peer Benchmarking and Competitive Context**

**Li-Cycle** (now part of Glencore)**, Redwood Materials, Ascend Elements, Aquametals, Recyclico and Cirba Solutions** are the primary North American competitors.

Redwood presents a significant challenge; JB Straubel, the co-founder of Tesla, left Tesla to become CEO of Redwood in 2019. The company has raised almost $2 billion in funding but remains private. Redwood has contracts with a host of EV makers, including Tesla, VW, BMW, and battery manufacturers like Panasonic.

I contend that ABAT has a significant and sustainable competitive advantage over Redwood that will enable it to operate at higher margins and ultimately make more profit.

The advantage is technological.

There are three ways to recycle batteries: direct recycling, pyrometallurgy, and hydrometallurgy.

Direct recycling is the simplest method; it involves removing the cathode material and putting it into another battery. No processing is involved, and everything except the cathode is lost. None of ABAT’s competitors is using this method.

Redwood employs pyrometallurgy, a well-established, decades-old battery recycling process. This method is easy to implement due to readily available equipment and well-known techniques. It involves burning batteries in a furnace to recover non-combustible materials (black mass,) separated from slag after cooling. A significant drawback of pyrometallurgy is the loss of graphite, electrolyte, plastics, aluminium, manganese, and lithium due to high temperatures. Only cobalt, copper, and nickel are recovered.

Probably the biggest barrier to entry for battery recycling companies is regulatory approval. You need a lot of permits. Pyrometallurgy is challenging to get approved because of the waste output from the blast furnaces; it is also costly, requiring vast amounts of energy. ABAT believes Redwood is having problems with their permitting work, which is slowing down the proposed growth in site installations. They had planned to build a furnace near every gigafactory, but that appears not to be happening.

They also believe Redwood is having problems with their feedstock suppliers, as multiple large-name OEM companies have moved to ABAT from Redwood recently, and batteries originally destined for Redwood are being diverted to ABAT. (**source:** interview with current Head of Quality at ABAT)

ABAT is using hydrometallurgy, which is a new process and is far more complicated requiring the invention of new methods and techniques. The batteries are placed underwater (they discharge at this point, removing fire risk) and are selectively dismantled before being ground into small pieces. This produces a liquid stream, a black mass slurry, that is dried (de-watered, they call it, as they use centrifugal driers and paddle dryers).

Black Mass that can be sold to customers for further recycling or, in the case of ABAT, goes into a second process where chemical extraction methods are used to recover the precious metals.

Hydrometallurgy is very efficient; 95% of the battery materials are recovered. It is low energy, because no heat is used, and has minimal environmental impact as the water is recycled through the system.

Other key competitors, Ascend Elements, Cirba Solutions, Li-cycle, Aqua Metals, and Recyclico, have all chosen to go down the Hydro route and are developing proprietary solutions.

The private Cirba Solutions, with an established network for recovering and recycling household batteries has several facilities some using Pyrometallurgy. It appears to be having trouble signing deals. The CEO said in the last earnings call the industry is **"very competitive right now"** with intense competition for feedstock access, noting that OEMs want to "control their supply chain and drive cost down and basically squeeze everybody's margin down to below 10%".

Ascend Elements is another growing private player, currently building a second large-scale recycling plant. They have a patented Hydro–cathode technology, taking the battery and producing precursors to cathode materials. Ascend quotes good recovery rates for the cathode metals and low cost operations but does not manage to recover the other metals and plastics

Aquametals (AQMS) is having trouble scaling and with a market cap of $5 million following a 90% share price fall in the last 12 months, its survival is in question.

Recyclico has seen a recent uptick in its share price, from $0.03 to $0.08 in the last three months following its termination of plans to build a JV site in Taiwan. It remains firmly in penny stock territory but does have some good patents and “bench-level” techniques.

Li-Cycle looked for some time like it might be the long-term winner in this industry; however, it grew too quickly, started to build a European facility before its first US facility was working well, and had not developed the technology necessary to recover metals inside the black mass it was producing. It went bankrupt and was acquired by Glencore, on whom it had relied for funding. Glencore has not announced its plans for the US Li Cycle sites, but appears to be pressing ahead with the European build, which is on one of its sites.

### Conclusion and Price Target

ABAT appears to have the technological edge in recycling when compared to its peer group and is signing long-term OEM contracts for its products.

Its competitive advantage is further solidified by owning the rights to the largest lithium deposits in the US, and the government support it is getting to build its extraction facility for those deposits.

ABAT is making commercial progress, having begun operations at its first recycling plant and seen margins increase. A grant has been made available to fund the construction of a second site with five times the capacity.

ABAT is securing the all-important raw materials of waste batteries, while competitors struggle, and is moving to multi-year contracts with key auto OEM customers.

The primary resource extraction is moving forward; initial samples have been extracted and processed on the pilot line, with samples sent to battery customers. EXIM Bank has written an LOI for almost $1 billion to fund the building of a full-scale facility.

It will take some time for the EXIM loan to be finalized. I would not expect it to happen until Q3 or Q4 2026 at the earliest. However, by then, we will have seen the results of the pilot samples and further assay testing on the recent test drilling.

It is a high-risk investment, with dilution almost a given; however, the management at ABAT has methodically executed their business plan over the last three years, achieving commercial operations before the first wave of EV batteries arrives for processing.

It is not possible to build a mathematical model for ABAT; we have no idea of the margins it will operate at or any guidance towards the volume it can sell in its recycling project. We do not have a start date for the sale of Lithium mined at its own site or an idea of the volume involved.

For a target, we will have to rely on a technical approach and hope to build a model next year. The EW chart suggests $10 in August 2026, and I will set that as the first target.
