# Trading Strategy

I had an interesting conversation with a subscriber during the week that prompted me to review my trading performance and assess whether my plan is the correct one.

I thought I would share the conversation as it took place over email.

**Michael messaged**

_“What was the reason you chose to sell DRSHF in July? I’m not trying to rub it in. Just trying to learn because, fortunately, I did not sell and added considerably to my holdings.”_

DRSHF is the US ticker for the company DroneShield. Here is the chart showing my entry in green and exit in red

[

![Image 5](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/48c83de6-c92a-4392-b098-a66bccd5a11b_979x762.png)



](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!plsC!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F48c83de6-c92a-4392-b098-a66bccd5a11b_979x762.png)

You can see Michael’s point; the price is up 64% since I closed and booked my 84% return.

Michael’s question is an excellent one and points to my “Active Management” trading strategy, a debate that investors have always had.

**Is it not better to buy and hold?**

This was my reply (shortened)

_The newsletter is built on my attempt to trade $250 a month into $100,000 in 5 years. It is designed to be accessible to all investors, especially those with relatively small amounts of capital._

_If my tactic were to buy and hold stocks, I would be limited to one investment of $250 per month, as that is the maximum I add to the account. In August and September, due to profits booked, I was able to buy over $2,300, almost ten times as much as I could if I were a buy-and-hold investor._

_So I didn’t really have a reason to exit DRHSF; it was just part of my trading plan to do so. All subscribers need to develop their own trading plan that suits their individual goals and financial situation._

_It is my job to keep unearthing new interesting companies with the potential to make us big returns._

**That question prompted some reflection.** After a subscriber chat comment suggested re-evaluating our exit strategy, following ONDS' post-exit rise, I decided to verify the soundness of my plan. I wanted to see if a simple buy-and-hold approach would have been more effective. This is precisely why I maintain meticulous records in a spreadsheet—they allow me to revisit past decisions and explore “what if” scenarios.

## My Exit Strategy is close if:

1.  The trade hits its target
    
    1.  This is my preferred approach
        
2.  The trade shows a near-vertical price rise, not justified by company fundamentals
    
    1.  This has accounted for the majority of exits.
        
    2.  It is related to the Hype Cycle, a key part of the overall strategy. I covered it in a [previous weekly update](https://open.substack.com/pub/stephentobin/p/weekly-portfolio-and-trading-strategy?r=nh85d&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&showWelcomeOnShare=false).
        
3.  I need the money to invest in another company.
    
    1.  I have not had to do this since year 1 of the plan; we are now in year 3.
        
4.  Share price falls
    
    1.  When it drops 30% I look closely and make a decision
        
    2.  If it reaches -40% I need a good reason to keep it open, which has never happened.
        

I decided to compare my strategy with two variants of the buy-and-hold approach.

**Firstly,** buying one stock each month with my $250 deposit and holding it. To make it fair, I took the first stock I bought each month, ignoring add-on trades.

**Secondly,** buying every trade over the period, including add-ons, investing $250 each time, and holding.

The table shows the results of all three strategies. (I did the research on Thursday; prices correct at that point)

[

![Image 6](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/cf1aaf27-5d11-4d1c-8974-39705d537686_1052x224.png)

The first strategy is the worst performer and would not have kept pace with the $100,000 targets, falling short by $2,053, or 14%. Choosing the best-performing stock rather than the first taken each month would have boosted returns. However, at the time, I would have had no idea which one was going to be the best, and I would not have invested in any if I thought they were going to underperform. It would have been a classic cherry-picking back test.

The second strategy, buying and holding all trades, makes the biggest profit in terms of dollars ($25,340) and would have built a substantial portfolio with 41 stocks spread across the emerging technologies I follow.

It seems a safer strategy due to its larger portfolio and should continue growing as the small-cap stocks we invest in continue to prosper. It also has an excellent return of 137%.

The Active Management (actual performance) has a better ROI than all three and operates within the fiscal restrictions I have placed on it. It remains above the $100,000 target by $2,663 (18%), recording a profit of $10,733, compared to $6,017 for the buy-and-hold strategy with the same fiscal restriction of $250 per month deposited.

I think the gap between the three strategies will grow over time. The active management strategy has the great benefit of compounding. In the other two methods, I would still be investing $250 in each trade. However, due to trades being closed, in the active management strategy, I am investing $500-$600 in each new trade and increasing position sizes to as much as $750.

The Active Management strategy has a higher risk profile, which is being rewarded with a higher return.

I hope this discussion will help everyone determine the right strategy for them. I think for most, it will be a combination of buy-and-hold and Active Management. Keep the stocks you like the look of and sell the ones you don’t. I will continue with my active management plan and see the five-year project to its conclusion.
