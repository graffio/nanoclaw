## Background

Voyager launched via an IPO in June 2025, it is a neo prime. A new technology defense prime with a significant space division. The company has been put together by acquiring companies with technology that allows it to build out its core technological advantages into a full-stack offering.

The company operates three reporting segments; these segments are synergistic, often selling the same base technology.

- **Defense & National Security:** Operating as the company's largest and fastest-growing division, this segment provides mission-critical technologies including controllable solid propulsion, signals intelligence, and advanced guidance, navigation, and control systems. This segment is tightly aligned with urgent U.S. military modernization priorities, heavily supporting marquee initiatives like the Missile Defense Agency's Next Generation Interceptor (NGI) and the multi-domain Golden Dome architecture.

- **Space Solutions:** This segment specializes in space infrastructure both hardware and software systems. It performs end-to-end space mission engineering, facilitates commercial, academic, and government missions ranging from low-Earth orbit to deep space.

- **Starlab Space Stations:** Representing a generational infrastructure opportunity, this segment manages a Voyager-led, majority-owned global joint venture dedicated to building a commercial replacement for the International Space Station (ISS). The Starlab joint venture operates with its own board and management team, supported by international equity partners including Airbus, Mitsubishi, MDA Space, and Palantir Technologies.

The company has acquired a number of businesses with existing revenue and contracts, and it continues to be acquisitive:

## The Missile Engine

I was made aware of the progress in this area when I spoke to an engineer at Raytheon. In the engineer's view, Voyager has a unique technology that offers significant advantages over current missile engines, and if the technology can fly as expected, it will quickly be adopted in multiple missile projects and upgrades due to its enhanced usability, storage, and cost advantages.

### The Technology

Today, most missiles run on rocket fuel — engines that are expensive, cannot be left fuelled for extended periods, and require expert maintenance. There are significant supply constraints on both engines and fuel; supply chains are long, and much of the technology is produced outside the US.

Liquid-fuel engines have been the only solution for most missiles because they can change speed, allowing a change of direction in flight. The engineers called this "throttleable."

**Voyager has a throttleable solid-fuel missile engine.**

Solid fuels are much cheaper and can be left in the missile, but historically it has been impossible to change the burn once in flight. Solid fuel engines are used in many missiles that have a pre-determined flight path. This is the problem Voyager has solved.

Voyager has secured a major contract with Lockheed Martin to supply its solid-fuel missile engine to serve as a roll-control system and a stage-separation propulsion system for the US Government's Next Generation Interceptor (NGI) program — a ground-based interceptor designed to protect the US homeland from ballistic missile threats.

The industry uses a Technology Readiness Level (TRL) scale:

![TRL Scale](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/3c21e11e-f0cd-4c17-853b-a83bfe3bb795_1103x320.png)

- An Anduril engineer thought VOYG was at TRL4 but said it was a significant technological advancement; he thought the technology was likely to be adopted if proven in NGI, as well as in THAAD, Patriot extended-range, and possibly the SM-3 missile system upgrade.
- A Raytheon engineer noted that Voyager has said they are between TRL8 and TRL9 in public documents and believed they would not be able to say that without Lockheed and the government confirming it. He believed they would not have won the NGI contract if still at TRL4. He believes Voyager has had static firings and sled-based firings.
- The Raytheon engineer said demand had exploded for missiles and no suppliers could keep up; he believed Voyager could alleviate many issues faced by legacy missile engine makers Aerojet and ATK. He also mentioned Raytheon had started buying engines from Nammo in Norway because they just can't get enough.
- The "kill vehicle" can turn off its thrust and coast, as well as perform maneuvers using this new technology — unique to solid-fuel systems.
- During earnings calls, Voyager management said its solid-fuel engines enable precise, scalable thrust maneuvering, improving interceptor maneuverability by 400% and extending missile range by 1,000%.

### Block Upgrades

Missile systems are upgraded in blocks. For the SM-3, they have already undergone 4 block upgrades, and Voyager technology was being designed into future block upgrades.

## Voyager's Full-Stack Strategy

In October 2025, Voyager acquired ExoTerra Resources to bolster propulsion capabilities and later acquired Estes Energetics, which designs and manufactures the solid fuel propulsion material. This gives them a US-based supply chain — a big deal for defense primes.

Voyager's missile guidance and control system has been integrated within Palantir's operating system and is operational on NASA's magnetospheric mission satellites and will be integrated in MDA Space's NGI guidance system. Their dual-use optical guidance technology is integrated in RTX's missile defense initiatives and Millennium Space (Boeing's) small satellite constellation program.

The NGI contract has already been awarded. Management is guiding to 44% midpoint growth in Defense for 2026, with more growth in 2027. If we assume 44% growth in 2026 and 2027, then 2027 revenue would be around \$350 million. Using a standard multiple of 10, we would get a value of \$3.5 billion — more than 100% upside for the company on the defense side alone.

## Space Technology

Voyager provides space technologies including propulsion systems, satellite maneuvering operations, and communications, as well as operating Bishop's Lock — the only permanent privately owned module on the ISS, which allows for supplies and payloads to move between space and the ISS. Voyager is the largest commercial user of the ISS, having deployed over 300 satellites and conducted more than 1,000 missions.

## Starlab — The Second Big Thing

NASA is funding the development of a replacement for the ISS, due to launch in 2029. Phase 1 awarded funding to three consortia, with the largest amount going to StarLab. In January 2025, StarLab became the first consortium to complete the critical design review, and the JV has already signed a launch contract with SpaceX for the new Starship launch vehicle.

Voyager owns 61% of the Starlab JV. The other members are: Airbus, Mitsubishi, MDA Space, Palantir, and Space Applications Services.

### NASA Space Station Competition

There are 4 companies chasing this business:

- **Axiom:** The current incumbent, with permission to attach modules to the ISS. Manufacturing Axiom Hub One, due for launch in early 2028. Has not yet passed the design freeze stage with NASA.
- **Starlab:** Has cleared design review and is in the manufacturing stage. Advantages from strength of JV partners, capital committed, and single-launch nature.
- **Orbital Reef (Blue Origin/Sierra Space):** Largest proposed station, 10-person inflatable habitat. Undergoing burst testing.
- **Vast:** Outsider, no Phase 1 funding, backed by Jed McCaleb. Haven-1 due for launch in 2027.
