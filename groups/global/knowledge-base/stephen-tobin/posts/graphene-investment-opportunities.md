**The History of the Wonder Material**

Graphene was first isolated in 2003 by Andre Geim and Kostya Novoselov at the University of Manchester. In the 20 years since its discovery, companies have been trying, and mostly failing, to develop manufacturing processes that can produce the material in sufficient volume and of sufficient quality to make it a commercial success. (the Scientists won a noble prize for their work).

I have found dozens of companies working on Graphene, from backyard start-ups to large multinationals. The properties of the material make it so unique that anyone can see its potential, but no one has yet managed to realize that potential.

I believe 2026 will be graphene's breakout year, and the target companies may experience explosive revenue growth and potentially exponential share price appreciation.

**What is Graphene**

Graphene is a single sheet of carbon atoms connected in a hexagonal lattice.

## Graphene Properties

It is the thinnest material known; and incredibly lightweight, with one square meter weighing just 0.77 milligrams.

As a single two-dimensional sheet, graphene boasts an exceptionally high surface area of around 2630 square meters per gram (that means 2 grams, 0.07 ounces, would cover an NFL football field). These physical dimensions are crucial for many of graphene's potential applications.

Graphene exhibits exceptionally high electrical conductivity, surpassing copper by up to 70%. (due to floating electrons above each carbon atom in the lattice)

Graphene is the strongest material ever discovered, 100 to 300 times stronger than steel.

A single graphene layer is highly transparent.

Graphene possesses exceptionally high thermal conductivity, with measured values ranging from 3000 to 5300 W/mK at room temperature, surpassing diamond and copper.

Graphene is remarkably flexible and stretchable, capable of stretching up to 20% without breaking. Additionally, it is highly impermeable to most gases and liquids.

## **Graphene Use Cases**

-   **Stronger and lighter composites:** Incorporating graphene into materials such as plastics, concrete, and metals can significantly enhance their strength, stiffness, and durability while reducing their weight. This has applications in aerospace, automotive, and construction industries.
    
-   **Coatings:** Graphene coatings offer excellent corrosion resistance, impermeability, and possess anti-bacterial properties.
    
-   **Water filtration:** Graphene membranes can be used for efficient and cost-effective water desalination and purification.
    
-   **Improved batteries:** Graphene can enhance the performance of lithium-ion batteries by increasing their capacity, charging speed, and lifespan.
    
-   **Solar cells:** Graphene can be used to improve the efficiency and reduce the cost of solar panels.
    
-   **Fuel Cells:** It can enhance the efficiency of fuel cells as a component in electrodes and membranes.
    
-   **Transparent conductive electrodes:** Graphene can replace indium tin oxide (ITO) in touchscreens, flexible displays, and solar cells, offering better performance and flexibility.
    
-   **Sensors:** Graphene's high sensitivity allows for the development of advanced chemical, biological, and pressure sensors.
    
-   **Drug delivery:** Graphene-based nanomaterials can be used to deliver drugs more effectively to targeted areas in the body.
    
-   **Tissue engineering:** Graphene can serve as a scaffold for cell growth and tissue regeneration.
    

## **The Problems**

Mass-producing high-quality, defect-free graphene remains a significant challenge. Ensuring uniformity and reproducibility at large scales is crucial, as defects can impact properties.

The high cost of graphene production and its energy-intensive production techniques have hindered widespread commercialization.

Seamlessly integrating graphene into existing manufacturing processes and devices presents challenges related to compatibility, transfer methods, and processing into usable forms.

## Graphene Manufacturing Landscape

Four publicly listed companies are moving towards large-scale production. They use different and proprietary production methods, target different end users and are at various points in the road to commercialization.

Manufacturers focus on producing graphene that is “good enough” for their specific use case. The better quality Graphene you want, the more it costs to make, and the more difficult it is to maintain quality.

Samsung and LG produce the highest-quality graphene in small quantities to utilize in their advanced semiconductor research. Some Chinese manufacturers produce low-quality graphene to mix with paint, allowing a single tin to go ten times further.

## Conclusion

Graphene is the strongest, lightest, and thinnest material known to man. It is transparent and flexible with high electrical and thermal conductivity. It has the potential to be a huge commercial success.

It has taken 20 years, but commercialization is finally beginning. Volume production techniques have been developed, and numerous applications have been found. The market is becoming segmented, with different qualities of graphene being used for various applications.

The target companies have developed processes that enable them to scale “good enough” graphene production for their chosen use cases. Both have obtained regulatory approval and developed customer contracts, suggesting that 2026 will be their breakout year.
