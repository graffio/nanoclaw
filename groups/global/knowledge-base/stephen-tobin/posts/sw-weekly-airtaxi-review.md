# SW Weekly: AirTaxi Review 

*Market sentiment continues to deteriorate, a move to cash may be warranted*

I looked at the robotaxi market last week, and there are clear parallels between the two sectors that imply take-off for investors might still be a little while away in the eVTOL world. I will present an overview of the sector in this issue.

Firstly, performance, it has been a very tough Q1, and this week was particularly poor. The portfolio is now down more than 13% in March and heading for its worst-ever monthly performance. This time last year I regretted having closed so many positions during the tariff shock and this time I worry I did not close enough.

I have so far exited five positions, all at a loss, and at the moment, it seems likely I will have to exit several more to preserve capital. I am currently below target on my five-year plan for the first time since mid-2024. I have lost $2,469 this month and am now $3,950 under target. It will be hard work to catch up, but the project does not end until August 2028, so I have plenty of time.

I trade emerging technology companies that I think have potential for near-exponential growth. These companies can suffer delays, they are heavily impacted by the markets due to their high risk profile but they can and do deliver strong returns over the longer term, how long they will take to recover and what will happen if the world is plunged into a recession is difficult to predict. Fortunately, I have quite a large cash balance, with a third of the portfolio in cash, so when things do improve, I have plenty of firepower.

The war in Iran is bad for the world, the US and Israel have overwhelming military power but Iran appears to have played its weak position very well and I am starting to wonder how the US gets out of this mess. Boots on the ground, tactical nuclear, or climb down, I hope I am wrong, but it is beginning to look like a disaster in the making.

The probability of a significant market correction is growing. I am not risk-averse and am generally comfortable with drawdown, but market sentiment is turning more bearish, making a sharp correction more likely. In these situations, moving more of the stock to cash makes sense.

## The Hype Cycle

The Gartner Hype Cycle is one of the three waves in the Waves part of the Strategic Waves trading plan. It tries to follow the hype around new technologies and plot their emergence along the proprietary Gartner Hype Cycle.

The Hype Cycle is a representation of investor sentiment as a technology moves from the lab to commercialization. A similar pattern often plays out in the share price of these companies.

[

[Image 3: Image 3](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/9edb3432-050f-4848-b2da-28c370d4ad8f_533x348.png)

As an investor, I aim to buy on the slope of enlightenment. This is when the technology has proven itself and is beginning to commercialize with its first orders, and the companies have concrete plans for scale-up with forecasted volume and profit on the near-term horizon.

The technology trigger is risky; we have all seen these stocks shoot higher after IPO or SPAC and then collapse as investors realize it might be a long time before this technology can generate any income. The trough of disillusionment can last for many years; many investors with a longer-term view hold through this, waiting for better times ahead.

It is impossible to know for certain where you are on the hype cycle until after the fact. I had thought eVTOL operations were already on the slope of enlightenment, with operations close at hand, but I have decided that operations may be close at hand, while revenue and profits may not be.

For Robotaxis, Gartner had 2015 as the peak of inflated expectations and by 2019 put them in the trough of disillusionment. Gartner quoted the edge cases of safety, snow and bad weather, human drivers, and complex junctions proving much harder to anticipate with a lack of high-quality sensors.

Gartner now has robotaxis on the slope of enlightenment as regulatory hurdles are passed, technical barriers are overcome, and fares become competitive with human-driven cabs. Companies are scaling production, unit profitability has arrived and the taxi industry is about to be hit by a huge disruptive force that will change it forever.

For Air Taxis, Gartner has 2025 being the peak of expectations (probably explaining last year’s big wins in JOBY) and 2026 into 2027 being the trough. They cite certification delays, infrastructure costs, and aircraft lift capacity as the primary barriers to overcome.

## Battery issues:

I spend much of my time doing in-person research. You can only gain so much by reading press releases and 10Ks. Management is always too positive about their companies, and I devote a lot of time to talking with people in the industry, often industry journalists are the best point of contact, as well as academic researchers.

There is growing skepticism about the payload these aircraft will have and battery cycle life is a major concern, Vertical Aerospace management often comment on how its battery business- replacing the batteries every year- is going to account for more than half of its business industry insiders are increasingly concerned that cycle life will make profitable operations very difficult and the weight of batteries is just too great.

If you are recharging every flight, there is a clear disconnect on profitability and battery replacement. Most eVTOL companies are guiding to 35 flights a day. It implies a cycle life of 80,000 cycles to complete an entire year, beyond that of any current technology. Electrovaya has the highest cycle life of a lithium battery I know of, and they can only manage 14,000.

Probably the best that could be hoped for, with the high power requirements of flight, is a cycle life of 10,000, allowing for 40-80 days of operations. Changing the batteries every month or two won't be cost-effective.

Battery technology leads to a second problem, “overweighting”. The batteries are too heavy, which means the aircraft cannot carry the specified payload. Deutsche Bank has consistently raised this issue and believes current technology, along with structural constraints, will prevent the aircraft from meeting the 1,000lb and 4-passenger targets that all manufacturers aim for.

Deutsche believes Joby management has now acknowledged that their 1,000 payload is “too aggressive”. In February, Deutsche said they did not believe current battery technology could support Vertical’s 6-passenger concept under real-world conditions and noted that flight testing at Vertical's UK-based test site was severely delayed by the weather.

The consensus appears to be that the aircraft cannot meet specifications due to the batteries, and that the batteries will make near-term operations uneconomic.

## Timelines are extending

In 2022, JOBY was guiding to certification in 2024, but by 2023, that had slipped to 2025. In 2024, it slipped further to 2026. Management now targets the beginning of FAA test flights in 2026, and the market consensus is that certification will come in 2027 at the earliest.

Archer has seen an almost identical drift; in 2023, they were confident the aircraft would be certified in late 2024. Today, they have submitted around a quarter of their certification documents and no longer provide a certification target date.

Verticals timeline has slipped from 2024, to 2026, to 2028, and in its latest document suggests commercialization in 2030.

## The Trough of Disillusionment

I believe we are now firmly in the trough. The battery issues are leading to a death by a thousand cuts, as manufacturers are forced to save weight to carry the payload. They will try to shave every gram they can, but it may not be enough; the current aircraft will likely have to lose a seat, which makes commercial operations very difficult. Compounding this with the economics of the battery life intensifies this concern, and the production targets make profitability seem impossible.

Archer, in my view, has been overpromising since day 1, and I place little trust in what they say. Even if we accept their figures, these four companies will average 3 aircraft a month each in 2028. With an average selling point of $5 million, that gives them around $180 million in revenue for the year, which is less than the quarterly burn for some of them.

The situation for the operators is even worse by 2029, if they meet all targets, Joby could be operating a fleet of 60 airtaxis. That’s more like a small local taxi firm than a global aviation company

On the positive side, the US government has worked hard to help these companies out. An executive order, the eIPP, provides a significant shortcut, allowing them to fly pre-certified in real-world environments, and they will be allowed to charge for these operations (cargo, medical transport). There will be 8 pilot projects starting in the summer of 2026.

It is data for flights. The government allows them to fly in exchange for complete black box data, which they will then use to write the final safety rules. The final safety rules are an issue- how can these companies build the final aircraft and operations when they don’t yet have the safety rules, and how will the FAA possibly be able to certify them for operations?

The US Air Force is also moving, setting its own military airworthiness rules which allow Joby and Blade to fly today, and the income helps keep these companies operating.

There is great concern from the insiders I have spoken to in the defence industry. I recently spoke to several people about missile products and asked for their views on Quantum computing and eVTOLs, as well as the combat readiness of these aircraft. The weight issues mean lightweight materials have to be used to produce them, so they cannot even resist small-arms fire, carry any defensive weapons, or carry anti-drone equipment. Hard to see how they can be combat useful.

(The research on missiles identified a target company that I will invest in as soon as market conditions allow)

## In Conclusion

Airtaxis seem to be where robotaxis were three years ago, a fantastic idea with a generally accepted business plan, but without the technical or legal framework to turn that business plan into a profitable operation. 2026 seems to be the inflection point for Robotaxis, and I am heavily invested, expecting to ride the slope of enlightenment to a big win over the next couple of years.

Air taxis seem to be in the trough, the US government is helping but it is very difficult to see how this can translate into profitable operations.

If the battery issues are resolved and certification arrives, profitable operations will face major hurdles, such as the number of pilots required, charging infrastructure, and the almost complete absence of vertiports.

I am going to withdraw from this industry for the time being. I still think it is going to be huge and some of these companies will be multi-baggers, but I don’t think now is the time to have my money tied up in them.

I will keep watching and will look to buy back into the leaders when I can see a business on the short-term horizon.
