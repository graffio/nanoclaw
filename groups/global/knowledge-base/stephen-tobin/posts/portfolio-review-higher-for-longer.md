# Portfolio Review: Higher for Longer?

*The Oil price spike will have consequences, reviewing the possibilities and making decisions*

I trade in a high-risk part of the market, emerging small-cap technology stocks. The portfolio suffers in a risk-off environment, and the attractiveness of some of the companies and technologies I invest in changes over time. At the time of writing, the portfolio is down 3.5% month-to-date. It is not a catastrophic loss, but I am reviewing the portfolio to ensure it does not become one.

**Oil and Politics**

Markets are moving on every small development in the Middle East, a comment by the President or a rumour that talks are taking place causes an overreaction. As always, I try to look through the noise to make a sensible decision and not panic.

The attack on Iran by the United States and Israel might have a profound and potentially long-lasting effect on oil prices. The Strait of Hormuz remains closed, and no countries have responded positively to the US request to send warships to secure safe passage for tankers and other merchant ships.

I did not expect this situation to arise; it is a typical “black swan” event. Limited military action was always a possibility, but talks seemed to be progressing well, and President Trump had run on a “no foreign wars” ticket, so I had assigned zero probability to this full-scale attack, which seems to be escalating almost daily. Boots on the ground seemed impossible, but I am now assigning a percentage to it.

Countries in the Gulf are trying to find ways to get exports flowing. Saudi Arabia uses the East-West pipeline, which can handle 7 million barrels a day, and the UAE uses the ADCOP pipeline, which can handle 1.8 million barrels a day. Iraq has just announced a deal to export through Turkey.

These actions will help, but they will not and cannot meet global demand. I have no idea how long the Strait will remain closed, but even when it opens, there will be shortages; it will take time to rebuild infrastructure, and many wells have been closed. Reopening a well takes time, and it does not always return to the pressure it had before the well was closed.

I think it is increasingly likely that oil prices will remain high for longer, and there is non zero probability of physical shortages.

It is time to look at the portfolio with a different eye, trying to decide which companies are at risk of a substantial and sustained drop in share price in this high-oil-price environment. To do that, I must first examine the potential economic ramifications of higher oil prices.

Oil price shocks don’t happen often, and I was not trading in the 1970s when oil prices quadrupled, leading to a global recession and stagflation. I was trading during the 1990 Gulf War, when prices spiked above $35, leading to a recession, and in 2008, when prices spiked to $147 just as the housing market was driving a global financial crisis.

## What are the risks

1.  Consumers change habits
    

High oil prices act like a tax; demand for gasoline is inelastic- people tend to buy the same amount regardless of price, meaning they have to cut back on discretionary spending. If prolonged, it can lead to a significant drop in demand for retail, travel, and leisure companies.

Oil is a big part of the cost base for many industries, and companies can either absorb the additional cost by reducing margins or pass the cost on to consumers, exacerbating the drop in demand.

2.  Monetary Policy tightens
    

Central banks typically move to keep a lid on inflation, raising interest rates or at least ending the cycle of rate cuts. The UK has already seen evidence of this with Mortgage rates moving higher this week and cut-price deals being removed from the market. Globally, we can expect higher for longer interest rates.

3.  Equities Sell Off
    

Previous oil price spikes have been accompanied by a notable sell-off in equities (except for energy companies). This often (especially in the US) makes people feel poorer as they see a drop in the value of brokerage accounts even before they actually feel the effect of increased prices. In these situations, people typically rotate out of risk assets (tech and small caps) into perceived safe havens, Gold, Bonds, and safe haven currencies.

4.  Structural Shifts
    

Previous high oil prices have seen improvements in productivity and fuel efficiency that became permanent. In this instance, it might reduce the payback period of green energy installations and perhaps see governments shift emphasis back towards EVs, heat pumps, solar, and wind energy. We may even see a rebirth in the Hydrogen economy; nuclear could also benefit, but that works on longer time frames.

In the longer term, the situation will highlight the need for energy self-sufficiency and the unpredictability of oil. Despite being the cheapest liquid on earth, its price and availability are beyond the control of most nations.

Alternative energy is likely to benefit, and that means electricity production and further electrification of everything.

In this context, I have reviewed the portfolio’s exposure, taking into account the potential short- and medium-term impacts of oil prices.

The portfolio is packed with small-cap tech companies, and its high beta nature means it is at risk of a correction. However, much of the tech is closely aligned with electric transport and lowering industrial costs, those technologies might suffer in the immediate sell off but outperform over the medium term if users transition to electric transport away from ICE vehicles

I have decided several holdings are too exposed and I need to exit; in all cases, I will be booking a loss. I will look to recycle the money into other companies that stand to benefit from the current environment, and the first of those investments will be out shortly.

I will be watching the market for the timing of exiting positions. I will send trade alerts before I exit. This is not a trade alert, just a review of my thinking.

The two areas without any concern are psychedelic drugs and defence. MDA Space is partly a defence company, and it also presents no concerns.

I have very little concern about the autonomous driving companies; they will all reduce costs for adopters, allowing some margin capture for trucking companies and lower costs for taxi users.

eVTOL companies are problematic, EH is targeting tourism, and although they should see a bounce when flights begin, they may find it more difficult to generate tourist business later on. EVTL is two years from operations, so it is a high-risk and needs cash. The two leaders, ACHR and JOBY, might see planned operations in the Middle East delayed to next year due to the war, which will hit the sector.

Recycling is a mixed bag; it reduces costs, increases margins, and improves local availability of critical materials. However, MTM, AREC, and ROOF are still developing the first sites and will need ongoing capital. They may be just too risky in this environment.

The new material companies are also mixed, NanoXplore is dependent on a pickup in auto manufacturing, which might not arrive for some time. ASPI was intending to float a subsidiary to raise cash, which will be more difficult, but is producing critical materials that will remain in a highly price-elastic demand.

AI companies are difficult; they are the definition of Tech, so they could all be tarred with the same brush. However, our holdings are spread across three different sectors. Tempus is unlikely to be affected, and any drawdown is an opportunity. Airship has increased competition, and both of its verticals may slow purchases. Large commercial customers may see a slowdown and look to save money, and the US Government may need to reduce spending on the border wall to cover the costs of the huge sums being spent on the war in Iran. SoundHound is exposed, as some of its biggest customers are in leisure and retail, both verticals that are likely to be hit hard.

In the “others” sector, I have major concerns with Byrna (BYRN). The soft earnings report this quarter may be a sign that consumer spending is already slowing and that their high-priced devices depend solely on discretionary retail spending. Hesai and Minth both supply the auto makers, but so far their home markets in China seem to be holding up well, and both have substantial shares in EVs, which might see a boost.

The companies of major concern are Byrna (BYRN), Quest Resources (QRHC), Airship AI (AISP), and Virgin Galactic (SPCE). The demand for all these stocks may decline, and I will look for opportunities to exit, sending trade alerts before doing so.

In the coming days, I will also send a Buy Alert moving money into Space and Defence, an area unlikely to be affected by the current problems.
