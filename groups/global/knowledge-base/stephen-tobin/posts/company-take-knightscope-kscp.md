# Company Take: Knightscope (KSCP)

*High potential robot security company*

## Robot Companies

I am continuing to work through the list of companies on my Robot watch list. I made one investment this week and want to make at least one more before the industry begins to take off. I explained the rationale behind my search for an [investment in this earlier post](https://stephentobin.substack.com/p/trade-alert-and-robots).

I want to find at least one company concentrated on robot security automation and one focused on Humanoid robot development.

My investment strategy focuses on identifying emerging technologies and then investing in high-potential companies that are poised to capitalize on the growth wave as the technology is adopted.

I believe I have identified my Humanoid Robot company and expect to make a purchase next week, but here I examine the leading candidate for investment in the robot security sector. I have decided not to invest yet.

## Knightscope (KSCP), Market Leader in Security Robots

Knightscope is a leader in AI security. It has Autonomous Security Robots \[ASRs\] designed to patrol on random or pre-determined routes and schedules. The ASRs have more than 2.5 million service hours under their belt and have proven to be reliable wheeled machines. KSCP announced its next-generation K7 ASR earlier this year, still a wheeled robot but it looks more like an SUV than a Dalek from the Dr Who series of the 1980s.

### Traction Developing

Q1 revenue was up 25% year on year to $2.9 million, backlog increased from $1.8m to $2.5m.

KSCP was awarded “authority to operate” by the US federal government and is currently the only security robot company with such authority, which opens up a vast potential market.

KSCP has deployed its first federal government robot to the Department of Veterans Affairs and won a phase 1 contract with the US Air Force. They have established a Washington sales office to drive this business.

The business model involves selling these devices as a service, thereby generating ongoing repeat business. They charge between $1 and $11 an hour for an annual subscription, and when you compare that to the cost of having security guards, the business rationale is clear.

KSCP reports that existing clients have extended their contracts for periods ranging from two to eight years. Earlier this month, KSCP announced $1 million of new orders for 535 ECDs and 7 new ASRs. 13 ASR contracts were renewed. These orders included a K5 robot for a real estate customer who also bought a K1, shown below. The K1 sits on top of a tower or on a wall, it is not a robot really more of an intelligent camera.

Target markets include healthcare, gaming, industrial, real estate, education, transportation, and government. It is a market with an enormous total addressable market (TAM), generating many billions of dollars annually.

In April, KSCP announced $1.2 million of orders for 11 ASRs and 70 ECDs. They also announced a new 33,000 sq ft headquarters in Silicon Valley and the start of recruitment for a second shift at its manufacturing site.

One of the contracts in April was 4 K5 robots to a Fortune 25 company. The ECDs all went to university and college campuses.

May saw $2 million in new contracts signed, including 114 ASR renewals, one of which was a 6-year contract with a casino. Long-term contracts were signed in self-storage, hospitality, and health care. A US airport bought 10 K1 blue light towers (that is, a K1 bundled with an ECD tower).

The remainder of this post examines Knightscope's financials and my trading plan for them. This financial work is funded by the paying subscribers and as a result reserved for them

  

### Financials

This is where the problems arrive. So far, this company is everything I am looking for. Proven technology, a growing order base working in the targeted AI robot security market, and working on private ground where the limitations of a wheeled robot are not relevant. I like the expansion into ECD towers and how that integrates with the existing product lineup.

KSCP has experienced growing revenue and secured long-term contracts in both the government and private sectors. They are forecasting significant growth from late 2025 and into 2026.

The problem is that they lack sufficient funds to make this happen. Despite a large capital raise late last year, which left them with positive net equity of $18 million and debt of $5 million, they do not have enough cash to get through 2025.

Dilution last year was an alarming 225%, the company is 82% owned by the general public, and the insiders own only 3%. The CEO received a total package of $2.2 million and a salary of $ 500,000 last year; that package is 300% higher than the average for companies of this size.

Taking into account the information from management, the orders announced this year, and the information we have about the cost base, I am using the following model of the company for 2025 and 2026.

The model predicts improving sales, declining costs, and a shift to a positive gross margin in Q4 this year. It is a big step forward for the company and I believe it will maintain that momentum into 2026.

The problem is the cash burn; operating income improves from -$29.7 million in 2024 to -$ 19.7 million in 2026, an improvement of $10 million, or 34%.

Unfortunately, this does not improve the balance sheet, and the cash balance will reach zero at the end of Q3 this year and then become negative $20 million by the end of 2026.

Knightscope has a market capitalization of $42 million, so raising $20 million is not an insurmountable challenge. Still, at the moment I can not forecast a breakeven date, and with a cash raise of $20 million, so 50% dilution, the cash will have been spent by the end of 2026, and another, probably larger, cash raise will be needed.

Although they are operating in a fast-growing market, revenue fell in FY 2024, and the hiring of a new shift implies that the company was running at maximum single-shift capacity to deliver a 2024 revenue of $10.8 million.

The sales cycle for this new technology is lengthy, and the subscription model, although ideal for generating long-term recurring revenue, results in slow revenue growth.

This weekly price chart shows the price collapse since launch, they have turned $100 shares into $5 shares, which is unfortunately typical for these early-stage companies.

## Conclusion

I am adding Knightscope to the eyes on list. I like the products and the strategy, but I need to see some proof that they can achieve profitability. That means fresh capital and increased sales. I don't want to be the fresh capital, and I definitely do not want to suffer a 95% loss, the way early investors did. As always, I am unhappy about companies raising money to pay their CEOs such large salaries that are out of step with the business's performance.

I will review them again in Q4 this year, when we will have more evidence of revenue growth and will be able to assess how they are managing the cash situation.
