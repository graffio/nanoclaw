# Trade Alert : A Re-Investment Thesis in a Hot Sector

*The Nature of Active Management*

I describe my investing as “Active Management”. It doesn’t suit everyone; most people prefer a buy-and-hold or technical setup strategy. Mine comes somewhere between the two.

The strategy was designed to take advantage of companies with emerging technology, and it does it very successfully.

I trade in high-risk assets, companies whose value cannot be justified by their current or past performance. They often have billion-dollar market caps with zero revenue. Few, if any, are profitable, and their value lies in the future.

These companies are prone to large moves on market sentiment. If the market moves to a risk-off footing, the indices might drop 2 or 3% but these emerging technology companies can easily drop 50% or more. Often, they rise and fall on single press releases or influencer coverage rather than the fundamentals of the business.

It means I often take profits early the share price can move vertically higher without any changes to the business, if the share price jumps higher without any news I take the win with the intention of buying again when the price returns to normality.

We have had some great examples of this recently, with multiple trades returning three-figure percentage returns. Still, perhaps the ABAT trade from last month shown below, a vertical move getting me to take profits. ABAT might be 10 years away from profits, but it has outstanding emerging technology, and when that technology becomes hot, the price will fly.

I follow a similar strategy when prices fall. At -30%, I start to reassess; it could be years before a company makes a profit, and if the markets turn lower, I could see an 80 or 90% drop in some of them. In fact, when I was building this strategy, I regularly did.

I always exit with the intention to buy again, preferring a small early loss to a large drawdown that could last a protracted period. I go over my fundamental analysis again and again to make sure I have not made an error, but I will buy when the market conditions that caused my exit have passed.

Today I will buy a company I exited last month, it was at the 30% decision mark, and the market was clearly moving to risk off. I was already in “move to cash” mode, and this company seemed likely to suffer greatly.

The company has a large market cap and virtually no revenue; it has excellent technology, but is still pre-commercialisation. It is almost the definition of an emerging technology company; it has first-mover advantages but is still building out its hardware/software stack.

As it turns out, it only dropped another 12%. It reported excellent earnings that validate my initial thesis, advancing its commercialization timeline and providing additional proof points for the value of its business.

The price went down, but the fundamentals improved. Today I will buy again and expect this investment to return a three-figure percentage gain over the next two years.

Active management is not to everyone’s taste, but it does preserve capital and has delivered outsized returns and continues to do so.

Key points that helped me decide to re-buy, not in any particular order of importance, as it is the combination of all of these that has led to my decision to re-invest.

1.  Extended routes
    

The Westbound route from Fort Worth to El Paso is now open, well ahead of schedule, and the Eastbound route will open in January. This was an essential route for Hirschbach, a key early customer of AUR. The route is 600 miles long and has been very difficult to staff and almost impossible for a human driver to complete in a single day (due to legal restrictions). The average truck has a 1,200-mile range on a tank of gas, so the Aurora can make the trip nonstop.

Phoenix will be added in Q1 2026, another 400 miles, and making a continuous route from Houston to Fort Worth to El Paso to Phoenix.

These long routes will double the revenue per truck and increase profits per truck multiple-fold.

2.  Pick up points
    

Currently, AUR offers a Transport-as-a-Service model. They drive freight between their hubs and customers have to drive the first miles and last miles. Soon (2027), they will move to a Driver as a Service model where customers use the system to go from their own delivery yards. In the Q&A section, the issue of customer pick up points was discussed. The CFO said, “ _One of them is on the misconception piece, right? Like if you think about endpoints, customer endpoints, roughly 80% of those endpoints are within a 5-mile range from a highway. So this is not a difficult technical challenge at all.”_

The CEO said “_So we have a system that’s capable and able to deliver this, like it works well. So for us, it’s just a matter of timing and sequencing when the volumes are sufficient that it’s relevant for our customers. And so we intend to be rolling that out to customers through the next year._

3.  Improved Technology
    

The second generation of the hardware is in testing, it reduces costs by 50% and provides much longer range. 1,000 meter perception using radar and LiDAR. Fabrinet will be making this version and B samples have been sent out.

Generation 3, which AUMOVIO will manufacture, recently spun out of Continental, will go into production in 2027. Gen 3 will be the scaled production model produced in the thousands, AUMOVIO has increased its floor space and number of production lines for this equipment and it will be based on NVIDIA Thor chips.

Gen 3 is designed to last for 1 million miles a significant advantage for operators.

4.  Improved driving times
    

In the last 5 weeks, Aurora has driven 50,000 miles, doubling its total so far. They generated over $1million in revenue (Q3) from its key customers Uber Freight, FedEx, Hirschbach, and Werner. The highest mileage truck has now logged 18,000 miles in a single month, which is double the industry standard.

5.  The monetization
    

AUR gave a case study of a single truck for a single week on its Fort Worth to Phoenix route. It provides a compelling case for trucking businesses.

AUR delivered $1 million in revenue with five trucks, they will have 10 on the road this quarter and expect to be in the hundreds of trucks by end of 2026. They also guided to positive gross margins from that point.

6.  New Customers
    

The new route led to two new contracts for fully driverless operations. All of these contracts are with major customers, as Aurora is not yet accepting business from smaller operators.

7.  New Trucks
    

AUR has two OEMs who will manufacture trucks using the Gen 3 system from 2027. AUR has been buying trucks from International for its Gen 2 system and will have 10 operational this quarter. Using International is interesting as International (formerly Navistar) have already signed with another autonomous trucking company PlusAI. International is owned by the Traton group who also have the MAN and Scania brands. They are using PlusAIs PlusDrive system for driver assist and have been involved in the development of PlusAIs autonomous system. The autonomous system is not on the road yet in any form so it will have several years of testing before it is available for sale. PlusDrive has been successful, Amazon bought 1,000 units and PlusAI reported more than 7,000 orders. Fitting the AUR system to International trucks proves what AUR have been saying all along that their system will work with all trucks and end users can choose it if they wish. They likely have a two to three year first mover advantage and that may be long enough to establish them as the de facto standard.

8.  Forward guidance.
    

AUR published its road map for 2026 and 2027

They are guiding to positive gross profit by the end of 2026 and positive income by the end of 2027. They have the move to customer-owned trucks starting in 2026 and being completed by 2027.

The forward guidance suggests that in the next two years, they will be at scale with profitable operations. A proven use case over thousands of miles of highway and owner-operated trucks operating 24 hours a day in all weather conditions. This will all be before any of the competition has approval to operate.

The ramp up is fast 1 truck 2024, 10 in 2025, hundreds in 2026, thousands in 2027. It is important to note that Aurora generally under-promises and over-delivers; they meet targets ahead of schedule and tend not to report or discuss what they hope, only what they know.

## Don’t forget the risk.

All of this is very positive **but it has not happened yet**, at the moment they are an $7 billion dollar company with almost no revenue, a handful of customers operating a business model that has never been tried before dependent on a technology that no one has yet got to work. Control position size and be prepared for both ups and downs.

It could be a ten-bagger, but it could easily come off the rails, a single crash, authorisation withheld, or a customer coming out with a better solution. The risks are real.
