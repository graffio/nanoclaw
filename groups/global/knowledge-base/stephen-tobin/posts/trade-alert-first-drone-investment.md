**Large Defense Prime Competition**

The interest of defence primes is driven by changing battlefield dynamics. Drone warfare has escalated due to low cost, technological advancements, and battlefield successes, notably in Ukraine.

Putin stated Russia received 140,000 drones in 2023, and was aiming for a 10x increase in 2024. Ukraine procured over 1.5m in 2024, and is expecting more in 2025 (4.5m FPV drone capacity).

Drone defense systems are categorized into 'soft kill' and 'hard kill' methods. 'Soft kill' systems disrupt drone control via jamming or spoofing, which is effective over shorter distances (<1 km) with minimal collateral damage; however, their effectiveness is limited against pre-programmed or fiber-optic-controlled drones. 'Hard kill' systems physically capture or destroy drones, using ballistic, laser, or microwave technologies. They are effective over longer distances (>1 km) but pose a greater risk of collateral damage. The cost-effectiveness of engagement is a crucial consideration in drone warfare.

**Drone Shield Integration**

Large defence primes are integrating DroneShields technology, particularly its detection and jamming devices, into their kinetic take-down systems. Lockheed Martin (Press release, Nov 2023, IR website) was the first to do this. Management have reported that this is now a common occurrence in the Ukraine active war zone. Effectiveness and costs are the key drivers of this integration. After buying the hardware, each individual engagement with a Drone Shield system has zero cost.
