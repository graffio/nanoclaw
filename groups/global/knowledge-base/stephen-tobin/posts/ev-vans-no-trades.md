# EV Vans: No Trades

*What you dont buy matters*

Earlier in the week, I published a sector review of the Electric Van market. The review was triggered by substantial price movement in the shares. We already own shares in BlueBird (BLBD), which is attempting to establish a foothold in the sector, but I began the review with the hope of finding a suitable investment that could yield high returns.

In this instance, I have decided not to invest. The top-line review revealed two prospective targets, XOS and REE. Neither is the kind of risk/reward equation I am looking for.

The large returns we generate come partly from the companies we buy but mainly from the companies we don’t. I am a small-cap investor; most of them fail, and avoiding those catastrophic losses is the most important part of the job.

## REE

It is not a company I would consider investing in for numerous reasons.

1.  Business Model Collapse
    

REE had been expected to begin volume production of its P7 vehicle this year and had stated that it had received large-scale orders from more than 30 companies. The P7 is an innovative vehicle, featuring a skateboard design with four “REE Corners” wheels and attached engines that deliver 4WD and 4-wheel steer. It is the only vehicle authorized to use drive-by-wire technology in the US, and its performance, on paper, appears to be significantly better than that of the competition. With production gearing up, large-scale orders in hand, and sufficient cash, they seemed to be on the verge of becoming a substantial niche manufacturer.

That plan is in tatters, REE has announced a pause in production! They are not going to build any P7 vehicles. Management claims this is due to U.S. tariffs, but it is very difficult to believe that. I think there are two possibilities: either the P7 doesn't work or it can't be built at a reasonable price point.

REE is letting go of the majority of its R&D and manufacturing workforce as it seeks to reduce costs, and with that workforce goes the future of the P7. It is unlikely that REE will ever manufacture a vehicle.

2.  New Business Model
    

REE will focus on selling its Software Defined Vehicle software and commercializing its other software products. There are no demonstrated customer wins for the SDV platform, although they do have an MOU. REE are no doubt searching for a partner to manufacture the product or buy the software, I cannot see it happening.

3.  Management
    

Always a key part of my research, and this is not one I would invest in. I find that many of the CEOs of these companies are serial entrepreneurs who jump from one idea to another, running multiple companies simultaneously and taking large salaries from each.

Their goal is to make money for themselves, not build a company, and they usually do it at the expense of retail shareholders like you and me.

The CEO of REE, Daniel Barel, exhibits several red flags. He founded REE and has been its CEO for 13 years. He owns 0.003% of REE, meaning he sold his shares to unsuspecting members of the public long ago. Well before the share price collapse after the production pause announcement.

Whilst working full-time at REE, he founded SpecterX and works there full-time, and also founded SoftWheel, where he works full-time. From 2007 to 2019, he was CEO (full-time) at Cautes in Hong Kong and founded Wooof in 2012, working full-time until 2018.

While being CEO at REE, he held four other full-time positions, collecting a substantial salary at each, while diluting REE shareholders by 160% in the last 12 months and destroying shareholder value. REE has seen its share price collapse from $12 to $0.62 in 2025, that is a 95% drop. Mr. Barel had already sold his shares, so he was unaffected and still received his salary. He has been very successful while taking his shareholders for fools; no wonder Wall Street is not invested.

### Conclusion

I will never invest in REE, it is off the list for good. It will do well to avoid bankruptcy from here, but its CEO has made a fortune. You have to watch out for guys like this; they spin a good yarn and pick neat technologies with a believable business model. The problem is motivation; they are in it for personal gain and are happy to do it at the expense of others.

A good CEO is in it for personal gain, but will not do it at the expense of his investors. Often, they seek the prestige of being a successful CEO more than the money.

## XOS

There are no issues with management or business models here; they are trying to build a solid company and putting real effort into controlling costs and developing commercial traction. Unfortunately, it is not going well.

A big miss on Q1 2025 earnings, caused by a timing issue as it had delivered the trucks but was unable to recognize the revenue. However, even allowing for the timing issue, commercial traction is too slow.

They do not have enough cash (only $5 m left) to cover the year and positive free cash flow still looks a couple of years out.

Xos exits Q1 2025 with improving gross margins but softer top-line and unit momentum, focused on working-capital discipline as it targets a return to delivery growth and narrower operating losses.

-   Deliveries peaked in 3Q24 and troughed in 4Q24, falling further in Q1 2025 as Xos re-balanced toward higher-margin Hubs and chassis.
    
-   Management expects shipments to re-accelerate in 2H25, supported by UPS (200 chassis) and Caltrans (20 Hubs) awards.
    

Management is guiding to 370 unit deliveries (Mid Point) in 2025 up from 297 in 2024. In Q1 2025 they delivered 29 but shipped 60. Looking at the cadence of deliveries it is difficult to see the company hitting its mid point of guidance for units.

## Opportunities

The UPS order underpins guidance, but we have already seen some delays

The first shipments of the XOS powertrain have been made to Bluebird. It is a higher-margin product. BLBD intends to make Stepvans with it using their chassis and the same upfitter that works on the UPS XOS machine.

CalTrans, California Department of Transportation is an essential customer of the XOS hub (a mobile charging station) and the state is expected to purchase more XOS vans. In the current political environment, these must be riskier than they were.

  
XOS has released a new powertrain that utilizes 90% of the same parts but supports larger trucks. However, this is not expected to go into production until H2 2026.

Xos management has alluded to tariff pressures and concerns; we will still have to wait to see how these play out.

### Cash

Xos has $4.7 million of cash, roughly 1 quarter’s cash runway. They have a convertible due in August, which they should be able to roll over; however, even so, I think they will need an additional $15 million in cash this year. That represents a very large amount of dilution. Management believes they can cover the cash burn by releasing money from inventory. I have doubts that this can be done and expect dilution to approach 75% this year.

## Conclusion

XOS is not ready for our investment, but I will continue to monitor it. Currently, it appears that long-suffering investors will face further dilution due to the ongoing timeline slip.
