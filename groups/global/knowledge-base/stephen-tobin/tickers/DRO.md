## Company Overview

DroneShield is an Australian company and the world's only publicly listed pure-play counter-unmanned-aerial-systems (C-UAS) specialist. It offers a vertically integrated hardware-software-AI stack for detecting, tracking, and defeating unauthorized drones across military, government, and civilian applications.

**Products:**

- DroneGun — handheld defeat system using smart jamming
- RfPatrol — body-worn RF detection device
- DroneSentry — fixed/on-the-move integrated detection-and-defeat system
- DroneSentry-C2 — command and control software powered by SensorFusionAI, integrating third-party radar, LiDAR, cameras, and ultrasound
- Sentry S — subscription-based lower-budget civilian solution (e.g. prisons)
- RFAI-2 — proprietary AI trained on 5 years of data from 1,600+ fielded devices, updated quarterly

**Technology position:** DroneShield's core competitive advantage is its AI-powered detection and "smart jamming" defeat technology. Smart jamming is the core defeat technology due to its cost-effectiveness, ability to counter large swarms, and lack of collateral impact. Each individual engagement after hardware purchase has zero cost. The company integrates radar, LiDAR, cameras, and ultrasound into its RF detection hardware, with constantly updating AI software increasing the number of drones it can detect.

**Revenue model:** Hardware sales + growing SaaS subscriptions. SaaS revenue doubled to \$1.3M in H1 2024, hit \$1.7M in Q1 2025 (+102% QoQ). Gross margins of 65-75%, significantly exceeding typical defence contractors.

- FY2024 revenue: A\$57.5M (record)
- FY2025 revenue: A\$216.5M (+276% YoY); underlying PBT A\$33.3M (15% margin); profit after tax A\$3.5M (+367%)
- FY2025 YTD through Dec: A\$165.2M (4.3x YoY growth)
- A\$49.6M single European contract in Dec 2025 (nearly equal to entire FY2024 revenue)
- Sales pipeline: A\$2.55B (Dec 2025), A\$2.3B committed (2026)
- SaaS revenue +475% in FY2025
- Total addressable market: US\$60B (CEO estimate; Stephen initially used US\$10B)

**Balance sheet:** A\$235M equity raise providing A\$221M liquidity with zero debt. \$311M shareholder equity. Cash runway of decades at current burn rate.

**Geographic diversification:** Europe (32%), Asia (23%), US (22%), Middle East (16%), UK (10%). Transformation from 90% domestic in 2023 to globally diversified. Active in approximately 50 countries.

**Key relationships:** Raytheon is a customer (DroneShield technology inside Raytheon's counter-drone systems). Lockheed Martin integration (Nov 2023). Defence primes are generally customers rather than competitors — their focus on expensive systems and slower innovation cycles does not align with the cost-symmetric, rapidly evolving nature of counter-drone technology.

**Manufacturing:** Low-capex model using contract manufacturers for assembly. Manufacturing capacity being expanded from A\$500M to A\$2.4B by end of 2026, with new facilities in Europe (Q1 2026) and US (mid-2026) alongside expanded Australian operations. Targeting 600 staff by end 2026.

## Competitive Position

Stephen's view: DroneShield is the only pure-play public C-UAS company globally, with a vertically integrated hardware-software-AI stack that differentiates it from diversified defence primes and niche competitors.

**vs Defence Primes (Raytheon, Lockheed, Anduril):** CEO Oleg Vornik was "extraordinarily dismissive" of competition from primes. Defence primes are customers, not competitors. Raytheon's counter-drone system has DroneShield inside. Anduril focuses mainly on enterprise command and control, not the detection/defeat layer.

**vs EOS (ASX:EOS):** Complementary rather than competitive. DroneShield leads in "soft kill" (jamming/defeat) while EOS leads in "hard kill" (cannons, missiles, lasers). Stephen views counter-drone as requiring a multi-layered solution of both. "You may see a DroneShield installation on top of a football stadium, but a nuclear power plant will need an EOS system." DRO and EOS prices are highly correlated. EOS recently bought a UK company building interceptor drones.

**vs Ondas (ONDS):** Ondas captures drones physically with nets. The Ondas system is "particularly effective against autonomous drones that cannot be disrupted by the DroneShield product." Complementary technology.

**vs Dedrone (AXON), D-Fend Solutions, Fortem, Zen Technologies:** Smaller competitors focused on specific geographic regions or niche applications. DroneShield has achieved superior global diversification.

**vs Ukrainian companies:** May compete at the cheaper end of products, but unlikely to have the technology to integrate with large-scale NATO systems the way EOS and DroneShield can. "The one thing the Ukraine companies might have is ready supply."

**CEO/management risk:** The single biggest risk factor. CEO Oleg Vornik, chairman, and a third director dumped their entire shareholdings (combined AUD\$70M) in November 2025, causing a catastrophic share price collapse of nearly 50%. This was compounded by a misreported \$7.6M US government contract (already announced). The US CEO resigned. Bell Potter (main broker) dropped coverage. Stephen: "I don't think we have ever seen them lie. The CEO never said he wouldn't sell all his shares — I just didn't think to ask him." New executive shareholding policy announced Dec 2025 to rebuild credibility.
