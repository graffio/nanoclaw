# Nuclear Energy: The Next Frontier

We may be entering the new nuclear era. The world is searching for a clean energy source, and many countries, those without fossil fuel reserves, are searching for energy independence. Clean energy offers benefits but has limitations in many areas. Solar requires sun and land, which not everywhere has, and battery storage is, at best, a sticking plaster.

The world needs low-cost, clean, reliable base-load energy. Electricity usage will likely increase significantly in the coming years as data centers, AI, and blockchain technology grow in popularity. Transport and materials handling are moving towards electricity. Nuclear seems like a good solution, and it may well be the only solution.

This is the background to the nuclear trade I mentioned recently. I still hope to take the trade soon, I am perhaps being overly cautious.

## Uranium Enrichment

The uranium enrichment market, a critical stage in the nuclear fuel cycle, is characterized by a concentrated competitive landscape dominated by a few large, often state-backed, multinational corporations. These established players possess significant capacity and long-standing contracts with nuclear utilities globally. However, emerging trends, particularly the growing demand for High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium (HALEU) to fuel advanced reactors, and the development of new enrichment technologies, are creating opportunities for smaller, more specialized companies to enter the market.

Major Competitors

A small number of key players with significant market share and extensive operational capabilities dominate the global uranium enrichment market:

-   Rosatom (Russia): A state-owned corporation, Rosatom is the leading global player in uranium enrichment, possessing a substantial share of the world's enrichment capacity using gas centrifuge technology.
    
-   Urenco (UK, Germany, Netherlands): This multinational consortium operates enrichment facilities in Europe and the United States, utilizing gas centrifuge technology.
    
-   Orano (France): A global nuclear fuel cycle company, Orano has a strong presence in uranium enrichment, employing gas centrifuge technology.
    
-   China National Nuclear Corporation (CNNC) (China): CNNC is a state-owned enterprise that provides nuclear fuel for China's expanding nuclear power sector, using gas centrifuge technology.
    
-   Kazatomprom (Kazakhstan): While primarily a uranium mining company, Kazatomprom also participates in enrichment activities through collaborative ventures.
    

These major competitors have established a strong foothold in the market due to:

-   Large-scale production capacities
    
-   Advanced gas centrifuge technology
    
-   Long-term contracts with utility providers
    
-   Stringent safety and regulatory compliance
    

Smaller and Emerging Competitors

While the market is concentrated, several smaller companies are emerging, often focusing on niche segments or developing innovative technologies:

-   Centrus Energy Corp. (USA): Centrus Energy is a U.S.-based supplier of enriched uranium fuel, utilizing pilot-scale gas centrifuge technology. It is also involved in the production of HALEU.
    
-   NANO Nuclear Energy Inc. (USA): This company is focused on developing advanced nuclear reactor technology, including microreactors, and is working towards HALEU fuel production.
    
-   Global Laser Enrichment LLC (GLE) (USA/Australia): A joint venture between Silex Systems and Cameco, GLE is developing the SILEX laser enrichment technology, which has the potential to reduce costs and increase efficiency significantly.
    
-   Laser Isotope Separation Technologies Inc. (USA) & General Matter Inc. (USA): The U.S. Department of Energy has selected these companies to potentially supply low-enriched uranium, suggesting the development of new enrichment capabilities.
    
-   ASP Isotopes. (SA): Focused on developing aerodynamic separation and quantum enrichment, a form of laser enrichment to enrich medical isotopes but is attempting to port the technology into Uranium enrichment.
    

## Market Dynamics and Trends

Several key trends are shaping the competitive landscape of the uranium enrichment market:

-   HALEU Demand: The increasing development of advanced reactors, including Small Modular Reactors (SMRs), is driving demand for HALEU fuel, creating opportunities for companies specializing in its production.
    
-   Technological Advancements: The development of more efficient enrichment technologies, such as laser enrichment, could disrupt the market and enable new competitors to emerge.
    
-   Energy Security and Geopolitical Factors: Growing concerns about energy security and geopolitical instability influence government policies and investments in domestic uranium enrichment capabilities.

# Enrichment

Natural uranium is composed of two isotopes, it is 99.3% U-238 and 0.7% U-235. U-235 is needed for nuclear fuel. Enriching means increasing the percentage of U-235 in the uranium mixture by removing some U-238. This is done by exploiting the different characteristics of the two isotopes.

The dominant method, used by all of the major players, including Centrus Energy, exploits the difference in weight between the two isotopes. Uranium gas is placed in a spinning centrifuge, and the heavier U-238 (heavier than U-235 because it has three extra neutrons) moves towards the edge of the centrifuge, where it can be removed. The lighter U-235 stays closer to the center.

Centrifuge technology is extraordinarily capital-intensive. It requires so much money that only governments can really build it. Centrus estimates the cost of building a commercial-scale centrifuge cascade at tens of billions of dollars and will be dependent on the US government to provide that funding.

The cost of fuel enrichment accounts for 30% of the total cost of nuclear fuel and 5% of the total electricity generating costs.

This is an industry ripe for disruption. New technology offers the possibility of low-capex, low-cost enrichment. Recent improvements in laser technology have made this possible, exploiting different properties of the isotopes rather than their weight.
