### Background

Ondas is evolving from a niche wireless‐network vendor into a defense and public-safety drone company. 2024–25 orders for its **Optimus autonomous drone** and **Iron Drone Raider counter-UAS** platforms have accelerated, lifting backlog well above near-term revenue forecasts and validating the product‐market fit. While execution risk and persistent cash burn remain material, Ondas is one of the few listed ways to gain exposure to rapidly expanding counter-drone and autonomous-surveillance markets.

These markets are forecast to see multi-year growth with CAGRs over 20% out to 2035.

### The Business Plan

Ondas has two divisions, each with its own CEO (both newly installed), and each division has proprietary technology that would be worthy of an investment. The Drone segment seems to be going through near exponential growth at the moment, and management is intent on scaling up this division quickly.

They have suggested a 2026 Revenue of $100 million, but that is dependent on acquisition activity, and they are negotiating four potential deals.

The other division has proprietary Dot 16 technology that delivers WiMax (long-range WiFi) and has recently been approved for use in the US. They have partnered with Siemens to deliver this product, and it has real, if slightly longer-term potential.

I reviewed Ondas for investment earlier this year. I deemed the network product too far from commercialization to warrant investment, and the counter-drone ability was not proven.

The counter drones are now battle-proven, sales are exploding higher with repeat business, making it worthy of an investment. The network product continues to make progress and with a recent installation in Chicago may be on the verge of breaking out into the wider US train network.

## Ondas Drones

Ondas has two systems, they share one key feature: **complete autonomous operations.** The Drones fly from a box that contains multiple drones and a robotic arm capable of changing batteries and payloads, they use AI to decide when to fly and work out their mission in flight making their own choices about how to complete the mission.

Ondas has partnered with Palantir to deliver this AI and with Mistral to deliver the robotics.

Ondas has multiple systems operational, and the feedback from military operations is good; they do have wartime experience and have performed admirably in two combat zones.

The lack of a proof of concept in the system was the main reason I bought DroneShield and not Ondas earlier this year. With clear proof of concept, Ondas now seems the better bet.

### July Investors Day

Ondas held an investors day on July 9th, where it presented its longer-term strategic priorities. The company reported revenue of $6 million for Q2 (above my forecast of $5.5). More impressively, they announced orders received of $39 million and have a backlog of $23 million. Ondas re-stated its FY guidance of $25 million.

Management said the two drone platforms were seeing broader adoption following the success of initial installations and that the company has added production capabilities to meet what they predict to be rapidly rising demand.

Both drone platforms are dual use (military and civilian).

**Iron Drone:** The Iron Drone is marketed to government and enterprise clients for security and critical infrastructure protection, safeguarding assets and people from hostile drones. Its drones are called Raiders and Raider deployments are ideal for sensitive locations such as borders, stadiums, schools, power plants, military bases, amusement parks, and public event venues.

The Raider system autonomously detects, tracks, and intercepts unauthorized or hostile unmanned aircraft. It is typically sold with multiple small UAVs housed in a docking station. Each Raider UAV features live video and IR capabilities, along with a “net” payload designed to intercept hostile drones. Upon detecting an unauthorized drone, one or more Raider UAVs can be autonomously deployed at high speeds to track the aircraft. If deemed hostile, the Raider UAV deploys its netting to physically intercept the drone. A parachute integrated with the netting ensures a safe descent for collection. I was sceptical about the ability of this system but it has proven to be effective in multiple war zones, it can capture multiple drone types. The nets deploy at speed and wrap around the propellers of much larger drones as well as small commercial ones. A clear development line was laid out in July with Iron Drone being developed to provide protection against new aerial threats and ground defence use cases.

The Iron Drone counter-UAS system addresses a critical vulnerability in homeland security and military operations: the rapid proliferation of UAV technology in lower airspace.

A comprehensive multi-layered solution is required, incorporating radar, command and control, and jamming capabilities. Iron Drone complements these defenses with its drone capture capability. The Ondas system is particularly effective against autonomous drones that cannot be disrupted by the DroneShield product.

**Optimus:** The Optimus is an autonomous "drone in a box" solution designed for on-demand aerial access in challenging environments. It boasts a 40-minute flight time and a 10-mile range. The drone can carry up to nine swappable payloads or sensors and utilizes up to eleven swappable batteries. A robotic arm facilitates autonomous payload and battery swaps, maximizing flight duration.

The platform has received a Type certificate from the FAA, confirming its airworthiness and adherence to safe operating standards. A new executive order will enable the FAA to establish clear metrics for assessing the performance and safety of Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS) operations, with recommendations for broader adoption. This development is expected to drive increased demand for systems like Optimus from public safety organizations and corporations, leading to wider deployments.

Ondas management also said it is working on first responder systems for public safety, tailoring the drone payload based on customer requirements.

## Acquisitions

Ondas management has proven its ability to scale and commercialize drone technology. They see this as a core competency for the company, Ondas intends to leverage this ability by acquiring companies with adjacent technologies that are not able to scale at the pace required by the market. The CEO said they had looked at more than 300 companies that met its criteria of existing early revenue and strong differentiated profit offering. The company engaged with 25 of the companies and has moved to formal negotiations with 4 of them.

These will be “tuck in” acquisitions, adding additional technology to the existing offerings.

## Finances

Ondas has cleaned its balance sheet, which was a real concern, reducing its debt to $5 million (all convertible). It ended June with $70 million in cash following its recent $46 million equity raise. Likely the convertible will result in some dilution (perhaps 4 million shares).

The balance sheet does provide the funds needed to pursue the management's growth plan. However, we should expect more dilution to fund acquisitions.

2024 revenue was $7 million and looks set for significant gains, orders already booked put the increase at several hundred percentage points, and momentum is growing.

Organic growth (no acquisitions) suggests 2025 revenue of $25 million, 2026 revenue of $40 million and profitability from Q3 2026 onwards. Growth should continue towards $150 million by 2030 and deliver $40 million in EBITDA.

Management said, at the investors' day, they were targeting revenue (including acquisitions) with a $100 million run rate in 2026 that represents a rapid growth rate well exceeding my initial forecasts.

The one hundred million run rate suggests at least $25 million in Q4 2026 (against my non-acquisition forecast of $9.5 million), as they are in advanced negotiations with the target companies. We would expect this figure to be fairly accurate, and management has been pretty good with revenue guidance in the past.

I will have to put the acquisitions to one side. I have no idea of the target companies or their products. All we know is they are adjacent technologies to the drone offering and have already developed initial sales with a strong IP-protected technology.

However, with management guiding to revenue 400% larger than my predictions, there is little point in presenting those predictions.

## Key Drivers of the Drone Segment

I believe the demand for the Ondas drone system is reaching an inflection point. Global governments are showing increased interest and investment in small Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs) due to their demonstrated effectiveness in military applications. This rising demand and awareness are further amplified by the growing need for counter-UAV devices as drone activity and autonomy escalate in active military campaigns.

Defense budgets worldwide are projected to increase significantly, with drone technology expected to capture a growing share as countries shift spending away from expensive traditional hardware like tanks and jets. Notably, NATO nations have agreed to raise their defense spending to 5% of GDP by 2030, a move that will see allied defense spending surge from $440 billion in 2025 to $1.2 trillion in 2030, representing a 2.5x increase.

In the US, there's a strong drive to establish a domestic drone manufacturing base. This initiative began with the 2023 National Defense Authorization Act, which banned the use of US federal funds for foreign drone companies. Subsequent stringent regulations have reinforced this effort. The latest development is an executive order signed by the President this month, designed to accelerate drone adoption in the US. This order will benefit the industry, and ONDS specifically, by simplifying the process for obtaining Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS) waivers, thereby boosting deployment by public safety officials and corporations. The order also emphasizes counter-UAS systems as a crucial safeguard.

The recent FY26 budget allocates $21.2 billion to programs relevant to Ondas. This includes:

-   $4.2 billion for drone and counter-UAS initiatives.
    
-   $1.2 billion for AI, autonomy, and ISR development.
    
-   $2.9 billion for border and maritime UAS and sensor deployments.
    
-   $12.5 billion in FAA investments to support BVLOS and autonomous airspace integration.
    

## Ondas Network products

Revenue here is small in comparison to the drone segment, revenues in Q1 were $0.2 million flat YoY. Revenue will likely be small for the next few years as the product gains traction. The sales cycle will be long as it is a new technology replacing an existing legacy product that has been in place for decades. They are focused on US railway operators, an industry not renowned for the quick take-up of new technology.

Ondas calls its network product “dot 16”, they are referring to the new standard from the IEEE 802.16

**Key Aspects of IEEE 802.16 (WiMAX):**

-   **Purpose:** The primary objective of 802.16 is to offer broadband wireless access, serving as an alternative to traditional wired "last mile" connections like DSL and cable for both residential and business users. It was designed to cover significantly larger geographic areas compared to Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11).
    
-   **Commercial Name:** While officially called WirelessMAN by the IEEE, it gained widespread recognition under its commercial name, WiMAX. The WiMAX Forum, an industry alliance, played a crucial role in promoting and certifying the interoperability of products based on this standard.
    
-   **Features:**
    
    -   **Wireless Broadband:** It provides high-speed data connectivity, initially offering speeds of 30-40 Mbps, with later versions reaching up to 1 Gbps for fixed stations.
        
    -   **Metropolitan Area Networks (MANs):** Unlike Wi-Fi, which is used for local area networks (LANs), 802.16 is engineered for broader coverage, typically spanning a few kilometers and up to 50 km.
        
    -   **Architecture:** An 802.16 network consists of subscriber stations that connect wirelessly to a base station (BS). The base station, in turn, connects to the internet via a backbone network and is responsible for managing channel allocation and controlling data transmission.
        

Ondas is attempting to commercialize its technology in partnership with Siemens for large scale railroad use and it is gaining traction. This month, [Ondas announced](https://ir.ondas.com/press-releases/detail/219/ondas-networks-completes-first-buildout-of-its-802-16) the latest installation of its product in Chicago, following the selection of dot16 by the Association of American Railroads. Ondas holds the IP behind critical software for dot 16. Although rollout is slow, Ondas reports large numbers of key customers running small-scale trials of the product.

The tie-up with Siemens delivers a competitive advantage. Siemens is a major player in the world's railway industry, with operations in 60 countries. Siemens Mobility (the train division) has revenue of $4.6 billion and is known for its development of partnerships with new technology companies and often acquires these partners at a later date.

Siemens is leading the integration of digital technology in train services and could make a material difference to Ondas revenue further down the tracks.

## Conclusion

Ondas fits all my criteria, unique emerging technology. Proven sales with commercialization just beginning. A target price with at least 100% upside, a solid balance sheet, and operating in a market generally forecast for growth.

We also have the backup of the network products and a potential out if Siemens decides to acquire.

Negatives revolve around execution, especially with the takeovers. Hopefully, the management team has identified good targets that meet the criteria they set out.

I like the takeover strategy; the industry is fragmented with too many small players, and many of the larger players do not have a sustainable technological advantage. If Ondas can build out its IP by buying tuck-in companies, its value will increase substantially.

For a 12-24 month trade, this is a good opportunity, but please remember it is an unprofitable small-cap company that is about to ramp its commercialization.

Things can and often do go horribly wrong. I am not a financial advisor, but even if I were, I would not advise a large investment in this company; control risk by controlling position size. My investment will be less than 4% of my equity and less than 10% of the cash unused in my account.
