# Trade Alert (#74): Buying a Nano Cap

*My Second Recycling Investment*

**I am buying a nanocap;** companies with values less than $50 million present additional problems and risks. Their shares can be more volatile, with low liquidity, meaning relatively small amounts of buying can significantly alter the price. Sudden spikes are common, and they often retrace quickly.

I will begin this investment at a smaller-than-usual size. I intend to build the position as the company meets milestones and targets.

The first milestone will be revenue recognition (hopefully later this year), the second will be the start of construction on the second site (Q1 2026), and operation of that second site (Q3 2026).

After those four investments, it will be a full-size position with a 2-year time horizon.

I expect to see this company develop into a large operation, with multiple sites across North America. It has proven its technical ability and has commissioned its first commercial site, with patent protected first mover advantage I think it has real possibilities.

## Trade Alert #74: Buying Northstar Clean Technologies (TSXZ: ROOF, OTCPK: ROOOF)

**Trade Takeaway:** I am going to invest US$300 in ROOF. The investment might be split between the two tickers, as I have some Canadian dollars in the account, but not enough for the full investment. I will review the costs first.

$300 is a half-size position, less than 2% of equity. I will look to grow the position in the future.

At the time of writing, Northstar Clean Technologies (TSXV:ROOF, OTCPK:ROOOF) has a share price of CAD $ 0.32 and a market capitalization of CAD $ 48 million.

Companies like this are not suitable for large investments, and if you decide to invest, I would strongly advise reducing your normal position size.

It is likely that, if successful, we will remain in ROOF for several years and will have ample time to increase the size of our investment as the risk recedes.

This piece is structured in an unusual way, firstly a high level overview and then a chronological report on the development of the company. Essentially, a summary of the notes I have made when following them. I hope the chronological nature will show why I have decided to invest today.

## Overview.

Northstar (ROOF) is a recycling company; its technology has many similarities with ABAT. I have been following both for a couple of years, and their progression towards commercialization appears to have moved in tandem.

ROOF is not in a sexy business like ABAT so it has flown under the radar, meme stock holders are not interested in boring roof tiles so we have not seen a hype cycle move.

The company is based in Canada, with plans to expand into the US, and focuses on recycling waste asphalt roofing tiles, commonly known as shingles.

They have a proprietary, patent-protected technology and have built and commissioned their first commercial-scale recycling plant following the successful operation of a pilot plant.

ROOF has a significant first mover advantage; the competition is moving forward, but they appear to be several years away from building a commercial plant, and ROOF has significant pull-through demand from its large customers and even bigger push demand, as roofing companies currently have to dispose of shingles in landfills, which is an expensive additional cost.

Their business process might be like ABAT, but their finances are more akin to ELVA when we first invested. Desperately short of cash, in negative equity with bridging loans, convertibles, private loans, and dilution, keeping the operation going.

## The Business Model

ROOF expects two revenue streams, the first is tipping fees. They will charge less than a landfill site for companies to drop their waste shingles at a ROOF site. This revenue started last quarter, with the first deliveries to the commercial-scale facility. Long term it will represent 25-30% of revenue.

ROOF will take discarded asphalt shingles, currently heading to landfill, and reprocess them with a proprietary chemical-based system into the original components, selling liquid asphalt, aggregate, fibre, and recently announced limestone.

ROOF management are incredibly concerned about the competitive situation and do not give much away, this was demonstrated yesterday when they announced they had successfully separated limestone from shingles.

In previous interviews they had said the output from the commercial scale plant was much better than from the pilot plant containing less contamination without divulging why. Yesterday a press release said a US patent has been awarded for the limestone extraction technique.

Although a low value commodity ($100 per tonne) its extraction does significantly improve the quality of the other products they have for sale.

Limestone gives ROOF three outputs, the most valuable of which is the extracted asphalt oil.

The outputs can be used in multiple construction products but the demand for roofing shingles is so large it will likely account for the vast majority of revenue for the foreseeable future.

The size of the market is staggering; 85% of US homes are roofed with shingles, accounting for 83 million homes roofed every year. When a house is re-roofed, the old shingles go to landfill. ROOF estimate: 16 million tonnes of shingles go to landfill in North America. (I checked the figure and the EPA estimates 15 million tonnes go to landfill in the US, I assume the other 1 million tonnes is Canada)

Asphalt is toxic in landfills; each ton of shingles contains more than a million barrels of oil, which seeps out of the landfill as the shingle decomposes.

The current efforts to recycle shingles are focused on repurposing the asphalt into hot-mix asphalt for roads. Simply shredding the shingles and mixing them into the hopper. This technique is not cost-effective and has generated little traction.

The ROOF process produces liquid asphalt and has patents awarded in Canada and the US. International patents have been applied for. The commercial site can turn the hot oil into pellets, which changes the economics dramatically, allowing for them to be shipped over longer distances.

The business model relies on multiple plants close to large conurbations. The used shingles cannot be economically transported for large distances, and drop-off sites must be easily accessible by roofing companies.

## Growth plans

The first commercial site in Calgary is complete and has been commissioned. It has the capacity for 150 tons per day and has a 100% off-take agreement in place with McAsphalt.

The first plants in the US will be at the sites of TAMKO manufacturing facilities. TAMKO is a strategic investor and one of the largest roofing companies in the US. TAMKO and ROOF have signed an MOU to build 4 facilities. The first TAMKO facility will be in Frederick Maryland close to an existing TAMKO site, permitting discussions have been initiated and it is expected to be site 3 opening in 2026.

A site has been selected for a facility in Hamilton, Ontario, and an LOI has been signed on the long-term lease from a port authority. This site is being built close to an existing McAsphalt facility and off take agreements are being negotiated, this will be site number 2 and is due to open in 2026.

ROOT plans to build its own facilities but is prepared to license its technology. Management has guided to the following rollout

## Chronological report

### 2021 The Birth of the Plan

In late 2020, ROOF enacted a reverse takeover of Empower Environmental Solutions, after which the shareholders of Empower owned 62% of the shares in the new company. The company filed its prospectus in June 2021 and raised CAD$12 million by issuing 34 million units at CAD$0.35 (one share and one warrant). The company listed on the TSX Venture exchange with Ticker ROOF and on the OTCQB US (pink sheets) market under the ticker ROOOF.

Empower had already developed its pilot facility in Delta, British Columbia, and initiated small-scale steady-state operations at the facility in February 2022. The pilot facility had two outputs: liquid asphalt and aggregate, which were delivered as samples to a range of customers, including road construction and shingle manufacturers.

The process is known as BEST, which stands for Bitumen Extraction and Separation Technology.

In March, Calgary was announced as the location for the first commercial site, and plans were unveiled to search for a suitable site and design a facility with a daily capacity of 150 tonnes.

### 2022: the road to commercialization

After receiving samples in 2021, McAsphalt signed a 5-year 100% off-take agreement for liquid Asphalt from the proposed Calgary plant. McAsphalt has 50 years of experience and operates 27 terminals across Canada, it is a wholly owned subsidiary of Colas, a Canadian infrastructure development company.

The Delta site was effectively shut down, allowing all resources to be redirected towards developing the Calgary site. The Delta site can “run on demand” but does so with negative gross margin.

ROOF acquired a 15 year lease on the Calgary site and the necessary permits to begin construction.

A private placement of 4.8million shares to Renewable U at CAD$0.40 secured total financing of CAD$43 million including debentures and debt.

ROOF received its first US patents in late 2022 with a 20 year life.

### 2023: Construction Begins

Roof took possession of the Calgary site in September with all necessary permits in place. Site development work began in March and was completed. Long lead items had been ordered, engineering design was 75% completed, and the first delivery of waste shingles arrived from IKO.

ROOT received a grant of CAD$7 million to develop the Calgary site and the Canadian patent was granted. The samples produced at the Delta site had been accepted by all recipients and negotiations had begun with numerous potential customers.

Further financing was arranged CAD$0.6 million in convertible debentures, CAD$2.7 million for shares @CAD$0.15, a credit facility for CAD$9 million.

TAMKO, invested CAD$8.5 million and agreed three rounds of further funding to allow for the building of ROOF sites near TAMKO facilities in the US.

### 2024: Commisioning Site 1

IKO Canada, the largest shingle manufacturer, signed a binding 5-year supply agreement to deliver waste shingles to the Calgary site.

Ecco Recycling signed a second supply agreement with a 3 year length.

Construction of the Calgary site was nearing completion, and by the end of 2024, commissioning had commenced. Milestone payments from the government were received to help with funding.

A series of private placements raised CAD$10 million and CVW provided up to $14 million in debentures.

ROOF signed the lease on the second site in Hamilton, Ontario. (adjacent to McAsphalt Ontario)

A further expansion agreement with YORK1 Environmental Waste Services was announced to colocate ROOF facilities at existing YORK1 sites. (removing permitting issues) YORK1 is another major Canadian infrastructure company with 9 recycling facilities and several more planned.

### 2025: Commercial Operations

By the end of Q2, construction was nearing completion in Calgary, and the company announced the first successful production of liquid asphalt, thereby freeing up additional government funding.

ROOF received an LOI from Export Development Canada to provide financial support for the building of the company’s first US site.

Further large sales of shares were announced (12million shares at CAD$0.30)

Management guides to the start of commercial operations.

### Earnings call Q2 2025

Fair value adjustments in the CVW debenture led to a loss of $367k in the quarter and $721k in H1.

Capex decreased to $1.9 m from $4.1. The completion of construction resulted in a $3.9 million milestone payment from Emissions Reduction Alberta.

ROOF exited the quarter with a $1.7 million deficit, $1.5 of which represents convertible debentures where the strike price is below current share price.

Raised $3.6m from private unit placement, giving sufficient cash flow to fund the business to operations, they expect a further payment from the ERA grant. A consistent stream of warrants and convertibles is being exercised, bringing in $150k during the quarter. They expect this to continue.

In the last 4 years, they have raised $58 million, $30 million of which is non-dilutive.

The product coming out of the Calgary site is better than the Delta site, above expectations, and the Delta product had already been accepted.

The output from Calgary comes in two parts: hot oil for local delivery, which is what came out of Delta, and asphalt pellets, which will have a much larger delivery potential. A new process takes the hot oil being produced and pellitizes it. Asphalt pellets are a globally traded commodity, and no other company has successfully produced them from used shingles.

The final grant payment will be made based on proven operations, which are designated at 80 tonnes per day moving through the facility. ROOT has been guided to achieve this in 2025. The Calgary facility has a capacity of 15 tonnes an hour, feedstock is secured for 150 tonnes so ten hours of operations and the CEO said he expects to hit that in Q1 2026 before moving to a second shift if they can secure the feedstock. That should not be difficult, as they are offering a significantly discounted price for dropping shingles relative to the landfill price.

Sites 2 and 3 continue to move forward: in Hamilton, the site-specific layout is being developed. The first meeting with the permitting body has taken place, and federal and provincial funding options have been identified.

US#1, site 3. Permitting authorities have been engaged, and negotiations have started on the feedstock.

TAMKO will be the major off-taker, but negotiations are underway with other potential customers. Once again, federal and local grants are available to help fund the facility.

Both facilities are being permitted for 80,000 tonnes, which is a double shift operation 24/7. Management guide to $100 million of turnover and $10 million of EBITDA per operating plant when doing 80,000 tonnes.

It means 10 sites have a potential for $1 billion in revenue and $100 million in EBITDA.

## Forecasts and Finances.

The thing I like most is the agreements in place. For the first Calgary site ROOF has guaranteed supply agreements with IKO and a 100% off take agreement with McAsphalt.

The Calgary site should resolve the balance sheet issues very quickly, and if they can repeat this, profitability is on the short-term horizon.

The balance sheet is complicated, lots of warrants, convertibles and different avenues of funding. Even allowing for the complications, the company is not in great shape.

## The Competition

The ROOF facility is the first of its kind, nobody else has a facility that can return shingles to its original constituents.

Shingles are recycled in small quantities by shredding them and mixing them with road asphalt. The practice is not suitable in all areas and not allowed in many, it produces an inferior road surface, accounting for the small uptake.

GAF, a major shingle company, has committed $100 million to develop its RoofCycle process to commercial scale. The company has developed a pilot facility and is trialling its recycled products as a small percentage of its Timberline Roof Shingles product. Timberline (in some small batches) contains a maximum of 7% recycled content. I have discounted this threat due to the small percentage of recycled material, the process turns the old shingles into granules that can be mixed with virgin material, it does not return the shingles to their original constituent parts.

Owens Corning is a major roofing company, they have a program with a partner to develop a recycling facility but it is still at the lab stage. Owens Corning does recycle asphalt into roads.

Sky Quarry (SKYQ) another emerging company aiming to repurpose shingles with a proprietary solvent technology. SKYQ owns an oil refinery in Nevada where it intends to refine the recovered bitumen oil from shingles into diesel fuel blending the recovered oil with conventional heavy crude.

SKYQ has acquired an oil sands remediation facility in Utah, which it intends to retrofit into its first operational shingle processing facility.

I have discounted SKYQ for several reasons: its market cap of $10 million is just too small for me to invest in, the majority of its income is forecast to come from the refinery, and that is a tough business at the moment, and finally, it has not yet managed to build an operational shingle recycling facility. It has unique and proprietary technology but has not proven it can work at scale.

Numerous other companies, notably The Shelly Company, Venture Corporation, North Texas Natural, and Roof Top Recycling, are all putting old shingles into hot mix asphalt for roads.

## Conclusion

The 100% deals at Calgary for input and output to the facility seriously de-risk that operation, we know the process works after the pilot facility and we know they can construct a commercial scale facility after Calgary.

Companies receiving sample output have signed offtake agreements and provided funding for new sites, wanting them located next to their facilities.

The market is huge, and ROOF has a patented, protected solution with first-mover advantages.

The risks are high, the company is tiny with no cash and negative equity, plus a whole load of debt.

I am going to take a half-size position in ROOF and will increase it to a full size after they register their first revenue from the sale of output and hit the 80,000 tonnes requirement for the final grant payment, hopefully later this year. I will look to add to the position when site two is operational and may continue to add for some time.
