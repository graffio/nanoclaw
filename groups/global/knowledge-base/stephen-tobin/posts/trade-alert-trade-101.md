## Trade Alert #101: Buying Compass Pathways (CMPS)

**Key Takeaway:** I will open a full-size position in CMPS ($600) with a midpoint order after this article is published. It follows yesterday’s topline release of the COMP 306 data and extra data announced during the investor call later in the day.

I covered much of the background to this trade in an article [published in November](https://stephentobin.substack.com/p/psychedelic-drug-therapy-competitive?utm_source=publication-search) on Substack. I also wrote in August this year on Substack and multiple times on Seeking Alpha over the last few years on this topic.

### The Business Case

CMPS is seeking approval for Treatment Resistant Depression [TRD], TRD is a rapidly expanding market with increased diagnosis and a better understanding of wellbeing in the community.

The only FDA approved drug for these patients is Spravato, an MDMA derived treatment sold by Johnson and Johnson that has turned into a blockbuster drug. Sales of Spravato have grown from $689 million in 2023 to $1.7 billion in 2025 and continues to grow at around 50% a year. Johnson & Johnson expects Spravato to reach $5 billion in the US alone in 2025. It is a monster drug with zero competition.

Spravato has led to a better understanding of the potential amongst clinicians and has driven the build-out of more than 3,000 clinics in the US able to deliver the therapy. Crucially, it has been shown that insurance will cover the cost of this type of treatment.

## The Case for Compass

CMPS released new data yesterday, including the first look at its second phase III trial, COMP006, and the 26-week readout of its first phase III trial, COMP005.

The FDA generally requires two successful Phase III trials to approve a drug. The first data release from COMP005 caused a drop in the share price because the results were below market expectations and those reported by earlier studies from multiple companies.

The recent results confirmed those findings, again pointing to moderate improvement. In this article I am going to compare these results with Spravatos’s two Phase IIIs and look for an explanation for the more modest results.

**CMPS 360 is the best we have seen.**

Companies use the MADRS scale to report improvements, this is a clinician administered questionnaire that attempts to measure a patient’s feeling of depression. It is a long-standing and accepted way of monitoring these treatments.

Both the Compass Phase III trials have shown a very similar improvement in meeting the primary endpoint at week 6.

Spravato never managed to achieve this. Johnson and Johnson conducted 5 phase III trials.

Transform 1 failed to reach its primary endpoint

Transform 2 was the flagship study in adults aged 18-64 that showed a statistically significant reduction in MADRS relative to a placebo.

Transform 3 failed to show any improvement in adults aged over 65

Aspire 1 and Aspire 2 both failed to show improvement relative to placebo at the four week endpoint.

Sustain 1: This was used as the second phase III success, but it was pushing the limits. The study showed that if patients who had responded initially stayed on the drug, they fared better than those who switched to placebo.

The pivotal Transform 2 showed a change in MADRS v Placebo of -4.0

Aspire 1 and 2 both included significant in patient care which is a big issue of contention. The quality of psychotherapy will inevitably have an impact on these therapies, and in these studies, it was proven to be as effective with or without the drug. One must also question the Transform 2 results as the unblinding will have failed, most patients will have known if they received a psychedelic drug or not, potentially biasing the results.

Despite these issues, it is turning into a blockbuster.

In the call after the recent release, Compass shared important data suggesting it is a far more effective drug than Spravato.

## Trial description

COMP005 is comparing the results of the drug v a placebo.

COMP006 is comparing different doses. 25mg, 10mg or 1mg.

The second trial removes the unblinding problem, and as there is a difference between how much is prescribed, it is most likely down to the drug and not the placebo effect.

## Trial Design

Compass have been careful with their trial design and importantly have not allowed for any psychiatric care in the testing. This means any effects are isolated to the drug but also implies the actual effects could be much higher if packaged with high quality psychiatric care as would be expected in the real world.

## Conclusion

Remember I am not medically trained and have no experience of these drugs or mental health issues, I do not understand the mechanisms of action or the pharmacology behind this technology.

By training, I am a mathematician, and as such, my conclusion is based on the statistics presented by Compass and nothing else.

But my conclusion is that the Compass drug looks likely to pass both Phase III trials, meeting its primary endpoints and showing a clear improvement over both a placebo and a low dose of the drug. The results are not clouded by other treatments, and they show results at least as good as Spravato, requiring a fraction of the number of doses.

Spravato was unable to replicate its phase III success in subsequent cohorts and showed no improvement over standard care in some trials. The Compass trial has been designed to cover all cohorts and is showing a meaningful difference in all of them, the addition of psychiatric care would further improve patient outcomes, but that is for future trials.

The only possible issue relating to the compass trial is how “white” it is; some 87% of all patients identified as being white, and that issue may have to be addressed in future studies.
