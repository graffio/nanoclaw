## Qubit Research

The table lists the main Qubit types and the companies researching them. The first, Superconducting, has many more companies than are listed. It is the go-to architecture in China and Japan, as well as in most existing computer/semiconductor companies, because it relies on established silicon chip engineering technology.

| Qubit Type | Key Companies | How it Works | Primary Pros | Primary Cons |
|------------|---------------|--------------|--------------|--------------|
| Superconducting | IBM, Google, D-Wave, Baidu, Rigetti | Uses superconducting circuits and "Josephson junctions" to create artificial atoms. | Extremely fast gate speeds; leverages existing chip fab. | Requires extreme cooling (≈15mK); high error rates. |
| Trapped Ion | IonQ, Quantinuum | Uses individual charged atoms (ions) suspended in a vacuum by electromagnetic fields. | Long coherence times; high-fidelity gates; "all-to-all" connectivity. | Slower gate operations; scaling to many qubits is physically complex. |
| Photonic | PsiQuantum, Xanadu | Uses particles of light (photons) traveling through optical circuits. | Can operate at room temperature; naturally suited for networking. | Difficult to make photons "interact" with each other; high loss rates. |
| Neutral Atom | QuEra, Pasqal, Atom Computing | Uses lasers ("optical tweezers") to trap and move uncharged atoms. | Massive scalability (1,000+ qubits); flexible connectivity. | Slower than superconducting; sensitive to laser noise. |
| Silicon Spin | Intel, Diraq, Quantum Motion | Uses the spin of an electron or nucleus trapped in a silicon "quantum dot." | Extremely small footprint; compatible with standard CMOS manufacturing. | Very sensitive to material impurities; still in relatively early stages. |
| Topological | Microsoft | Uses "Majorana quasiparticles" to encode info in the way particles move around each other. | Theoretically "immune" to most noise and environmental errors. | Extremely difficult to prove and engineer; still largely experimental. |

*Source image: [Qubit architecture table](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!X6iu!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fde2a713f-1600-4a33-bb35-348316fac339_1600x668.png)*

One of these architectures will win the race to build a working quantum computer. My Strategic Waves trading plan values two things in emerging technology: being the first and being the best. I disregard any other positions.

There are two front-runners for being the best. Superconducting qubits have an enormous advantage over all other architectures; they are as much as 1000x faster at performing operations. They could be second to market and still be successful because of this speed advantage. Topological qubits are the most elegant, if the Majorana particle can be produced the way Microsoft suggests then it will likely dominate the space. It should be as fast as a superconducting machine and be immune to environmental disturbances.

I am putting Microsoft to one side for the time being. I have covered the technology in detail, but until we have more definitive information about the technologies existence.

The players face the same problems. Can they correct errors, and can we make systems big enough to solve something useful?

It has been over 30 years since Peter Shor posted his algorithm that would allow a computer to break today's financial encryption systems. Initially it was thought a machine would need billions of qubits to do it, the work of a generation of computer scientists brought that number down to a million but it has remained decades beyond current capabilities. However, things are changing faster than at any time since Shor's original work.

This week, a team at CalTech showed a design for a machine that could run Shor's algorithm with just 9000 qubits using neutral atoms — a number that is perhaps achievable this decade. This is bigger news than it appears because it is one thing to design a machine with few qubits, but to run Shor's you have to correct errors in real time. The CalTech team showed a way to do it, with fewer qubits than had previously been proposed.

## Neutral Atoms — The Emerging Front-Runner?

### QuEra Computing

**QuEra** is a spinoff from Harvard and MIT, and its technology is based on neutral atoms, or more specifically, neutral rubidium atoms. Rubidium is used in many precision instruments including atomic clocks, its properties have been very well studied.

- **Error Correction Progress:** QuEra published a landmark paper last year using the error-correcting Bacon-Shor code, showing that they can now correct two types of errors that crop up in real-time computing; computational errors and erasure errors. When in error correction mode, QuEra effectively turns 48 physical qubits into a single logical qubit.

- **Scale:** At 256 qubits, it is behind superconducting competitors in raw count, but with 48:1 physical to logical qubit compression the effective count is improved.

- **Speed Trade-Off:** The big downside is that all neutral atom machines are significantly slower than superconducting machines. At around 2 microseconds per gate cycle versus 50 nanoseconds for superconducting, the speed is 40x slower per gate. These machines will not win on raw compute.

### Pasqal

**Pasqal** is a French company spun out of Institut d'Optique and is also pursuing neutral atoms. They have 1000 qubits — significantly more than QuEra.

- **Architecture:** Their approach uses a 2D and 3D array of neutral atoms. This enables connectivity patterns that superconducting computers cannot achieve.

- **Business Focus:** Pasqal are focused on near-term applications that work with noisy qubits, particularly optimization problems like logistics.

### Atom Computing

**Atom Computing** is a US-based startup. They built a 1,225-qubit machine in 2023 and are focused on using ytterbium-171 atoms.

- **Milestone:** They have demonstrated the ability to entangle qubits — a key benchmark for quantum advantage.

- **Differentiation:** The use of ytterbium gives Atom Computing potential long coherence times that could outperform rubidium-based approaches.
