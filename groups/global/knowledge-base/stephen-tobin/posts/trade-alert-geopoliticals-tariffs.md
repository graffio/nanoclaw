# Trade Alert: Geopoliticals, Tariffs and a Short Report 

*Action and decisions needed*

A surge of geopolitical news hit several stocks yesterday, and a short seller report hit one.

I have decided to take action, closing one more stock and putting in exit strategies for two others.

## Geo Politics Improving

Peace talks between Russia and Ukraine may lead to a slowing of military spending, especially of ordnance. However, countries offering air and sea support to Ukraine may increase the requirement for drones and air defence systems.

In a similar move, Hamas has accepted the terms of a potential ceasefire with Israel. It is unlikely they had much of a choice with Israel's overwhelming military superiority and obvious desire to destroy that organization.

One of the driving forces behind some of our investments has been the potential for increased spending by Nato members as they grow their military expenditure towards 5% of GDP. There is a real chance that as soon as these conflicts come to an end, governments will turn their attention elsewhere; they all have domestic issues to deal with and will face opposition to increased spending on the military when other areas need the money. Politicians have notoriously short memories and the ability to back track on everything they have said if it will give them a few extra votes.

I exited British Aerospace yesterday for a 38% profit, increasing the cash balance in response to these improvements in the geopolitical situation.

I have also reconsidered tactics for our other defence and done holdings.

## **Inflation Surge Driven by Tariff Pass-Through**

The 0.9% month-over-month (MoM) jump in final demand PPI marked the strongest single-month gain since March 2022, signaling accelerating wholesale price pressures directly tied to recently implemented tariffs. Services PPI surged 1.1% MoM —led by a 2% spike in wholesale and retail margins —while goods prices excluding food and energy rose 0.4%, indicating broadening inflationary pressures beyond initial tariff targets. Businesses are shifting from absorbing costs to passing tariffs onto consumers, with U.S. weighted-average tariff rates climbing from near 3% to 18%, a trend expected to persist through late 2025.

Core PPI excluding food, energy, and trade services posted its strongest advance since 2022, raising concerns about the durability of disinflation trends.

The PPI is a concern and may be an indicator of a coming slowdown in activity and a correction in share prices. Less disposable income means less saving, reducing some buying on the stock market. Increased PPI can lead to reduced earnings and hiring in companies, which in turn leads to a slowing stock market.

Overall, now might be a good time to increase cash balances and reduce risk exposure.

## **Sector-Specific Tariff Impacts**

On Monday, the Trump administration significantly increased the scope of its steel and aluminium tariffs, adding a further 400 product categories. It isn't easy to work out precisely what the new product categories are (the press release only includes the 10-digit customs code). Still, it is reported that they contain parts for wind turbines, bulldozers, heavy machinery, rail cars, compressors, and pumps.

The reach of the tariffs in the automotive industry was extended to include components for buses, air conditioners, exhaust systems, steel for electric vehicles, and EV components.

Household appliances were also hit with refrigerators, freezers, and dryers facing new tariffs.

Tesla had asked for steel for electric vehicles and wind turbines to be excluded, as there is currently no domestic supply.

Proposed 50% copper tariffs threaten to disrupt infrastructure, renewables, transportation, and data services, with JPMorgan warning of "most disruptive" effects on end-user sectors reliant on critical materials.

Our Holdings in EVs and clean energy are under consideration as a result of these changes, and I intend to exit one position today.

## **Global Supply Chain and Trade Fragmentation**

Tariffs are accelerating global trade fragmentation, with emerging economies facing slower export growth and intensified Chinese competition in automotive and medium-to-high-end sectors. Europe’s import dependency on non-EU manufactured goods has risen from 15% to 25% over 20 years, particularly in high-tech components like telecommunications equipment (82.5% dependency), worsening vulnerability to tariff shocks.

The overall changes are positive for our holdings of Chinese stocks, and I will look to add to them in the coming weeks.

## **Policy and Market Reactions**

Industry analysts are publishing their thoughts. Here is a quick rundown

-   Federal Reserve Dilemma: The PPI surge complicates the Fed’s path, as wholesale price acceleration risks delaying September rate cuts despite a cooling labor market. Markets now weigh whether the jump reflects a one-off tariff adjustment or persistent inflation, with Treasury yields rising post-release.
    
-   Global Growth Revisions: TA Securities slashed 2025 GDP growth forecasts to 4.4% from 4.8%-5.3%, citing trade uncertainties and policy risks, while the IMF, World Bank, and OECD collectively cut global growth projections by 0.2–0.5 percentage points due to tariffs and geopolitical conflicts.
    
-   Equity Market Pressures: Persistent foreign capital outflows and eroding investor confidence—driven by Israel-Iran conflict escalation and U.S. tariff policies—have exerted downward pressure on regional equities.
    

## **Forward-Looking Risks**

Negotiations with China continue despite the passing of the deadline date; China does not appear rattled by the tariffs and may not be prepared to go as far as the US administration would like to seek a deal.

This confluence of tariff-driven inflation, fragmented trade flows, and geopolitical instability suggests elevated volatility will persist through 2H 2025.

It changes my base case; I had assumed H2 would be the end of the volatility, and expected to see renewed growth later in the year. I am now moving to a more conservative outlook.

I think it may be wise to increase our cash holdings, reduce some of the risk we have, and rotate out of areas likely to be affected most by the tariff changes.

## Aurora Innovation (AUR)

Dropped yesterday on the release of a short seller report. Kerrisdale Capital is a reasonable outfit, and this short report was quite well done. They did not raise any concerns of dishonesty but called into question the potential of the business model.

The Kerrisdale argument is that a hub-and-spoke business model will be less competitive because of the cost of drayage combined with the cost of the self-driving trucks.

Drayage is driving the truck from a hub near a highway to the center of a city. The Aurora driver only goes between hubs; it will not drive through cities.

Aurora has built three hubs to allow it to test its product and refine its operations.

Kerrisdale implies that this is the business model, where Aurora or someone else builds hubs with trucking companies delivering loads to the hub. Aurora then carries the load to another hub where the shipping company picks it up and drives it to its destination.

That is not the business model; if it were, I would agree with Kerrisdale, as such a model would never work.

Aurora does not intend to operate a hub and spoke network, nor will it own any trucks beyond the initial test fleet.

The business model is to sell Aurora on board trucks to large distribution companies.

Kerrisdale did not consider that the main customers of Aurora already operate a hub-and-spoke network. FedEx has seven SuperHubs in Memphis, Indianapolis, Newark, Greensboro, Fort Worth, Oakland, and Anchorage.

Memphis and Indianapolis are the two largest.

FedEx has a wide distribution of regional hubs that link into the superhubs. All the superhubs and most of the regional hubs are close to the highway network, so they will not require drayage.

This image (from FedEx) shows how regional hubs spoke into the Minneapolis superhub.

Only 5 of the 21 regional hubs are positioned away from the highway network.

FedEx will be able to use Aurora Trucks between the majority of its regional hubs and its superhubs and between all of its superhubs.

The second issue was the price of the trucks. We know the price of these systems fall as they move to production. Pony reduced theirs by 70%, I see no reason why Aurora and its partner Continental cannot do the same.

Kerrisdale also ignores the critical issue of driver shortages; FedEx and the rest are having trouble hiring and retaining enough drivers. Aurora allows them to replace drivers going between 75% of their hubs.

It is a good short report; it presents a believable story and fits the data they present. As is often the case, it misses out more data than it includes. The purpose of the report is to get the share price to fall, and it looks like it might work.

People who do not look into it in any depth will accept the report and sell, moving the stock lower. If this happens, I will exit and buy again when it hits the bottom.
