# Weekly: Stock Recovery Ends November

*Target List Continues to Grow*

## Trading Performance

The account ended the week at $17,333, up from $16,262 this time last week, an increase of $1,071 or 6.6%.

Gains were broad-based, with 15 of our 17 holdings registering gains; the best performer rose 19% and the worst dropped 7% on what looked like quite positive earnings.

The gains were not enough to wipe out the losses suffered in the first part of the month, and the account closed down 11.5% in November, our worst monthly performance since inception, breaking a 7-month streak of gains.

Year to date, we are up 73% and the Annualized IRR is 124.9%. We remain ahead of our target of growing a $ 250-a-month investment to $100,000 in five years.

## Trading Activity

We closed a lot of trades in November, a significant pullback in risk-based assets forced me to enact the move to cash plan. I trade in high-risk securities, and they suffer greatly in any pullback. I preserve capital by exiting those that seem most at risk, and it has left us with the highest cash balance in our history.

## Outlook

I am currently working through all the companies we have exited, looking for bargains: companies that have pulled back more than they should and now present outsized potential.

The emerging technology sectors I trade rotate slowly and one of those rotations is underway, recycling technology in a range of materials is reaching an inflection point and 2026 is likely to be the time the market wakes up to the potential. We already have two recycling stocks and I hope to double that to four in December. Numerous potential targets exist, and it is a matter of choosing those with technological and market progress that offer near-term commercial success potential.

Recycling will be replacing LiDAR in my list of emerging technologies. LiDAR is now mainstream, heading towards a commodity price-competitive marketplace. We are heavily invested in our chosen LiDAR company and will hold that position as it reaps the benefits of its advanced technology, commercial positioning, and scaled production.

The Small Drone market will follow LiDAR, another technology that is now established, and its protagonists are overpriced relative to their future cash flows, which will be squeezed by intense competition. I still like large drones, eVTOL, and counter-drone technologies as these remain pre-commercialisation. In the case of the counter-drone, we still await the emergence of the defining technology.

Quantum remains of genuine interest and was one of my main research areas last week. The technology continues to make progress, but D-Wave apart, is probably a decade away from commercialization, and I think a lot of investing pain lies ahead.

IONQ is my biggest concern. With a market cap of $18 billion, it is drawing in an enormous amount of interest from institutions and retail investors alike. The problem remains quite simple, IONQ’s quantum computer will never reach the market. They will keep talking about the amazing progress they are making with the technology and how it is speeding up relative to classical machines, but they avoid the unsolvable problems of gate speed, error correction, and scaling.

IONQ is using its cash to buy companies with technology in networking, sensing, and space, and that may be the way forward for them. Still, investors will inevitably get frustrated by the lack of commercial progress on their quantum computer. Let me be clear: there is no commercial progress. They sold one machine in 2023 (plus a contract to upgrade it), none in 2024, none in 2025, and have not given guidance on any further sales. No paying companies are using the technology over the cloud to solve real business problems because the system is not reliable enough or capable enough and I maintain it never will be. I first raised my concerns about IONQ’s tech in April 2024 when I published an article on Seeking Alpha called “Don’t buy the wrong Qubit Technology”. I think I was correct then and have not changed my opinion.

There are quantum companies I want to buy, but I am worried that the hype surrounding IONQ will bring all of the companies lower when it eventually bursts.

I hope to buy at least three companies next week, and five or six before the markets slow for the Christmas holidays, putting the large cash balance to work in emerging technology companies that could see inflection points in 2026

The Margin Portfolio

The experimental account with IG Markets in the UK delivered strong returns, averaging 11% per month over six months. The account had several problems. The first was the lack of available companies to trade; many of my biggest wins were unavailable on the platform, which significantly reduced profits. A second, bigger problem was the increased volatility. I have been unable to work out a strategy that would deliver long-term growth with an acceptable level of risk, and as a result, I have decided to end the project. The only way to trade profitably was to limit trades to less than a week, and I am not really interested in that kind of high-frequency trading.

I have withdrawn my funds (total invested: £1,500; profit: £776) and will look for a new project in 2026.
