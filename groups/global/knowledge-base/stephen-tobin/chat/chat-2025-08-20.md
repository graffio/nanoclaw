> **Stephen Tobin** (08:50): It is a good question. Like most of my rules, they have been developed to avoid situations that have happened in the past. I first started investing in emerging technology in the 1990s when I bought into the dotcom frenzy. I have made and lost quite a lot in that time and for a long time my results were far more volatile than the markets. I have bought large numbers of stocks that collapsed as much as 90% and consequently ended up with a lot of very small holdings. As I watched, some of these stocks rebounded quickly, a few failed, but most just carried on for years with little change. I keep accurate records of every trade I have ever taken on my spreadsheets and base all of my rules on these past trades. When I analyzed all the results, 30%-40% appeared to be a sensible point to stop trades; once they get past that, the chance of short-term recovery drops significantly. I developed the rule: Analyse at 30% close at 40%. Of course, I will wait a bit if I can see a catalyst in the coming weeks that could change things. In the 2000s, I had so much of my capital tied up in these small stocks that I had to do a real job, and investing was a loss-making hobby. If I had cut my losses and invested the money in my winning stocks I would be far richer than I am.
The rules about taking profit near the top came from a similar study of results. DDD is one I still remember. I bought it for $1 and watched it rise to $100 before collapsing back to $1.
As for buying back in, there are two parts to the plan. The first is to decide if my original thesis is correct, which comes down to the technology. Is it good? Is it disruptive? Will it sell?: The second is working out why the stock collapsed in the first place. Looking at QSI, it could be the revenue miss. If so, I would look for clear evidence that revenue is returning; without it, I won't buy again. With all the stocks I exit, I wait for a change in price before reevaluating. I have a spreadsheet called Sectors, which tracks all the technologies I am interested in and shows price changes when I see an uptick on a stock or a sector I have previously owned I add the name to my prospect list. This separate spreadsheet keeps track of all the companies I need to review.
