## Trade Alert: Buying Virgin Galactic (SPCE)

**Key Takeaway:** I will place a MidPrice order, targeting a position size of $500 in Virgin Galactic (SPCE) before markets open today.

## Thesis

SPCE has a unique air-launched spacecraft, using a mothership to carry the spacecraft to high altitude before the rocket-powered craft disengages and powers into space. The spacecraft will move beyond the space boundary around 50 miles above Earth. It will stay in this microgravity area for around 4 or 5 minutes before gliding back to earth.

The technology is differentiated from its only competitor, Blue Origin, who offer a traditional vertical take-off and parachute back to earth space experience.

SPCE's technology has been proven with its earlier VSS Unity space craft, and it has taken a total of 800 Bookings for its $600,000 space experience. It will be enough to keep it in operation for two years, and it will reopen for bookings in Q1 2026.

In addition, SPCE is attacking the large microgravity scientific market, where it will have a near monopoly on quick turnaround experimentation. A competitive advantage likely to drive its 2029 revenue over $1 billion, deliver profitable operations in 2026, and generate free cash flow in 2027.

The space craft technology of SPCE gives it a substantial and sustainable competitive advantage. This is an industry with high barriers to entry, and the unique operating model of SPCE gives it a cost structure that will likely give it the lion's share of this new market. Forecasts put the microgravity market in the tens of billions of dollars by 2030, and currently, SPCE looks set to be the only volume player.

### The Competitive Situation

Blue Origin is not publicly stating its flight price, but for $150,000, you can make a reservation and discuss a bespoke pricing model. In its inaugural flight, seats went for $28 million. Estimates put the current cost in the low millions.

The New Shepherd reusable spacecraft can carry six people and has flown 75 people into space. It is designed to be used 25 times and fly once a month.

In contrast, the SPCE Delta spacecraft will be able to fly twice a week for 500 missions, and charge a figure below $500,000 when in full operation.

The difference between the operating parameters is what makes me bullish SPCE.

## SPCE Timeline

SPCE began commercial operations in 2022 using its second spaceship VSS Unity. It shifted from Unity's monthly flights to quarterly in Q3 2023 to prioritize development of its next-generation Delta-class spaceships. Unity's final commercial flight, Galactic 7, was on June 8, 2024, after which operations paused.

Unity generated only $1.8-2.4 million in revenue per flight, with one flight a month, costs exceeded revenue, which drove the transition to the next generation Delta-Class spaceships.

Delta ships offer significant improvements: 50% more seats (six vs. four), $2.7-3.6 million revenue per flight, and a target of eight flights per month, creating a 12x revenue multiplier. SPCE is a predominantly fixed cost operation with limited variable cost so the increase in revenue will drive extremely high margins and a very profitable business.

Delta's technical advancements include high-temperature BMI composites for weight reduction and increased capacity, and a maintenance revolution, reducing inter-flight inspections from 14 days for Unity to less than a day for Delta. Each Delta ship is designed for 500+ flights, with an estimated recurring production cost of $50-60 million and payback periods under six months.

Assembly of Delta ships began in Q1 2025, with the first research and private astronaut flights expected in Fall 2026. (recently delayed from summer 2026).

With the existing mother ship, VMS Eve, supporting three missions weekly, the first two Delta ships can perform ~125 flights annually, carrying 750+ astronauts, generating ~$450 million in annualized revenue at $600,000 per seat. Virgin Galactic plans a fully utilized spaceport with 4-5 spaceships and two motherships, enabling 300-400 flights annually and over $1 billion in revenue.

VMS Eve can support three flights per week, initially constraining Delta operations. Virgin Galactic's Launch Vehicle X (LVX) program is developing a second-generation mothership, due by 2028, which will also target high-altitude, heavy-lift capabilities for government and research as an additional market. The second LVX mothership will be added in 2029 alongside the third and fourth Delta ships, targeting up to $1 billion in annual revenue. Spaceflights are projected to have contribution margins exceeding 80% with substantial fixed cost leverage.

## Commercial Demand

Virgin Galactic's new Delta class spacecraft will facilitate scientific experiments by providing a brief microgravity environment during suborbital flights. These experiments offer a cost-effective, faster alternative to International Space Station (ISS) research and can test equipment for future orbital missions.

The short four to five-minute weightless period necessitates carefully designed experiments. Many are self-contained "payload lockers" that run autonomously, pre-programmed to collect data or activate sensors. For other experiments, a researcher flies with the payload to make real-time adjustments and observations. The frequent flight schedule allows for rapid iteration and refinement of experiments.

A significant problem exists for companies that want to perform research in space or manufacture things. It is relatively easy to get your stuff into space, with a lot of capacity and multiple rockets launching almost weekly from several suppliers. The problem is getting the experiments back; there is virtually zero capacity for returning materials to Earth. Remember how long the astronauts had to wait on the International Space Station when NASA decided the Boeing ship that brought them was unsafe to get them back.

Using SPCE, companies and universities will be able to book a locker, do the experiment, and get it back the same day, This will allow for immediate analysis of data and quick re-testing. A locker might cost $100,000, a small price to pay to test equipment before you book a $10 million flight to and from ISS.

**Payload Capacity of Delta Class**

SPCE has partnered with Redwire for the design of lockers and racks to hold the experiments.

**Payload Racks:** Each Delta class spaceship will be capable of holding up to **five payload racks**.

**Payload Lockers:** These racks can accommodate up to **four "single middeck locker equivalents" (MLEs)**.

**Total Capacity:** This means a single Delta class flight could theoretically carry up to **20 lockers** if the entire cabin is dedicated to payloads. However, the system is flexible and can be mixed with passenger seats. For example, a flight might have three passenger seats and two payload racks, offering a blend of human-tended and autonomous research.

This flexible, modular design is a key feature of the Delta class, allowing Virgin Galactic to accommodate a wide range of research missions.

The 20 lockers would deliver $2 million in revenue, lower than the $3.6 million expected from the tourist flights. The tourist's flight price includes a two-week-long astronaut training and experience holiday. A high-class full-service experience for the world's richest people making the research flights possibly more profitable.

**Estimates for the Demand for Suborbital Research Services**

While it's impossible to provide a single, definitive number for demand, various market research reports and industry analyses offer strong indicators of a growing market. A few key factors drive the demand for suborbital research:

-   **Lower Cost and Faster Turnaround:** As discussed, suborbital flights are significantly cheaper and more frequent than orbital missions. This makes them highly attractive for researchers who need to test technology, gather data quickly, or iterate on an experiment multiple times.
    
-   **Technology Readiness Level (TRL) Advancement:** Suborbital flights are an ideal way to advance a technology's TRL. Researchers can demonstrate a payload in a real-world space environment, collect valuable data, and use that "flight heritage" to secure funding for a more expensive orbital mission.
    
-   **Government and Academic Funding:** Institutions like NASA and other space agencies have dedicated programs, such as NASA's Flight Opportunities program, to fund suborbital research. This provides a clear customer base.
    
-   **Growing Satellite Industry:** With the proliferation of CubeSats and other small satellites, there is a rising demand for low-cost testing services for components, sensors, and instruments before they are launched into orbit.

Bookings will reopen in Q1 2026, it will be a critical moment when we will be able to gauge the size of this market. But they already have enough for two years of operations with their current lineup, so visibility to 2028.

SPCE has not revealed the size of its commercial backlog, but has said the first flights will be carrying commercial payload and are “sold out with more demand than we can cope with.”
