## Cash is Everything

This month, I have opened one full-size position and two half-size positions.

**Full Size Positions**

I set my position size to 3% and use that amount when opening trades, unless the trade qualifies as a half-size position. Trade 93 is an example of this: 54 × 11.11 = 599.94. The account is currently at $20,647, so 3% is approximately $600.

**Half-Size Positions**

All additional positions are opened at half size; trade 95 is an example. I opened a position in this company in June 2025, and it remains open, so this month’s trade is an addition; hence, half-size.

Companies considered to be of particularly high risk are also opened at half size, trade 94 is an example of the. The company is new to the markets and is desperately short of cash, if it solves its cash position I will increase the position size by taking a further half size trade. Companies with a market cap less than $100 million are considered high risk and always open with half-size trades, we have two such companies on the account.

**Cash Flow**

It’s all about cash, to take these full size and half size positions I need to have enough cash on the account. As with all businesses, cash flow is the lifeblood of investing.

My $250-to-$100K project imposes severe restrictions on the amount of cash I can invest. To meet targets, I must generate cash, or I will be unable to take additional trades.

The required investment is beginning to grow substantially as the account grows.

Where will all this cash come from? Initially, my $250 deposit covered a large percentage of the cash needed in a month, but no longer. This month, I will need $2,400, and the amount is set to grow.

This diagram shows the target equity size for the account in 2026. On average, I take 3 full positions and 2 half positions every month. The opening cash balance of $5,956 is an actual figure; the rest are forecasts and targets. Without generating cash from selling shares, I will run out of cash in April.

There are several ways to look at this; the obvious one is that I need to generate $28,644 in cash by this time next year by closing trades, which sounds quite alarming.

Performance so far makes it less concerning.

I am now halfway through the project, this is month 30 of 60, so I have a lot of data to use.

My average trade length is 150 days, so I could assume that trades taken in January will have to pay for those taken 6 months later. So the $2,200 invested in January needs to be $3,200 in July. That is a 45% profit margin, and at the moment, my average is 66%, so all seems good, but I will be watching this cash position carefully.

It does mean I will need to continue closing trades earlier rather than letting them run longer. Trade length is a contentious issue, as I have missed out on multiple big wins after closing. It is a balancing act; I need enough money to trade, but don’t want to miss out on returns by closing too early.

Cash beats everything else; I don’t have to worry about profit left on the table as long as there is enough cash in the account to take next month’s trades.

To hit the target, I must generate enough cash and choose carefully which companies to invest in. Past performance suggests I will continue to hit targets and move this project towards its longer-term goal of $100,000 by August 2028.
