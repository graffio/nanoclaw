# Note -- October 23, 2025

The Volatility Trade-Off: Why My $100K Goal Remains on Track

Yesterday was a tough day. When you look at your portfolio and see all 21 holdings in the red, it’s impossible not to feel a sharp pang of doubt. But this is exactly when we must stop, take a breath, and zoom out.

It’s important to remember why we’re in this game: the five-year plan to reach $100,000 remains firmly on track. The red ink is a cost of admission for the kind of growth we seek, and today, we’re going to normalize that pain using history and our high-risk strategy.

1. Context: Market Corrections Are Normal, Not Rare

What we experienced yesterday, and what we might be experiencing this week, is a normal market correction, or perhaps just a steep pullback. For long-term investors, these events are not a crisis; they are simply the market doing its job—shedding excess or reacting to uncertainty.

History clearly shows that these dips are frequent:

Pullbacks (-5\% or more): These occur about once a year  and are typically short-lived.

Corrections (-10\% to -20\%): The market experiences a full correction, on average, every 1.5 years. The average correction sees a peak-to-trough drop of  -13.5\% and tends to recover in under a year.

Bear Markets: Most corrections do not turn into bear markets (drops of 20% or more) usually its when a correction coincides with a major economic recession.

If you’re waiting for a period where the market just goes up forever, you’ll be waiting for a very long time. Volatility is the price you pay for equity returns.

2. Acknowledging Our Strategy’s Reality

The core reason we need this historical perspective is that our portfolio is not the S&P 500. We are specifically targeting small-cap, pre-revenue app companies. This strategy is designed for maximum asymmetrical returns—the possibility of a 4x or 10x winner—but it comes with a proportional increase in risk.

Here’s the reality of investing in this niche:

Small-Caps are Inherently More Volatile: Small-cap indexes (like the Russell 2000) are generally known to be significantly more volatile than the large-cap S&P 500. Smaller companies have fewer financial resources to weather economic storms, less diversified revenue streams, and less liquidity, making their stock prices highly reactive to market sentiment.

The "Pre-Revenue" Multiplier: Our companies aren't just small; they're pre-revenue. This means their valuations are based entirely on future potential and investor sentiment, not on current profits or cash flow. When the general market gets nervous, capital naturally flows out of the riskiest, most speculative bets first. A 10% drop in the S&P 500 can easily translate into a 30% drop in a basket of pre-revenue holdings. We must normalize this exponential volatility.

The Growth Trade-Off: This heightened risk is the trade-off we willingly accept. High volatility is the twin brother of high potential return. For a five-year, accelerated growth plan like ours, maintaining exposure to these high-risk assets—especially when they are "on sale"—is essential to achieving the target.

3. Discipline and the Long Haul

Yesterday’s red board proves one thing: our investments are highly sensitive to market-wide fear. But our $100,000 goal isn't built on a daily timeline; it's built on a five-year horizon.

The market has always recovered, and the small-cap asset class, while bumpy, has historically delivered higher long-term returns than large-cap stocks. The discipline required is simple, but hard:

Stay Invested.

Stick to the Plan.

View volatility as opportunity.

We are in the right asset class for our goal, and we have the right perspective to weather the inevitable storms. This is simply the cost of doing business on the road to $100K.

3. Progress on the plan

To transform our $250 a month investment into $100,000 in five years we need to deliver an average monthly compound return of 5.2%, even after yesterday's drop we are over 6% in October and continue to move towards our target.
