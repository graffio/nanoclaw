## Counter Drone Technology

Counter-drone operations are an emerging field; companies are taking multiple approaches. Drone Shield is developing defeat technology that interferes with a drone’s communication and directional awareness. Ondas is developing a capture system where one drone fires a net at a hostile drone to capture it. EOS is developing hard kill systems, shooting down drones with cannons, missiles, and lasers.

Drone defence will probably require a layered solution of all three. EOS is a military solution, designed for warfare and the protection of critical infrastructure. You may see a Drone Shield installation on top of a football stadium, an Ondas system on a border wall, but a nuclear power plant will need an EOS system.

I will be reviewing ONDS and DRO this week and may buy one or both. If I don’t like either, I will likely increase the size of my EOS position.

## EOS A summary

Founded in 1983 after Australia privatised its space program, EOS operates in Space and Defence Systems. The Defence segment presents immediate opportunities with advanced counter-drone kinetic and laser kill autonomous technologies.

EOS has a global presence across Australia, the US, Europe, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia, supplying remote defence systems to over ten countries. The US, Saudi Arabia, and UAE have each purchased more than 500 EOS systems.

Earlier this year, EOS secured its initial sale of the counter-drone High Power Laser (HELW) system through a A$125 million contract with a European NATO member. The company has projected a second transaction for Q1 2026 and anticipates achieving a 50% share of the HELW market, citing its first-mover advantage and proprietary, patent-protected technology.

## EOS a turnaround story

EOS has a history of overpromising and underdelivering; it has always had a terrible balance sheet with repeated losses.

The turnaround began in mid-2022 with the appointment of a new CEO, CFO and Chairman. The new management team immediately embarked on a multi-phase turnaround plan.

**Phase 1:** Now complete, was the restructuring of the business, cutting unnecessary costs, terminating the perennially loss-making SpaceLink, and its connected debts. The impact was immediate, with EOS ending 2022 with a positive cash balance.

**Phase 2:** Collecting cash and securing new orders. This is a work in progress but is showing excellent results. Contracts have been renegotiated and the order book has grown over 100% to A$170 million, all of which is expected to be delivered in 2025 and 2026. In Q1 2025, EOS delivered a positive operating cash flow of A$30 million, a clear sign that its management is delivering on promises made.

## The Products

EOS Defence Systems manufactures and sells remote weapon systems (RWS) for unmanned ground vehicles (UGV), as well as counter-drone installations.

The division represents the majority of current income and the focus of the company for the next 5 years. EOS is a recognised leader in providing firepower for unmanned ground vehicles. The EOS systems feature sensors to identify threats, various weapons systems to neutralise that and decision making AI.

EOS partners with multiple manufacturers of these UGVs, which are becoming a staple of the world’s military, providing added lethality without casualties. The systems are battle-proven in multiple conflicts, including Ukraine, which has bought more than $180 million worth of systems.

The RWS 400 has more than 2,500 units in the field and was this year replaced by the RWS 500, with a launch customer expected soon.

EOS has expanded its vertical markets with this technology, firstly into marine lethality systems with the R400-M, which has market-leading precision and range, able to fire from 7.62mm cannons all the way up to Javelin, Stinger, and Spike missiles.

Secondly, Force Protection systems fit on light tactical vehicles, providing autonomous protection from threats.

Finally and most importantly, counter-drone systems. EOS offers two counter-drone systems.

The Slinger: Capable of tracking drones using radar and autonomously firing a 30mm cannon to destroy incoming threats. The system weighs less than 400 kg, making it suitable for multiple use cases.

Titanis: A more advanced system of detection using radar, infrared, and cameras can deploy its radio frequency inhibitor to defeat drones before switching to a hard-kill technology if required. Titanis can counter drone swarms and can incorporate multiple hard kill units. (ie, lots of guns, missiles, or lasers).

Apollo: This is the emerging technology driving my investment. Drones are evolving over time, they are developing rapid manoeuvre tactics, new coatings and autonomous operations that make swarms harder to track and defeat. The traditional kill method of using cannons is proving less effective; they cannot fire enough rounds and need to cool down regularly. Advanced swarm tactics are proving increasingly difficult to destroy.

Apollo is a 50-100 kW high-energy laser system that can disable more than 20 drones a minute (5 times better than a cannon) and does not need to cool down between attacks. Swarm attacks are generally coordinated by loitering drones up to 10km away. Apollo can autonomously identify and target these loitering drones. Apollo can operate independently, firing continuously, and fits inside a 20-foot container; it fully integrates into NATO air defence systems.

## Commercial Progress

In August this year, EOS announced the world’s first export order for a 100kw laser defence system. The order followed three years of field testing by NATO countries; the system will be going into Europe.

The business case for laser counter-drone technology is clear. Drones are becoming shielded to defeat jamming attempts or operate autonomously making defeat technology obsolete.

Missile systems are very effective, but they can cost up to a million dollars per missile, and when drones can cost as little as $1,000, the defenders will run out of money and hardware very quickly. Kinetic systems like the EOS R400 using cannon fire are very effective up to 2km but cannot defeat drone swarms sufficiently to neutralize the threat. High-energy lasers have several advantages: they have a potentially unlimited magazine, a very low cost per shot, and can fire in all directions.

The EOS HELW will be priced between US$30 and US$60 million, a high upfront amount. Still, since each subsequent shot costs only $1, and the system comes with an extended range and the ability to defeat drone swarms, it may prove to be a cost-effective long-term solution. It is expected that customers will order a trial system before ordering larger quantities.

The TAM for this system (outside of the US) is forecast to be around 200 units or $10 billion over the next five years. In the 50-200 kW market, there are minimal players, EOS, one company in the UK, and one in Israel. The US is focused on 300kw systems and above, targeting the threat of hypersonic missiles, which currently have no countermeasures. Smaller 20kw lasers can only cope with group one drones, but with lower barriers to entry, there are multiple players.

EOS demonstrated its first system in 2023 and owns all the relevant IP, with the IP based in Singapore. The company is well placed to target Countries seeking non-US solutions.

The deal they signed is the first in the industry, giving EOS substantial first-mover advantages. We have already seen their ability to transport their tech into other verticals. I would expect Naval systems in the short term and further development into larger systems in the medium term.

With first mover advantage, protected IP, proven operational ability, and a history of supplying successful remote weapon systems, I think EOS could capture the majority of the projected 5-year TAM of $10 billion.

The Middle East is forecast to purchase more than A$500 million in R500 RWS systems in 2026.

The HELW system is a potential game changer. The first order has been received and at A$125 million is equal to 70% of 2024 revenue. A second order has been guided for Q1, and if the first system is successfully installed, then EOS has a huge opportunity.

They are first to market with a device that offers protection from autonomous drone swarms and larger independent drones able to take them down at greater distances than previous technologies. It could well represent $5 billion of sales in the decade. Management believes it might be US$5 billion in the next 5 years. I have not built that into my model, but it represents significant upside.
