## Semiconductors


Datacenter capacity is set to double by 2030, with 100 GW of capacity added and $ 1 trillion spent each year. AI is expected to account for more than half of that capacity, when it is currently near 20%. The semiconductor market is also expected to pass $ trillion by 2030, with energy becoming the largest constraint, putting a focus on liquid cooling, energy efficiency, and self-generated power.

A transition from Silicon to Silicon Carbide and Gallium Nitride is taking place. New AI chips require a lot of power, as much as 1,500 watts per cluster. The Silicon chips we have used for decades can not cope with this new power; they heat up too much and take up too much space. A generational change is required for this new architecture, and companies with technology in these areas have potential value.

The large incumbents like Infineon, STMicroelectronics, and Delta Electronics are scaling rapidly to meet new requirements, which will put pressure on smaller or emerging players, but some of these players have interesting and potentially disruptive technologies.

Traditional datacenter power involves multiple conversion stages, resulting in up to 15% energy loss. New entrants are targeting the elimination of these stages through Solid-State Transformer (SST) technology and high-voltage direct current (HVDC) architectures.

I have put together a list of potential targets and one large company that can not be ignored in the discussion.

![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/2df531ff-070c-418e-8848-2859f6c7a406_1083x246.png)



**Coherent** is generally cited by Wall Street as the top “pick and shovel” play for the AI boom, with over 70% of its revenue coming from its Datacenter & Communications segment. The company is shifting production to 6-inch wafers, which quadruples capacity and lowers costs by 50% compared to traditional 3-inch processes. This scale has enabled them to secure multi-billion-dollar long-term agreements with customers such as NVIDIA, which recently made a $2 billion equity investment in the firm. Coherent’s primary moat is its vertical integration in Indium Phosphide (InP) and VCSEL laser technology, which it uses to manufacture high-speed 800G and 1.6T optical transceivers. Coherent faces competition from Chinese companies like InnoLight, which are ramping production and a long-term shift towards co-packaged products that will likely reduce the TAM of the industry.

**Vicor** is focused on solving the “last inch” power-delivery problem for AI accelerators, where processors require high currents at ultra-low voltages. Proprietary technology allows power to be delivered from directly beneath the processor, reducing power losses and saving board space. The company also maintains a high-margin **IP licensing** business that is expected to double over the next few years.

**SolarEdge** is leveraging nearly two decades of DC power expertise to disrupt the datacenter market with a proprietary SST solution targeting over 99% efficiency. They have partnered with Infineon to optimize Silicon Carbide switching. While they are in technical discussions with hyperscalers, prototype testing is expected in 2027, with mass deployment targeted for 2028.

**Navitas** is performing a business pivot to take advantage of the data center opportunity. It is pivoting away from consumer electronics in its Navitas 2.0 management strategy. Navitas integrates Gallium Nitride and Silicon Carbide into high-efficiency power modules. Their breakthrough 800V-to-50V DC-DC platform  targets the next-generation 800V architectures being championed by NVIDIA. Their fabless model allows for rapid innovation and lower fixed costs.

**WolfSpeed **was once the undisputed leader in Silicon Carbide, however its reputation has suffered following its recent bankruptcy and financial restructuring**.** However its primary advantage remains its massive 200mm Silicon Carbide wafer manufacturing capability in its Mohawk Valley and John Palmour facilities. Their Gen 4 devices are purpose-built for the high-voltage demands of AI datacenters, and the company reported a 50% quarter-over-quarter growth in AI datacenter revenue in late 2025

**Ideal Power** is a micro-cap company commercializing its proprietary bidirectional semiconductor technology that can reduce power losses by 50% or more compared to conventional switches. In datacenters, it targets solid-state circuit breakers (SSCB) and UPS systems, where its bidirectional nature can reduce component counts by 75%. The company holds a deep moat of 100 issued patents. It has a reasonable balance sheet but with only $6 million in cash and only 17 employees will it be able to compete with these multi billion dollar companies?

Conclusions

I want some exposure to datacenters and am looking at several potential plays.

I have previously invested in Ceres, Capstone, and Bloom, all of which are involved in generating electricity on-site for these projects. The availability of electricity is a huge bottleneck and there are other innovative small companies trying to move in this power generation area that remain of interest.

The next area of interest is companies like Keel who can provide ready to go facilities. Bitfarms (now rebranded as Keel Infrastructure) was trade number 3 in the Strategic Wave Trading Portfolio, delivering a 200% return, and is now ending its bitcoin mining operations and turning over its facilities to AI datacenters. Bitfarms is selling its bitcoin treasury and bitcoin mining machines to pay for the transition. 

Power is a scarce resource in many areas and those that already have it, especially in the right areas (close to major conurbations and fibre optic infrastructure) have the opportunity to lease that power. If they can sign the right contracts with the right customers they could see a significant re rating. If BitFarms can change their facilities from low-rated, high-risk bitcoin mining to long-term leaseholds with investment-grade companies, they are likely to see a significant stock price appreciation.

Finally there are the emerging technology companies discussed in this piece, if one of them can deliver a power or performance advantage over the incumbents they could also see swift price appreciation and perhaps takeover speculation.
