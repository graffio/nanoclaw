Metallium Limited is commercializing its proprietary **Flash Joule Heating (FJH) technology**.

**FJH** is a disruptive process that can improve metal recovery from various waste streams (e.g., e-waste, red mud) compared to existing methods like hydrometallurgy and pyrometallurgy. It offers:

-   Higher recoveries
    
-   Improved ESG
    
-   Lower costs
    
-   Shorter lead times
    

FJH was developed at Rice University, and MTM has the global rights to use the technology. It works by using a material’s own resistance to create heat. The “flash” in the name is to reflect its similarity with lightning strikes. It has three stages. Firstly, the feedstock is ground into a powder and placed inside a ceramic tube. Secondly a massive surge of DC electricity held in a capacitor bank is applied, within a single second of the current being applied the temperature of the feedstock reaches 3,000 degrees C, the sudden heating breaks chemical bonds that would normally keep high value metals inside the waste material. Finally, the material is subject to more “flashes,” turning the targeted metal into a vapour which is removed and quickly cooled.

FJH is very quick and does not use chemicals. It is relatively low energy and does not use water.

MTM has focused on processing high-value **e-waste** (printed circuit boards and semiconductor waste) using a build-own-operate model. After lab and pilot de-risking, MTM is moving to **commercial-scale production in Chambers County, Texas.**

MTM has an MOU with Glencore to establish a long-term offtake agreement that should have been announced by the end of 2025. The deal would be a game-changer as Glencore would supply the majority of the required feedstock and take 75% of the output.

The collaboration intends to place MTM operations near major e-waste collection hubs throughout the US.

The business model includes licensing the technology to other local recycling companies as well as the establishment of major hubs with Glencore.

## December 12th

Trading in MTM shares was suspended as the company requested time to prepare a press release about the details of a collaboration project. I had expected this to be the final Glencore deal and was expecting to read the details of the contract before issuing a trade alert if everything was good.

It turned out to be a curve ball, an entirely new agreement with a separate company that I had never heard of and it took some time to work through the deal and the new partner to come to an opinion.

The new agreement is with ElementUSA, a start-up company with no existing operations (red flag for sure, a start-up in place of Glencore?). Element has the rights to 30 million tonnes of mineral-rich red mud (a byproduct of aluminum production) in Louisiana. The deal is to extract Gallium and Scandium from the red mud, two rare earth metals critical for defence manufacturing, especially missiles, 5G and missile defence systems.

The US government heavily backs ElementUSA. In January, they got a $30 million grant from the DoD, and towards the end of the year, they announced a goal to process 1 million tons of the red mud per annum in a new $850 million facility. Element will integrate the MTM technology into this huge Louisiana site.

Element will provide MTM with US$10 million in non-dilutive funding to help deploy the technology at Elements pilot facility based in Texas. Phase B will be the commercial scale plant in Louisiana. The deal is a huge de-risker for this investment, Element has 30 million tons of feedstock and a big budget, it will also provide a short path to commercial scale operations with phase 1 completing in 2026 and phase 2 in 2027.

It sounded too good to be true, so we have the normal question to answer: Is it a scam?

How does a start-up company with no operations secure $30 million from the DoD, a 30-million-ton supply of red mud, and the funding to build an $850 million facility?

It's all about the people: If you wanted to put together an A-team for a start-up in this industry, it would probably resemble the ElementUSA lineup.

The CEO is the former CEO of ROC Minerals, a company he founded and scaled into one of America’s largest suppliers of key resources. He has a reputation for managing large scale complex mineral supply chains and successfully incorporating new technologies.

The chairman has 11 years at JP Morgan and serves on the board of Firefly Aerospace, which successfully landed a spaceship on the moon in 2025, and is a big user of rare earth metals.

The VP of technology has 30 years in this technology having managed operations at Arizona Lithium, one of the biggest refining sites in the country.

The board and the military connections.

Getting the backing of the DoD and subsequent contracts as well as knowing exactly what products are needed is likely down to a high powered board including the former commander of Air Force Global Strike Command, the former Vice Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff and a retired three-star general who was the Director for Defence Policy.

So ElementUSA has the people with the contacts and the experience. It has raised the money and secured the deals, but most importantly, it has chosen MTM technology. I often say winners win and this is a winning team one I am happy to invest in.

Note: the Glencore deal has not gone away and this new deal in no way impacts that important stepping stone, they are using the same technology but for completely different feedstocks and will coexist. If the Glencore deal is released we could see another surge in share price.

## The Kicker

I said at the beginning that MTM was a junior miner transitioning into a US tech stock. In the US it will be an “urban miner” using waste to mine critical minerals. e-Waste presents enormous potential. The DoW has designated gallium and germanium as critical minerals and the US has no domestic supply of gallium and needs to import 50% of its germanium needs. MTM has proven its ability to recover large amounts of these metals

Here is the kicker, providing additional value: MTM has significant mining reserves and projects that should drive the share price as they become recoverable. In Canada they own the Pomme Project, a carbonatite REE rich resource. The site is 7Km from the world class Montviel Deposit that holds more than 266 million tonnes of REE. Initial drilling at the MTM site shows similar holdings and MTM also owns the Montviel south project in the same area.

MTM owns four large deposits in Australia, West Arunta, East Claverton, Ravensthorpe and Mukinbudin all of which have shown high concentrations of key metals especially magnet and lithium deposits.

The FJH technology can be used to extract metals from these clay, hard rock, and carbonatite deposits, providing significant long-term potential.

## Finances

For an early-stage company, MTM's finances are pretty solid, and for a junior miner, they are outstanding. It has positive shareholder equity of AUD$26 million, zero debt, and cash on hand of AUD$7 million.

The big issue is they have less than a year of cash runway and as a result shareholders suffered 50% dilution in 2025 and can expect more in 2026. The general public owns 70% of the shares and insiders 9%. Currently, Wall Street has less than 20% of the company, but that could change as the company moves to being a US tech stock. I tentatively predict a US listing in 2026 that would see a meaningful change to the Wall Street engagement.

The CEO package is a possible issue (thinking of DroneShield) his total pay packet is AUD$18 million which is high for a company of this size but his actual salary is only AUD$400k that is about a quarter of what the CEO of a similar sized US company would expect to earn.
