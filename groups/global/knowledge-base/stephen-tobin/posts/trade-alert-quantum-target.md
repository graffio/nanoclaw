# Trade Alert: Quantum Target

*Do we need a new quantum target?*

D-Wave (QBTS) has been a big winner for us in the last 12 months, but before reinvesting, I need answers to some key questions about D-Wave and its quantum computer.

The former **VP of sales explained precisely what D-Wave’s professional services** are and what they can charge.

He said, “ _We would sit down with the customer and review some of their existing optimization problems,”_ pointing out that D-Wave has a number of the world's best PhD optimization mathematicians on the payroll who could write the problem in a way the D-Wave hybrid solver could understand.

He said the hybrid solver was a combination of classical GPUs and a QPU, and stated that it was unclear how much, if any, work the QPU was performing.

D-Wave would charge between $ 250,000 and $ 350,000 for this consulting, which would typically last for six months.

He said, “_These are real business optimization problems that the customers wanted to solve_”.

At the end of the process, the customer would have a new solution, but “_**the ROI on the deal was not great for the customer**_”, and they typically did not come back with more problems for D-Wave to look at.

He implied that the improvement was due to the PhDs' ability to rewrite the optimization problem and stated that “_they would reduce the runtime of the problem from 60 minutes to 30 minutes_”. He believed that **a classical optimization company like Gurobi could offer the same improvement** if it had the same quality of optimization PhDs.

Once the consulting work was complete, the expert raised the issue of how to charge the customer for running the application, which might run for a few seconds each day on the cloud. He mentioned GM saying, “_We found a solution that added some value, but how do you charge them after the initial consulting gig?_” They would not pay much to save a few minutes of runtime and were not interested in spending another $ 300,000 to achieve a similar small improvement.

He said, “_the value is not overwhelming for the customer nor is the differentiation above classical”_

The expert noted that the **customer count is almost static**; it was over 100 when he left, and in the two years since, it had only increased to 130. He firmly believes that customers are doing one problem, thinking the results are OK, better than they had, but not enough of an improvement to justify the cost, hence they don't come back with a second problem, but continue to contact D-Wave to see how things progress.

The interview answered two critical questions: what is Professional Services, and why is it not growing? We now know that it is a six-month project to analyze an optimization problem a company has and deliver a computer program that runs on the D-Wave machine to solve the problem. We also know the answer provided is a bit better than the one customers had, but the $350k price tag of the service meant it did not provide a good ROI, and hence, it has not been growing.

## Advantage 2

One crucial factor is the time the two interviewees were working with D-Wave. The VP of sales was with D-Wave when they had the Advantage 1 processor. The CEO at Codebugged is working with D-Wave today using the Advantage 2 processor.

One of the first things the CEO said was “_you have to realize **Advantage 1 was super shit** before Advantage 2 there was nothing from any quantum company that was good enough for a production run_”

He said Advantage 1 would not scale up to larger problems, and if you tried to scale it, a lot of errors would occur. However, with **Advantage 2, scaling is no longer a problem,** and he has seen multiple delivery companies now using D-Wave to determine their most efficient routes.

Advantage 2 was released to a select number of clients at the beginning of the year, including CodeBugged.

Before Advantage 2 arrived, CodeBugged was using Nvidia GPU integrations to run all optimization problems. They have a high-profile list of customers and specialize in writing bespoke optimization code for large companies.

CodeBugged focuses on a CPU-GPU-QPU hybrid solving method for challenging optimization problems, with the CPU handling the operating system, the GPU performing backpropagation and learning algorithms, and the QPU handling mathematical calculations.

Interestingly, the CEO considers the GPU to be more powerful using an analogy of **the world’s strongest man (a GPU) and an army of weak men (the QPU).**

The CEO spoke about a specific example they have worked on and deployed for a customer. It is an optimization problem for a company with 1,000 trucks.

I will attempt to explain the mathematical background behind this problem before sharing the CEO’s insights into the business case. To simplify the analysis, I will disregard the complexities of the various trucks, their starting points, driving hours, truck sizes, and the amount of diesel used by different types of trucks, as well as the sizes of parcels.

If we assume it is just 100 parcels that need to be delivered with the lowest mileage. The total number of possible routes is (rounded to one significant figure)

900,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000

The number gives an idea of the scale of the problem. Each route is easy to calculate; you add up 100 numbers, but you have to do it so many times that it is nearly impossible for any classical computer to solve in a reasonable amount of time. You need vast amounts of parallelism, with thousands or millions of machines working simultaneously on the same problem.

This is the army of weak men fighting the world's strongest man in his analogy.

The CEO said if you spent $100K on GPU, you would be able to get the answer in 500 hours, using the D-Wave annealer, he gets to a solution in under an hour. However, he brought up a problem: you cannot be certain that the D-Wave is providing the optimal solution, which requires some explanation.

The CEO said companies have the option of **spending tens of millions of dollars on GPUs and obtaining the perfect answer,** or purchasing a QCaaS package from D-Wave and receiving an answer that is in the top 2 or 3 percent of all answers.

### It’s Not Just The Processor

When discussing why users have not begun to spend more, he replied, “_They have, in 2025 they have, but the Advantage 2 was not generally available, now it is._”

He discussed what might be driving the sudden uptick he has observed, and he highlighted some points I had not previously considered.

The year 2024 marked a significant period for quantum research, driven by advancements in both availability and algorithms. The focus extended beyond hardware to encompass compilers and software code designed for quantum processing units (QPUs). Researchers at institutions like Berkeley, various companies, and Chinese developers published numerous papers in 2024, detailing methods to write code that yields highly accurate results on QPUs.

QPU code requires a complete rewrite from GPU code. **New libraries in 2025,** alongside mature tools like Microsoft Quantum Azure Braket and QIO, have revolutionized accessibility. Developers can now **submit Python optimization** problems instead of writing quantum math.

The CEO emphasised that the **changes to the coding ecosystem were as important as the changes to the processor.**

The **barriers to entry have fallen,** and he believes adoption is begining.

## Risks

I believe the risks are tilted to the upside, in terms of Quantum competition it currently has none. IBM has guided the development of a small-scale error-corrected machine in 2029. Still, it will likely require an order of magnitude more qubits to compute these large-scale optimization problems within a timescale that competes with D-Wave’s annealer. D-Wave likely has a 15-20 year monopoly in this niche.

D-Wave will face competition from simulated annealers, companies trying to deliver the effect of annealing but using existing classical methods. These machines offer several advantages; they are error-free, can be used at room temperature, and their architecture is well understood, making manufacturing relatively straightforward. The problem is scale, the whole point of annealing is to solve problems with a high degree of complexity.

The three leaders in this field are Japanese companies. Toshiba is in first place with its SBA, it on GPU hardware and can handle 100,000 variables and constraints. Hitachi is next with around 20,000 variable capacity, and Fujitsu, with its custom-built chip, the DAO, can cope with 8,000 variables.

The D-Wave Advantage 2 has a capacity for 2 million variables and constraints. The classical simulators are improving, but D-Wave is improving exponentially faster; the gap is growing.

## Conclusion

I think D-Wave has a significant and sustainable competitive advantage in solving large-scale optimization problems for enterprise customers.

Detractors argue that the quantum machine is not performing the work, and the proof of quantum supremacy was somewhat of a setup, but they are missing the point. The annealer is solving real optimization problems for real enterprise customers. I don't think those companies will care how much of the work is being done by the annealer or whether the spin glass simulation paper was a put-up job.

Enterprise customers are buying access to the Advantage 2 because it can provide them with a good enough solution to their optimization problems at a fraction of the price it would take them to solve the same problem on a GPU system.

The fact that they can write in Python, use libraries of existing code, and refer to scientific papers just makes the barriers to entry smaller.
