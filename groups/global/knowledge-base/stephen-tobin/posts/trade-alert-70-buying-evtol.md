It means Vertical will be the lowest cost manufacturer, selling the best aircraft with the highest standards of safety.

**Vertical is a strong buy on every metric.**

## Unique Business Model

Vertical will be an OEM, leveraging the existing aerospace supply chain to build its aircraft.

Aciturri Aerostuctures will supply the entire airframe for the VX4. Aciturri currently supplies structures for Airbus (A350, A380, and A400) and Boeing (737, 777, 787, and Dreamliner), as well as several smaller aircraft manufacturers.

GKN supplies the electrical wiring and interconnect, as they do for Boeing, Airbus, and Lockheed Martin.

Honeywell supplies the flight control and management systems, as it does for Airbus, Boeing, and Bombardier.

That is three major suppliers; there are many others, but of a similar heritage. Many of the components have already been certified and have proven their effectiveness in real-world operations. It could account for the speed at which Vertical is moving through the testing and production phases.

### Certification

Vertical is pursuing a dual UK/EU certification plan; they have designed and built the aircraft to meet the already published plans in the UK, and the final edition of the European means of compliance was published in July this year, aligning with the UK standards. In the US, the rules are opaque and manufacturer-specific.

Currently, the FAA and the Europeans have not agreed on a common framework. It is expected that this will happen, and JOBY has a deal with Virgin Atlantic, anticipating that the UK and Europe will agree on the FAA rules.

In the US a recent executive order “[Unleashing American Drone Dominance](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/06/unleashing-american-drone-dominance/)” following the FAA’s Innovate 2028 document is attempting to make compliance more straightforward and quicker. This may help in the US but may hinder sales in Europe.

The Europeans have set a higher safety standard for airframes. Europe has adopted the 10^-9 error rate used by the FAA and all other major aviation bodies for large commercial aircraft. The FAA has reduced this to 10^-7 for eVTOL aircraft, following consultation with industry bodies.

It suggests that European safety standards are two orders of magnitude higher (approximately 100 times as high). When certified, Vertical will pass the US requirements, but JOBY and Archer will not pass the European ones.

It may all be negotiated away, but the Europeans and the Americans are not negotiating well at the moment. It would be the perfect scenario for Vertical if Mr. Trump were to ban European eVTOLs, because they would not reduce their safety standards.

# Forecasts and Models

## eVTOL market

Numerous market predictions are suggesting enormous growth, but we should consider where that growth is coming from.

They will have military and commercial applications.

**In the commercial market**, there are medevac, transportation, and cargo services.

Transport is the big one; the air taxi service we hear about. The largest market will be around major airports, where eVTOLs will carry passengers to and from airplanes. That’s why the airlines are involved. This will be an entirely new market, requiring new infrastructure and likely taking time to develop.

Helicopters do not currently perform this service, as they are too expensive to operate and are prohibited due to safety concerns.

A more immediate market will be the one for superlight to medium helicopters; eVTOLs could take a substantial part of this market very quickly. If eVTOL companies can bring their hybrid drive trains with a range of over 500 miles, the cannibalization of the helicopter market will intensify.

**In the military,** the use case is probably limited in the early days. I doubt the first aircraft will have the resilience needed to be a military asset, but they may be useful for moving cargo and medevac. However, bigger drones and autonomous vehicles may be preferred.

In light of all this, I forecast slower growth than most people expect, with limited sales for the next five years. However, I anticipate it accelerating as we pass 2030, possibly reaching 10,000 aircraft by 2035.

The total helicopter market is approximately $50 billion, and I believe the total market for the eVTOL is likely around $500 billion, but that total is perhaps another 15 years away.

It is this slower initial market growth forecast that has made me discount Joby as an investment this time around. I think people will be disappointed with the slow take-up of sales, and Joby, as the highest market cap player, will be the one to see its share price correct over the short term.
